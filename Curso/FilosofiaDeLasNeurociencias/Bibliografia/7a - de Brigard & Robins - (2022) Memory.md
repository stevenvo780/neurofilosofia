 Chapter Overview
 This chapter provides a discussion of memory in philosophy and neuroscience,
with each section organized around three questions: (1) What is memory? (2)
What is remembering? (3) What are memories? Each section begins by exploring
ways that memory has been taxonomized, both by scientists and by philosophers
of memory, emphasizing the kinds that have been particularly relevant for discus -
 sions at the intersection of these disciplines. The focus moves next to the mental
act of remembering—the processes that are involved in our capacity to encode,
store,  and  retrieve  information—and  the  mechanisms  that  make  remembering
unique but also similar to other cognitive operations. Finally, each section draws
attention to the individual mental states we call “memories,” regardless of whether
they involve memory traces or representations, and what the nature of such entities
may be. Throughout, we make note of the ways in which philosophical and neu -
 roscientifc work on memory have been pursued independently and discuss the
current  trend  toward  interdisciplinary,  collaborative  work  on  these  basic
questions.
 Key Terms
 Encoding-storage-retrieval (E-S-R) model : standard view of memory accord -
 ing to which information is first encoded, then stored, and then retrieved at
recollection.
 Engram or memory trace : vehicles that enable the preservation of memories
from encoding to retrieval.
 Standard Model of Memory (SMM) : influential taxonomy of memory sys -
 tems that divides long-term memory into declarative (explicit) and nondeclarative
(implicit).
 Episodic memory (EM) : memory for specific spatiotemporal events; it is said
to be accompanied by autonoetic consciousness (i.e., awareness of oneself as
having experienced the event in the past).

 1.  INTRODUCTION
 The word “memory” is polysemous. It can be used in reference to at least one of three
things. First, it may refer to the psychological  faculty  by means of which we encode,
store, and retrieve information. Second, it may refer to the  processes  of encoding, storing,
and retrieving information. Or, third, it may refer to the encoded, stored, and retrieved
 information  itself. Memory researchers have been interested in all three senses of the term,
and their views on each often intersect.
 1.1.  Historical Overview
 What is memory? Understood as a faculty, researchers—both philosophers and scientists—
take memory to mean, at least prima facie, our capacity to bring back to mind information
previously acquired in order to deploy it for present and future purposes. This definition
is, of course, contentious; for some it may be too restrictive, while for others too permis -
 sive. Nevertheless, if there is something almost all memory researchers would agree on,
it is that the term “memory” does  not  refer to a single monolithic psychological faculty
but, instead, that there are different kinds of memory. What they disagree on, however,
is what these kinds are and how to distinguish them.
 The idea that there are different kinds of memory is old. Aristotle, for instance,
famously distinguished between  memory  and  reminiscence , the former roughly correspond -
 ing to the retention of temporally based information from past events, and the latter to
the act of retrieving previously stored information (Chapell, 2017). This distinction was
conceptual, and it was grounded in the theoretical apparatus of his theory of the mind.
Yet, it had profound empirical consequences. He thought, for instance, that memory was
perceptual, and thus certain animals had memory. By contrast, he took reminiscence
to be a kind of inference, and, thus, only humans, with their intellectual souls, were
capable of reminiscing. Augustine ( Confessions , Book X) also talked about different kinds
of memory. He argued, for instance, that there is a kind of memory that is imagistic, as
when one remembers particular objects, but there is also a kind of memory that is not,
as when one remembers mathematical laws. Locke (1694/1979) also distinguished two
kinds of memory:  contemplation , or the retention of consciously entertained information
for short periods of time—roughly what we would call nowadays  working memory— and
 memory proper , which he famously identifed with the moniker “the storehouse of our

 ideas.” A similar distinction was later recapitulated by Russell (1921), who distinguished
between  immediate  and  pure  memory, with only the latter pertaining the retention of
ideas of temporally remote events no longer present in one’s immediate memory. James
(1890) offered another well-known distinction, between  primary  and  secondary  memory,
with the former roughly corresponding to skills and habits and the latter to what the
standard model calls  declarative memory  (see later discussion). This, of course, is only a
small selection of the many kinds of memory that philosophers have offered in the past.
Nevertheless, they all share something in common: they are established mostly on  con -
 ceptual  grounds. Their relationship to empirical evidence is, at best, tenuous.
 In addition to wondering about the nature of memory, researchers have been interested
in what we  do  with memory, namely, remembering. Across these various ways of making
distinctions within memory, philosophers tend to agree with the idea that remembering is a
 diachronic  capacity that requires (at least) two distinct events. There must be an initial event
where one acquires information and also a subsequent event during which the previously
acquired information is called to mind. According to Ryle, remembering is merely “having
learned something and not forgotten it” (1949, p. 272). This is true enough, but many have
wanted to say more. Even Ryle’s minimalist construal involves reference to both learning
and the later activity of not forgetting. Most memory theorists prefer to put the point more
substantively: remembering is more than not forgetting, it is  retention . The initial event or
activity must be connected to the later event or activity through an extended process.
 Cognitive psychologists and neuroscientists also agree that remembering is a diachronic
process, but they tend to think that it involves three stages: encoding, storage, and retrieval.
 Encoding  refers to the process by which information makes its way into memory. Accord -
 ing to many theorists, one must actually attend to a piece of information in order for it
to be a candidate for remembering.  Storage  refers to the process by which information is
maintained in memory, and  retrieval  is the process by which information is recovered from
memory. The  encoding-storage-retrieval , or  E-S-R ,  model  of memory refects the infor -
 mation-processing framework around which much of contemporary cognitive psychology
is structured (Atkinson & Shiffrin, 1968). It is rare to fnd an introduction to memory
that does not begin with a description of these three processes. Jenkins (1979) identifes
these processes as essential features of memory experiments, and Schacter (2001) refers
to them as the “pretheoretical orienting framework within which a systematic analysis of
memory can be pursued” (p. 138).
 Finally, both scientists and philosophers of memory have also wondered about the
nature of memories per se. If remembering across its many forms is diachronic, then it is
reasonable to assume that something must connect these processes across time. Research -
 ers call this connecting factor by many names, including “memory representation,” “ mem -
 ory trace ,” and “ engram .” The notion of memory trace has been central to discussions of
memory for as long as this psychological capacity has been discussed. However, central
aspects of the trace remain hotly contested, not only because the interpretation of memory
traces varies from the metaphoric to the literal (Draaisma, 2000; Roediger, 1980) but also
because, even when interpreted literally, their precise nature is unclear.
 The idea that our experiences leave traces in our memory dates at least to Plato, who
compared the process to that of seal rings leaving impressions on a wax table (Theaete -
 tus, 1922). For Plato, these  impressions  needed to resemble the past events that they
represented. Aristotle also thought that it was necessary for memory traces to be similar

 to the events of which they were traces. However, he thought this condition was insuf -

 tion condition), and (3) to be similar or isomorphic to the event experienced (similarity
condition). Despite its ancient origin, Aristotle’s notion of memory trace scaffolded many
discussions in the philosophy of memory throughout the modern period and remained
almost identical until the frst half of the 20th century (Malcolm, 1977).
 1.2.  Historical Development
 Despite shared origins, philosophical and empirical research on these questions—about
the forms of memory, the process of remembering, and the nature of memories them -
 selves—progressed independently. While philosophers and neuroscientists have generally
agreed about the need to distinguish various forms of memory, to characterize each stage
of the remembering process, and to account for the underlying memories, each field has
focused on distinct methods, processes, and levels of explanation.
 More recently, philosophers have gone beyond conceptual taxonomies of memory to
also offer  grammatical  ones (e.g., Bernecker, 2010). According to this approach, distinct
kinds of memory correspond to the different kinds of direct objects that the verb “to
remember” can take on. Specifcally, one can remember (1) particulars, such as objects
or persons (e.g., “Cecilia remembers her childhood teacher”); (2) events and actions (e.g.,
“Edgar remembers falling down the stairs”); or (3) facts and propositions (e.g., “I remember
that the Pythagorean theorem is  a 2  +  b 2  =  c 2 ”). Unfortunately, to distinguish kinds of memo -
 ries on grammatical grounds is problematic. For one, it is linguistically chauvinistic, as there
are plenty of languages that do not conform to the syntactic structure of the English verb
“to remember” (Amberer, 2007). Even if there were conformity, this grammatical structure
would be unlikely to mirror psychological kinds, because it is perfectly possible to employ
different grammatical forms to express the same thought. Harry may remember the event
of meeting Sally and may express it as either “I remember meeting Sally” or “I remember
that I met Sally,” without it meaning that in the former case he is expressing a different
kind of mental content than in the latter. To take a grammatical difference as evidence for
a difference in thought at best gets the order of explanation backwards.
 Perhaps the most predominant taxonomy among scientists, and increasingly so among
philosophers, is known as the  Standard Model of Memory  ( SMM ). Initially suggested by
Squire (1986) to account for neuropathological evidence, the SMM was solidifed in the
following years by incorporating further  empirical  fndings from research on human and
 nonhuman animals (Box 21.1). Importantly,  the SMM draws distinctions on the basis
of  double dissociations : roughly, the empirical demonstration that two psychological pro -
 cesses can occur independently of each other (see Chapter 2). The frst such distinction
is between short- and long-term memory, with the latter in turn splitting into declarative
 (or explicit) and nondeclarative (or implicit) memory (Figure 21.1).

 Due to intractable epilepsy, in 1953, at the age of 27, Henry Molaison or H.M.
underwent a surgical procedure that removed both hippocampi and much of the
surrounding tissue in the medial temporal lobes. As a result, H.M. lost his capacity
to store and retrieve verbally reportable information whose acquisition required
conscious awareness. By contrast, the surgery spared many of H.M.’s cognitive
capacities, including perception, higher-order reasoning, and short-term memory,
that is, the retention of information for brief periods of time (Scoville  & Milner,
1957). Critically, it also seemed to spare the acquisition and retention of both
perceptual and motor skills, which are acquired over time, need not be verbally
reportable, and do not typically require conscious awareness to encode or retrieve
(Milner et al., 1968). Thus, H.M.’s single dissociation case suggests that encoding
in short-term memory can be preserved when encoding in long-term memory is
impaired, and that nondeclarative or implicit memory can occur without declara -
 tive or explicit memory.
 While H.M.’s case cannot provide evidence for double dissociation on its
own, further studies provided the required evidence. For instance, Warrington
and Shallice (1969) reported the case of K.F., who presented with severe short-
term memory defcits due to a parieto-occipital lesion and yet had preserved
long-term  memory  (i.e.,  did  not  exhibit  anterograde  or  retrograde  amnesia).
Likewise, Gabrieli and colleagues (1995) reported the case of M.S. who, after
a lesion in the right occipital lobe, showed impairments in implicit memory but
not explicit memory. Additionally, Knowlton et  al. (1996) showed that while
individuals with medial temporal lobe amnesia (such as H.M.) were able to slowly
learn probabilistic associations, individuals with damage in the basal ganglia
and striatum—such as patients with Parkinson’s or Huntington’s disease—could
not. By contrast, basal ganglia damage did not impair performance on recogni -
 tion tasks, precisely the tasks on which individuals with amnesia were profoundly
impaired.
 Finally, research with neurotypical populations, as well as comparative stud -
 ies with nonhuman animals, helped to solidify the idea that long-term memory
comprised two main kinds of memory: declarative or explicit and nondeclara -
 tive or implicit (Squire, 1992). Further refnements divided the latter into four
subclasses, each associated with a distinct neuroanatomical substrate. Prim -
 ing—or the increased propensity to generate a response given the implicit
processing of a previously presented stimulus—was associated with the neo -
 cortex; procedural memory (i.e., memory for skills and habits) became associ -
 ated with the basal ganglia/striatum, the motor cortex, and the cerebellum;
emotional associative learning was associated with the amygdala; and nonas -
 sociative  learning,  such  as  refexes,  was  associated  with  neural  refex
pathways.

 Declarative or explicit memory is often divided into two subclasses as well, thanks to
Tulving’s infuential distinction between  episodic memory  ( EM ) and  semantic memory
 ( SM ) (Tulving, 1972). Initially, this distinction was proposed on the basis of putative
computational differences between the two. The frst difference had to do with the nature
of the stored information: EM stored information according to spatiotemporal relations,
while SM stored it according to semantic associations. Second, the information in EM
was autobiographical and was recorded directly (i.e., the information recorded had to be
experienced), whereas in SM the information encoded need not be autobiographical and
it could be recorded indirectly. Third, EM was prone to interference and modifcation
in a way that SM was not. A few years later, Tulving (1985) refned this defnition and
introduced a phenomenological criterion to distinguish the two: EM involved autonoetic
consciousness (self-knowing), while SM involved noetic consciousness (knowing)—proce -
 dural memory was thought to involve anoetic (nonknowing) consciousness. What exactly
these terms mean, and how these kinds of consciousness relate to the different kinds of
memory, is still a matter of debate. Nevertheless, Tulving’s infuential proposal is that EM
and SM constitute two distinct kinds of memory, with independent computational and
phenomenological characteristics.
 With regard to the process of remembering, philosophers and psychologists have
shared a commitment to the E-S-R approach, but have focused their work on different
ends of the process. For memory scientists, most of the interest in memory processes
has been concerned with encoding. In a 2000 paper, Roediger estimated that 80% of
experiments in the cognitive psychology of memory had been focused on encoding.
Researchers vary the conditions of encoding and observe their infuence on subsequent
retention. In neuroscience, much of the focus is on the process of  consolidation : the
encoding process by which information moves from temporary to long-term memory

 storage. The observation that newly acquired information takes a while to solidify into
a long-term memory has been noted by many, including the Roman rhetorician Quin -
 tillian (IC  AD /1921) and Ribot (1882), a doctor working with amnesic patients over a
century ago. In contemporary memory science, most distinguish between two forms of
consolidation, each with a distinct timescale (McGaugh, 2000). First, there is  synaptic
consolidation , which is the initial stabilization of the memory trace or engram that
occurs directly after learning (i.e., within the frst few minutes or hours). The process
of synaptic consolidation, in turn, is understood as the process by which these strength -
 ened connections are stabilized so as to make the engram long-lasting. Without synap -
 tic consolidation, the strengthened connection between neurons that occurred during
encoding will weaken and the memory will be lost (Weiler et al., 1995). Second, there
is  systems consolidation , which occurs at some time point after synaptic consolidation,
where the memory moves from the hippocampus to the neocortex neocortex. The
shifting of memories from one system to another is thought to aid in overall memory
storage by preventing the hippocampus from becoming too overloaded with memories.
The precise timescale for systems consolidation is a matter of some debate; it is thought
to occur anywhere from a few weeks or months to possibly even years or decades after
the initial encoding.
 Philosophical accounts of remembering have often focused on the other end of the
remembering process, namely, retrieval and the concomitant experience of remembering.
Specifcally, philosophers have emphasized the difference between the content brought to
mind in remembering and the content entertained during the initial experience. However,
this approach introduces the challenge of characterizing the precise relationship between
the contents of the encoded and the retrieved representations.  Content variantism  holds
that the content of the retrieved representation differs from that of the encoded one.
Throughout history, there have been a range of views on how precisely they differ. Some
philosophers suggested that the retrieved content is accompanied by an additional  cogni -
 tive  state, such as the belief that the remembered event happened in the past (James,
1890; Russell, 1921). Others held instead that the additional state was  affective , such as
an emotion or feeling of pastness (Spinoza, 1677/1985). Finally, for other philosophers,
the retrieved content is accompanied by a second-order representation or  apperception  of
the encoded content (Leibniz, 1714/1991).
 In contrast, preservationist views of remembering often advocate for content invari -
 antism, according to which the content of the encoded representation is identical to
that of the retrieved one (Zemach, 1968). The most prominent form of preservation-
ism has been the Causal Theory of Memory. The basic commitment to remembering
as a causal process is widespread; support can be found from Aristotle through to Ayer
(1956). The offcial Causal Theory of Memory, however, is generally attributed to Martin
and Deutscher (1966). The view, which has been refned and extended by others more
recently (e.g., Bernecker, 2010; Debus, 2010; Perrin, 2018), involves commitment to
remembering as a causal process, as well as an additional set of criteria that helps to
identify which causal relations between the past event and its subsequent activation
count as remembering.

research on the memory trace. There is a shared commitment to the existence of some

 such trace, but philosophers have focused primarily on its content, while neuroscientists
have focused on the underlying mechanism (De Brigard, 2020). On the philosophy side,
the debate has focused on how to interpret three putative conditions for an entity to
count as a memory trace, which derive from Aristotle’s account as described previously:
 (1)  the  causal condition , whereby a memory trace must play a causal role in the recollec -
 tion of the memory of which it is a trace; (2) the  retention condition , according to which
a memory trace must retain the experienced content from encoding to retrieval; and (3)
the  isomorphism condition , according to which a memory trace must structurally resemble
that which it represents (De Brigard, 2014a).
 Philosophical theories about memory trace content can be distinguished by how
they characterize the relationship between the content in the initial perceptual experi -
 ence and the content in the remembering of that experience. There are three general
approaches. According to  direct  representationalism , memory traces are representations
created only after perception. In this view, perception is unmediated and representa -
 tions of the object to be remembered are formed after this initial sensory act.  Semidirect
 representationalism, in contrast, characterizes perception as mediated by a representa -
 tion. This representation persists and is reactivated in remembering. In other words, the
memory trace is the very same representation created during the initial perception and
then retrieved during recollection. Finally,  indirect  representationalism takes perception
to be mediated by a representation, as semidirect representationalism does, but it takes
this representation to be numerically distinct from the representation that serves as the
memory trace. The representations may carry the same content but are not the same
tokens of that content.
 On the science side, memory researchers have long been dedicated to the search
 for the cognitive and neural mechanisms that make remembering possible (Box 21.2).
 In a pioneering work, the German biologist Semon (1921) coined the term  engram
 in the early 20th century, refashioning the age-old memory trace as the neurological
process by which information is encoded, stored, and retrieved. Semon’s work was cut
short by his early death. Work on engrams languished afterward and was challenged by
trenchant skepticism from Lashley (1933) and others. The engram’s revival began as
Hebb’s work on neural organization became applicable to memory. Hebb’s suggestion
was that the strength of the connection between neurons—the synapse—changes as a
function of the interaction, making it easier for these neurons to reactivate together,
thereby facilitating the retrieval of information encoded during the initial experience
(Hebb, 1949). This, in turn, led to the discovery of long-term potentiation as the
more precise mechanism for strengthening synaptic connections in memory (Bliss &
Lømo, 1973; Craver, 2003). Hebb’s theory of neural communication, coupled with
the discovery from Milner and colleagues that the hippocampus was the neural struc -
 ture supporting long-term memory (as discussed in Section 2), led neurobiologists to
look for these structural changes in the synaptic connectivity between hippocampal
neurons. Our understanding of how hippocampal neurons communicate and change
so as to support retention has been refned and revised over time, but nonetheless it
remains the case that “the prevailing view is that the formation of an engram involves
strengthening of synaptic connections between populations of neurons that are active
during encoding” (Josselyn et al., 2015, p. 421).

 Box 21.2  Neural mechanisms of episodic memory
 The case of H.M. evidenced the critical role the hippocampus plays in episodic
memory. Located in the middle of the temporal lobes, the hippocampus is a structure
of ~3.5 cm 3  of archicortex (archi = old). Unlike the neocortex, which comprises six
cortical layers, the hippocampus presents only three, and neuroanatomically it is
divided into the dentate gyrus, the cornu ammonis (CA) felds (CA1, CA2, and CA3),
and the subiculum. Along with the adjacent pre- and parasubiculum and the entorinal,
perirhinal, and parahippocampal cortices, these structures form the medial temporal
lobe (MTL).
 According to the SMM, when an event is encoded, it undergoes an initial
synaptic consolidation in the hippocampus, whereby the information is stabilized
 in a process that lasts from a few minutes to 24  hours (straight arrow in Fig -
 ure 21.2) . This rapid, synaptic consolidation can be interrupted, and its disruption
via drugs or trauma can lead to memory loss. Neurophysiologically, synaptic
consolidation critically depends on long-term potentiation, which contributes to the
strengthening of synapses and to neural plasticity (see Chapter  3). Information
that has been consolidated at the synaptic level in the hippocampus then undergoes
a second, systems consolidation, whereby the different components of the episode
are stored in the regions of the neocortex that initially process them (curved arrows
in Figure 21.2). Finally, at retrieval, the prefrontal cortex is thought to orchestrate
the reactivation of the relevant portions of the neocortex, leading to the reinstate -
 ment of the encoded memory (dotted arrows in Figure  21.2). This process is
thought to be independent of the hippocampus, which would explain why damage
in the hippocampus leads to anterograde amnesia—that is, the incapacity to
encode new episodic memories—but not to total retrograde amnesia, that is, the
incapacity to retrieve episodic information acquired prior to the cerebral insult.
 Recently, however, the standard view of consolidation has been challenged on
a number of fronts. One such challenge comes from the observation that the amount
of remnant hippocampal tissue seems to negatively correlate with the extent of the
retrograde amnesia, so that individuals with damage to the entire hippocampal
complex suffered complete amnesia regardless of the age of the past memories.
This suggest that either those memories were never fully consolidated, despite their
age, or that the hippocampus is still required for memory retrieval, which both go
against the traditional view of consolidation from the SMM. The  multiple trace
theory  (MTT) has been proposed to account for this and other conficting counter-
evidence, and it holds that although the prefrontal cortex may initiate the retrieval
effort, the hippocampus is still required to help to piece together the information
distributed in the neocortex (Nadel & Moscovitch, 1997; Moscovitch et al., 2005).
Another challenge to the standard view from the SMM comes from research on
reconsolidation, according to which, upon retrieval, memories become labile and
prone to modifcation. These fndings, once again, suggest that processes akin to
those of synaptic consolidation occur at retrieval, further suggesting that the MTL
is engaged both at encoding and retrieval of episodic information.

 2.  CONTEMPORARY ISSUES
 The story of the philosophy and neuroscience of memory, as told earlier, is of two inde -
 pendent research trajectories. In recent years, however, the concerns of these fields have
begun to intersect, resulting in more collaborative and interdisciplinary work.
 On the question of memory systems, philosophers have noticed that several empirical
dissociations captured by the SMM seem to mirror conceptual distinctions well-known
to them. For instance, some argue that the distinction between declarative-explicit and
nondeclarative-implicit memory refects the traditional distinction between know-that
and know-how (Ryle, 1949). As a result, some philosophers have used the SMM to argue
not only for a conceptual but for a psychological distinction between the two kinds of
knowledge (Wallis, 2008; Devitt, 2011; Glick, 2011; Brown, 2013). By contrast, oth -
 ers have used concerns about the SMM to argue against the claim that know-that and
know-how are psychologically different kinds of knowledge (Stanley & Krakauer, 2013;
De Brigard, 2019).
 The distinction between EM and SM also seems to coincide with conceptual distinc -
 tions drawn by philosophers (e.g., Augustine). However, there is still much disagree -
 ment as to what makes EM distinct from SM. One possibility is to characterize them
functionally, in the spirit of Tulving’s initial proposal. Accordingly, it has been suggested
that EM processes spatiotemporal information about  what, when , and  where  something
occurred. Such characterization separates EM from reportability and awareness and thus,
in principle, enables the possibility of identifying EM in nonhuman animals (Clayton &
Dickinson, 1998). But others disagree, arguing not only that SM could suffce to provide
 what-when-where  information but also that such characterization misses an essential fea -
 ture of EM: its peculiar phenomenology, that is, the fact that it feels like mentally travel -
 ing to one’s past. Unfortunately, understanding the psychological requirements of this
experience has proved elusive. Some suggest, for instance, that EM requires the capacity
to deploy temporal concepts (McCormack & Hoerl, 2008), or a sense of self (Rowlands,
2016), or even a noninferential connection with autonoetic consciousness (Klein, 2015).
 A related issue is that of distinguishing EM from imagination. Historically, both phi -
 losophers and scientists have acknowledged critical similarities between the two facul -
 ties. For instance, both involve representing objects or events that need not be present.
But what exactly distinguishes them is still a matter of debate. For some, the difference
between memory and imagination lies in something  intrinsic  to their content, such as
vivacity or associated cognitive or affective states. Unfortunately, these views are fraught
with problems, such as the fact that some imaginings can be experienced more vividly than
certain memories, or the fact that one may associate memory beliefs or feelings to con -
 tents one only imagined. Alternatively, some argue that the difference between memory
and imagination is  extrinsic  to their contents, as memories—unlike imaginations—need
to be true and/or should be causally related to the event they are about. Yet, once again,
these options face diffculties, not only because the precise causal relationship between
the encoded and remembered event is diffcult to characterize but also because memories
are often false (for a review, see De Brigard, 2017). As a result, many contemporary views
reject the claim that memory and imagination are two different faculties and suggest
instead that memory is a subclass of a more general cognitive capacity to generate mental
simulations (Schacter & Addis, 2007; De Brigard, 2014b; Michaelian, 2016).

 Just as the distinctions between forms of memory have begun to break down, recent
work on the process of remembering has brought challenges to our understanding of both
ends of the process—encoding  and  retrieval. On the encoding side, the idea that synaptic
consolidation brings permanence has been increasingly undermined by a steady line of
research, which indicates that memories undergo a new period of vulnerability every time
they are activated (Sara, 2000). Even though this mechanistic process is not identical to
the initial consolidation, it has become known as  reconsolidation  (Nader et al., 2000). Now,
instead of viewing consolidation as a one-time, fnalizing activity, neuroscientists think
that “consolidations never end” (Dudai, 2012, p. 227). The impact of reconsolidation on
our understanding of remembering goes beyond changes to our characterization of its
cellular and molecular mechanisms: it contributes to the increasingly popular view of
remembering as a dynamic process (Silva, 2007). Systems consolidation, too, is no longer
the received view of long-term memory preservation. In the previous section, we noted
the persistence of debates over its time course. This has been taken by many as reason
to question whether systems consolidation in fact occurs (Box 21.2).
 At the other end of the process, philosophical accounts of remembering have also faced
numerous challenges. Content variantists have offered various proposals for the “some -
 thing else” that accompanies the previously stored information in the act of remembering.
It is now well established that none of these proposed  memory markers  can adequately
capture all, and only, the relevant cases (Bernecker, 2010). Worse, these various accom -
 paniments to retrieved information, however characterized, appear to be woefully unreli -
 able. Decades of empirical work into the pervasiveness of memory errors reveal that the
feeling of remembering is fully separable from the successful retention of information.
Across a range of conditions, it is continually demonstrated that people can be led to
think that they are remembering when they are not (Loftus, 1997; Roediger & McDer -
 mott, 1995). In response, many philosophical accounts of remembering now rule out the
need for any particular feeling, judgment, or explicit endorsement of what is retained
(Martin & Deutscher, 1966), although there are some who continue to advocate for this
as an essential component (e.g., Debus, 2018). Either way, content variantism involves a
violation of the idea that remembering is focused exclusively on retention, and under -
 standing the precise degree of variation is the topic of current investigations (Michaelian,
2016; Bernecker & Michaelian, 2017).
 These challenges have contributed to a widespread shift in how remembering is char -
 acterized, from a process of  retention  to a process of  reconstruction . It is not entirely
clear what is meant by reconstruction, nor whether everyone who endorses this shift in
terminology has the same conception in mind. But generally, those who favor reconstruc -
 tion deny or downplay the role of explicitly stored information in memory and highlight
instead the information and activities that occur after the event being remembered and
at the time of recall.
 Changes to the process of remembering bring with them changes to the idea of what
is stored in memory across this process. Many philosophers of memory continue to dis -
 cuss and appeal to memory traces, although it is now common practice to challenge the
aforementioned conditions. The most common form of resistance is to the conception
of memory traces as localized in a particular representation or mechanism in the brain.
Sutton (1998) pioneered this line of work on memory traces, arguing for a view of traces
as distributed and dynamic in philosophical work on memory dating back to the 17th


<!-- Page 13 -->

Memory  337
 century. There are now many proposals for distributed traces on offer (e.g., Michaelian,
2011; De Brigard, 2014a). Other philosophers have argued that the contents of memory
traces can be extended beyond one’s brain (Clark & Chalmers, 1998) and even be distrib -
 uted across people (Sutton, 2010). There are also theorists who have suggested that what
makes a memory trace is not a categorical but a dispositional property (Robins, 2012;
De Brigard, 2014a, 2017). And, consistently, many currently argue that in order to fully
understand the nature of memory traces and the veracity of their conditions, we need to
turn to research in the cognitive psychology and neuroscience of memory.
 While conceptions of the memory trace are becoming increasingly less traditional in
philosophy, advances in cellular and molecular neuroscience have led many in this area
to believe that the engram, in the classical sense, has fnally been found (Josselyn et al.,
2015). The transition from early work on the neural mechanisms of memory to the current
place of the engram in memory science is the result of a steady trajectory of experimental
discovery across many areas and levels of neuroscience (Silva et al., 2014). These advances
have been made possible by the development of new experimental tools: the use of
transgenic model organisms, photon microscopy and other imaging techniques, and, most
recently and most signifcantly, optogenetics. Optogenetics is an intervention technique
that allows for the control of neural activity with light (Deisseroth, 2011). It has been used
to provide the frst model of false memory in nonhuman animals, to change the valence of
memories (from positive to negative and vice versa), and to recover memories that were
previously assumed to have been lost (Lui et al., 2012; Redondo et al., 2014; Ryan et al.,
2015). These results expand, enhance, and in some respects challenge assumptions about
the nature of memory and its underlying mechanisms. For some, these advances in the
neurobiology of memory provide support for a fully reductionist view of memory, where
the capacity is understood entirely in terms of the operations of these neural processes
(Bickle, 2006, 2020; see also Chapter 3). Those who support this reductionist picture
often speak in terms of engrams exclusively, setting aside the term memory trace and
associated concerns about content, representation, and subjective experience.
 3.  FUTURE DIRECTIONS
 As the science and philosophy of memory continue to develop and interanimate, it is dif -
 ficult to anticipate trends for future research. Current discussions, however, offer glimpses
of what may come. Our chapter began with the questions that shaped early inquiry—
 What is memory? What is remembering? What are memories ? As research continues and
interdisciplinary connections deepen, these questions are all being asked anew.
 First, on the question of memory types, it seems likely that the SMM will be rejected.
More and more empirical evidence is strongly suggesting that these allegedly distinct
memories are profoundly intertwined, both psychologically and neuroanatomically, and
that the traditional distinctions may not be carving nature at its joints. Indeed, recent
proposals have offered alternative taxonomies based either on the nature of memory
representations (Reder et al., 2009) or on their processes (Henke, 2010; Cabeza & Mosco -
 vitch, 2013). A related line of research pertains to the relationship between memory and
other cognitive processes, including not only imagination but also perception and working
memory. Despite the apparently obvious fact that they are distinct, there is much evidence


<!-- Page 14 -->

338  Felipe De Brigard and Sarah Robins
 of neural overlap and reuse across these structures, suggesting that our reconceptualiza -
 tion of memory may have to go beyond memory itself and into other cognitive capacities
(Barense et al., 2010).
 These changing ideas about memory’s systems and relation to other capacities impact,
too, our conception of remembering. Researchers are rethinking our understanding of
memory’s function. The traditional view held that the function of memory was to remem -
 ber the past, understood as encoding and storing a faithful representation of a past event
in order to retrieve it with fdelity (Boyle, 2019). With retention no longer central to
the process of remembering, this view is now contested. If retrieval is reconstructive,
then how should we understand the function of remembering? A range of alternative
functional hypotheses have been suggested, including the idea that memory is primarily
forward looking, in that its primary role is to enable simulations of possible future events
(Suddendorf & Corballis, 2007), or that its primary role is to strengthen social relations
(Alea & Bluck, 2003) or even to curb future decision-making (Boyer, 2008).
 All of these shifts make the long-standing commitment to the memory trace more
uncertain than ever. Moving forward, our progress in understanding memory traces will
follow the distinct lines of inquiry brought on by the development and use of new
investigative techniques in neuroscience and the theoretical exploration of the reasons
and requirements for traces among philosophers of memory. In addition, any signifcant
advances will require a rapprochement between these divergent approaches to the under -
 lying mechanisms.
 Neuroscientists will need to explore how far optogenetics can go in allowing us to
identify and manipulate memory traces. The technique thus far has been exciting because
it offers a way to intervene into the memory trace directly, circumventing the retrieval
process. In this way, it provides a novel workaround for many methodological challenges
of memory research (Robins, 2016, forthcoming). How this will change experimental
practice and how it will be further enhanced by the development of other techniques
have become topics of discussion, among both philosophers of science (Sullivan, 2016)
and neuroscientists (Josselyn et al., 2015). On the other end, as philosophers have jet -
 tisoned more and more of the traditional conditions on memory traces, there is renewed
exploration of why there has been so much agreement on the need for a trace to begin
with. Can we have an account of memory without it? Several recent trace-free proposals
are on offer (Hutto & Peeters, 2018; Michaelian, 2016). Others fnd it diffcult to jettison
the concept entirely (De Brigard, 2020) and make the kinds of modifcations discussed
previously. Unfortunately, there is little agreement about which conditions to retain and
which to let go.
 Summary of Key Ideas
 These are revolutionary times for the science of memory. Extant fndings are
debunking traditional models, and recent technological advances are enabling us
to study memory processes at increasingly diverse temporal and spatial resolutions.
Thankfully, philosophy of memory has taken notice and is undergoing a comple -
 mentary revolution of its own, revisiting traditional distinctions, reevaluating long-
established assumptions, and rebuking tacitly accepted conditions. The time is

In this chapter, we explored a few avenues for pursuing this development. Specif -

and its place in our social, emotional, and moral lives, questions about memory
are likely to persist and expand as the joint exploration of this capacity continues
