FACE  TO  FACE  WITH YOUR BRAIN
 The author's brain, scanned in 2003 on one of the first 3-tesla MRI magnets in the world, at
the Service Hospitalier Frédéric Joliot in Orsay, France. The surface of the author's cortex
was reconstructed in three dimensions using the Anatomist software, developed by Jean-
François Mangin's team and freely available at brainvisa.info.
 Seeing  your  own  brain  for  the  first  time  is  a  moving  and  intimate
experience. Does my whole person, my wits, my will, really fit into this kilo
and a half of soft matter? Can everything I feel, all I am, be reduced to the
discharge of a few tens of billions of neurons, carefully laid down by half a
billion years of evolution and fifty-seven years of education? And will brain
imaging discover in my convolutions some hidden secrets, new recipes for
happiness, or, on the contrary, some past or future disorder?


<!-- Page 9 -->

I will always remember my first brain scan. My nose crushed and
fighting claustrophobia, I slid inside the huge prototype magnetic resonance
imaging (MRI) magnet of the Service Hospitalier Frédéric Joliot, where I
had been working for the past few years. A few minutes later, my anatomy,
down to a submillimeter scale, scrolled on a computer screen—including a
large black hole in my white matter! Fortunately enough, it was a false
alarm, quickly dispelled by the neuroradiologist: just a glitch in the image
reconstruction algorithm.
There is nothing out of the ordinary in my brain anatomy, which is
typical of a middle-aged human adult. What is dazzling, however, is the
steady progress of brain exploration techniques and the scientific advances
that they now afford in linking body and mind. The skull has become
transparent:  thoughts  can  now  be  decoded  from  brain  signals,  and
neurological  and  psychiatric  diseases  are  finally  beginning  to  be
understood. In the course of my career, I have visited many of these
emerging “observatories of the mind” where one can visualize the entire
living human brain in a matter of seconds, or the activity of every single
neuron in a small animal. Europe, China, the United States, and Japan have
launched ambitious, billion-dollar projects to map the human brain—and I
was part of those bold and controversial efforts. I have also seen the first
mathematical theories attempt to explain how the material activity of
neurons can engender our conscious thoughts.
It is this worldwide project, this global intellectual adventure, that I want
to recount here by sharing with you the most spectacular images that
punctuate this conquest of the brain. My choice is inevitably subjective, but
it allows me to explain, in the simplest possible terms, what we can see with
our brain scanners, how we make it visible, and to what extent a neural
code for our thoughts emerges from those recordings.
Welcome to my cabinet of curiosities—and welcome to the intimacy of
 your  brain! As you browse through the following pages, I would like you to
keep in mind a stunning thought: all the gorgeous neurobiological processes
that you are about to discover are taking place,  right now , inside your own
brain. This book is a mirror of your mind. Seize it and discover, deep inside
yourself,  the  concealed  machinery  that  generates  your  thoughts  and
feelings.

THE  PIONEERS
 Thomas Geminus,  Compendiosa totius anatomiae delineatio aere exarata  (1545).
 Brain: An apparatus with which we think we think.
 —Ambrose Bierce ,  The Devil's Dictionary  (1906)
 We are our brains. To paraphrase Shakespeare's Prospero, brains are the real
stuff as dreams are made on—in fact, not just dreams, but also feelings,
thoughts, perceptions of all sorts.
How was this fact discovered? Who launched the conquest of the brain?
Which bold explorer first dared to dissect a corpse and explore the very
organ of the self?
Prehistory gives us evidence of many a successful skull trepanation—the
drilling of a hole into the skull, typically to release intracranial pressure
from a brain injury. Such daring surgeries suggest that humans quickly
understood that the mind relates to the brain. Egyptian doctors already


<!-- Page 11 -->

knew about the main structures of the brain and their pathologies. However,
as we will see in the following pages, it was not until the 19th century, with
the study of lesions, and then the 20th century and the inception of brain
imaging, that we became fully aware of this simple fact: at all scales, from
the molecule to the synapse, the neuron, the circuit, and the convolutions of
the cortex, all the structures of the brain serve only one purpose: to think.
From the surgeon's scalpel to positron emission tomography and MRI,
the evolution of brain exploration tools played a central role in this
conquest. The British zoologist J. Z. Young once remarked that “we create
tools and shape ourselves by using them.” In neuroscience, this phrase takes
on a particular resonance: each new image of the brain invites us to rethink
the links between our body and our mind.

THE DREAM OF A  THOUGHT DECODER
 Engraving by Mattheus Greuter (16th century), available in the collections of the Wellcome
Foundation,  https://wellcomecollection.org.
 A man is lying on a stretcher. He is conscious, as evidenced by his waving
hand. However, his head is hidden inside an alchemy oven, and from the
chimney flows a flurry of images, ideas, signs, and dreams. He is lost in his
thoughts, but his mental images have lost all intimacy: everyone can now
see them, and if he thinks that he can hide from us the size of his underwear
or the identity of his mistress, he is mistaken: you will find them, in a good
place, in the drawings that divulge what is on his mind!
In this engraving from the beginning of the Renaissance, a twenty-first-
century neuroscientist cannot help but see the anticipation of a familiar


<!-- Page 13 -->

device: a functional magnetic resonance imaging machine, or functional
MRI for short—a machine that can measure the activity of the human brain,
down to the darkest corners of our cerebral convolutions, and project them
in the form of images readable by others.
How real is this vision? Do such mind-reading machines already exist?
What exactly can we read from an MRI scan of the living human brain? Do
we understand enough about the genesis of our thoughts to reconstruct them
from a mere peek at the underlying brain circuits? Does each of our ideas
correspond to a decodable brain state, and to what extent can we decipher
them from the outside? And how did scientists get to this point?

HEART OR BRAIN?  METAPHORS OF MINDS
 Gregor Reisch,  Margarita philosophica  (1503). Lib. X, trac. II, fig. 18.
 Aristotle (384–322 BCE) wrongly thought that the heart, not the brain, is
the organ of mind. The reason was simple: do our heartbeats not track the
flow of our thoughts and emotions? It is to this intuitive theory that we still
owe many of our expressions, particularly those that convey our passions: a
heart of stone, to break your heart, taking things to heart, my heart melts for
my sweetheart, and other heartfelt sentiments.
The brain was then seen as a refrigerator whose main role was to cool
the blood that our emotions heated. However, in ancient times, extending
the observations of Egyptian therapists, a Greek physician, Hippocrates


<!-- Page 15 -->

(460–375 BCE), had already discovered the truth: it is our brain, rather than
our heart, that feels, ponders, and reasons. Hippocrates knew that its
diseases could paralyze us, make us lose speech, or turn us into epileptic
puppets—epilepsy  was  the  “sacred  disease,”  already  recognized  for
centuries in Indian Ayurvedic medicine.
But how did this strange organ work? The Hippocratic school likened the
brain to the only machines that were available at the time: the hydraulic and
pneumatic automata of Heron of Alexandria, which brought to a mechanical
life the statues and the doors of temples. The metaphor gave birth to the
first model of the brain: the theory of humors, four bodily fluids—blood,
phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile—that were thought to convey the
messages of the body. These four substances had to remain balanced for a
person to remain in good health—good-humored, neither too sanguine or
too phlegmatic, without excess bile or melancholy.
By following up on the first careful brain dissections, performed by
Herophilus of Chalcedon and his school of anatomy (about 330–260 BCE),
the Greek physician Galen (129–201) obtained evidence that seemed to
strongly support the theory of humors: the brain was indeed hollow and
comprised several vessels filled with liquid. This was the discovery of the
cerebral ventricles. As for the nerves, they looked like pipes that could carry
the humors and therefore convey information to and from the entire body.
Thanks to Galen's writings, the Hippocratic theory spread throughout the
Middle Ages and into the Renaissance. In this image by Gregor Reisch
(1467–1525), author of an encyclopedia called  The Philosophical Pearl , we
see the brain subdivided into three ventricles. The first, the seat of the
“common sense,” gathers information from taste, smell, hearing, and sight
—and its recesses are called fantasy and imagination. The faculties of
cogitation and estimation are located in the second ventricle, while the third
ensures, as in a cellar, the long-term storage of memories.

VESALIUS:  THE ERA OF PRECISION
ANATOMY
 Andreas Vesalius,  De humani corporis fabrica  (1543). Wellcome Foundation Collection,
 https://wellcomecollection.org.
 A period of artistic and scientific ebullition, the Renaissance revisited, with
a critical eye, the theories of the great scholars of antiquity.
Andreas  Vesalius  (1514–1564)  was  Renaissance's  most  renowned
anatomist. From an early age, he grew a fascination for the human body.
His birthplace, in Brussels, was located opposite the gallows, and he


<!-- Page 17 -->

sometimes borrowed bones from there. His training as a medical student led
him to attend the greatest European universities: Louvain, Paris, Padua, and
finally Venice, where he became a professor of surgery and anatomy at the
age of twenty-three.
While the faculties of medicine of the time mainly dissected Galen's
writings, Vesalius sought the truth at its source, in the library of human
anatomy. At the age of twenty, in Paris, he became one of the first dissectors
of corpses, which he obtained from the gallows of Montfaucon or the Holy
Innocents’ cemetery. His reputation grew as he pointed out the errors of the
ancient anatomists and their translators. From then on, his entire life was
devoted to understanding what he called, with Cicero, the “fabric” of the
human body: the arrangement of its parts into a harmonious whole, a
sophisticated mechanism where each organ seemed to fall perfectly in place
within an overall functional architecture.
In 1543, he published his masterpiece:  De humani corporis fabrica , “On
the fabric of the human body,” an encyclopedia in seven volumes and
nearly  700  pages,  illustrated  with  superb  anatomical  plates.  These
woodcuts, true works of art, remain appreciated by collectors to this day.
The illustrators are not known with certainty, but some engravings may be
due  to  pupils  of Titian,  perhaps  even  the  master  himself,  and  some
speculated that Leonardo da Vinci could have contributed.
The beauty of those anatomical images is not only plastic. In the
 Fabrica , art and science meet in an unclassifiable work that, by laying bare
the splendor and the complexity of the human body, invites a scientific
explanation. Vesalius’ masterpiece would inspire several generations of
brain explorers. Counterfeits were innumerable: until the end of the 17th
century, there was hardly a book on the brain that did not copy his
engravings. The most famous of these plagiarists was a certain . . . René
Descartes.

RENÉ DESCARTES’S  COGITATIVE
SUBSTANCE


<!-- Page 20 -->

René Descartes,  De homine  (1662). Engraving by Florentio Schuyl, Andreas Vesalius,
available in the collections of the Wellcome Foundation,  https://wellcomecollection.org.
 While Andreas Vesalius painted the anatomy of the brain and nerves in


<!-- Page 21 -->

every  detail,  René  Descartes  (1596–1650)  sought  to  understand  it. A
philosopher, mathematician, physicist, but also an aspiring physiologist,
Descartes was fascinated by the origin of ideas: where do our thoughts
come from, and how do we come to know things?
 His  Treatise on Man  was so revolutionary that, for fear of the Church, he
did not publish it during his lifetime. The book appeared in print only in
1662, twelve years after the author's death, in a Latin translation illustrated
with engravings by Schuyl. It contained a stunning revelation: Descartes
was a stanch materialist, at least as far as the body was concerned.
According  to  him,  the  human  body  is  just  a  sophisticated  machine,
controlled by the great organ of the brain. The nerves are pipes that transmit
the pressure of “animal spirits,” material corpuscles composed of the “finest
and most subtle” parts of the blood. When those spirits reach the pineal
gland, in the center of the brain, they cause it to tilt one way or the other,
thus redirecting the pressure to various muscles and giving rise to all our
movements.  When  we  point  a  finger  at  an  arrow,  “the  idea  of  this
movement of the limbs consists only in the way in which these spirits come
out of this gland.”
Descartes uses a whole series of metaphors: the brain is a loom that
receives the imprint of memory; it is a receptacle of fluids that can lean
only in a single direction, thus forbidding us from attending to more than
one thing at a time; it is also a wind organ whose pressure regulates
consciousness and sleep. A resolute materialist, Descartes speculated that


<!-- Page 22 -->

“these functions follow quite naturally in this machine from the sole
disposition of its organs, no more and no less than do the movements of a
clock.”
Why, then, do we remember him as a defender of the sanctity of the
soul? On reading his  Treatise on Man , it does seem strange that Descartes
was also the originator of Cartesian dualism, the idea that the human soul
rests on a distinct “cogitative substance” radically different from ordinary
matter. Descartes, however, was just being honest: he failed to grasp how
the automatisms of the body could explain two faculties that he considered
unique to our species: the faculty “to use words by composing them” and
the immense flexibility of our behaviors. Those faculties were beyond
mechanics, Descartes argued, and required a soul. The idea that the brain
machine could contain language circuits that caused, mechanically, our
words remained foreign to him.

THOMAS WILLIS,  EXPLORER OF THE
CORTEX AND NERVES
 Thomas Willis, schematic section of the human brain showing the cortex, striatum, and
cerebellum.  De anima brutorum quae hominis vitalis ac sensitiva est  (1672). Wellcome
Foundation Collections,  https://wellcomecollection.org.
 Descartes’ ideas remained rooted in antiquity: he still conceived of the brain
as a hydraulic or pneumatic machine, in the spirit of Heron of Alexandria's
automata. It is to Thomas Willis (1621–1675), a physician and professor at
Oxford, that we owe a great turn toward modernity: the definitive rejection
of the ventricular theory of the brain. Willis understood that the ventricles,
while real, did not play a major role in thought. Their liquid contents served
only to collect the waste of the nervous system, from the medulla to the


<!-- Page 24 -->

brain. It was in the folds of the cerebral cortex that the origins of thought
should be sought.
1
 Willis owed his fame to a curious incident. In 1650, he obtained the
corpse of a woman, Anne Green, who has just been hanged for the murder
of her baby—but under Willis's attentive care, she resuscitated! After twelve
hours, she could talk, four days later she started eating again, and she lived
to give birth to three other children.
This episode illustrates Willis's remarkable attention to his patients, to
which he owes his most important discoveries. By carefully keeping track
of  their  symptoms  and,  after  their  death,  correlating  them  with  the
observations he made during dissection, he attempted to understand how the
brain worked.
In 1663, Willis was elected a member of the young Royal Society. The
following year, his treatise on cerebral anatomy was published.  Cerebri
anatome  was to remain the bible of neurologists for nearly two centuries.
Willis's discoveries were innumerable: he named many cerebral structures
such as the claustrum, the striatum, or the anterior commissure; he revised
the classification of the cranial nerves, which dated back to Galen; he traced
the anatomical course of the nerves and the medulla, illustrating them with
superb engravings by Christopher Wren, the future architect of St. Paul's
Cathedral in London; and finally, he established the difference, still relevant
today, between
the central nervous system: the brain and spinal cord
the peripheral nervous system: the sensory and motor nerves
the autonomic nervous system, which controls, in an involuntary and
unconscious way, our digestive organs, smooth muscles, heart, and
some endocrine glands.
In his second book on the brain,  De anima brutorum  (On the soul of
brutes), Willis argued that the folds of the cortex shelter all our conscious
thoughts and actions. As the brain gets more and more folded, Willis states,
we move from “brutes,” blindly submitting to instincts, as is typical of
smooth-brained animals such as fish, birds, or even cats, to the exercise of
free will that reaches its pinnacle in humans.

PHRENOLOGY:  TALENTS AND BUMPS
 Lithograph by J. Lump (1826),  George Combe Lecturing on Phrenology, Portrayed with
Protuberances on His Head . Wellcome Foundation Collection,
 https://wellcomecollection.org.
 René Descartes, a pioneer of neurophysiology, remained unable to envisage
a machine that could speak and think. While he lacked imagination, many
enlightenment philosophers of the eighteenth century had too much of it. At
that time, speculation on the origin of the soul was going loose, and never
mind if the data were still lacking. Thus, the French physician Julien de la
Mettrie  (1709–1751)  did  not  hesitate  to  publish  two  ultra-materialist
pamphlets,  A Natural History of the Soul  (1745) and  Man a Machine
 (1748). For him, a man was just an animal like any other—he merely had a
slightly larger brain, and he was better “trained”: “A man has been trained


<!-- Page 26 -->

as an animal. . . . A geometer has learned to make the most difficult
demonstrations and calculations, like a monkey to take off or put on his
little hat” (Julien de la Mettrie,  Man a Machine  [1748]).
 For this staunch empiricist, the case was clear: the brain was comprised
of  automatisms  similar  to  those  of  Vaucanson's  Digesting  Duck,  an
automaton that stunned Versailles. “The soul,” says La Mettrie, “is but an
empty word, of which no one has any idea, and which an enlightened man
should use only to signify the part in us that thinks.” The outcry was huge:
the French parliament condemned La Mettrie, and he had to leave on exile
while his book went up in smoke.
Heir to these debates, the German physician and neuroanatomist Franz
Josef Gall (1758–1828) set out to collect more empirical data. Gall began
his career by inventing a brilliant dissection method . . . but soon went
down another path, and ended up measuring the hollows and bumps of the
skull. He became convinced that each mental faculty possesses a distinct
seat in the cortex and that since the brain exerts pressure on the cranium, the
greater or lesser expansion of our talents was faithfully reflected on the
skull. Scientists had a hump for math, criminals the bulge of cunning; even
the “love of one's offspring” or the “sense of satire and witticism” could
now be located in the brain.
Gall's cranioscopy, which was renamed “phrenology” in 1810 by his
assistant Johann Spurzheim, was all the rage until the middle of the
nineteenth century. It took a debate at the French Academy of Sciences,
sponsored by Napoleon, for the French physiologist Pierre Flourens to


<!-- Page 27 -->

refute its principles. Flourens's  Examination of Phrenology , from 1842, was
without  appeal:  this  pseudoscience  was  just  a  muddle  of  unfounded
speculations. This did not prevent it from having a legacy that was at once
dubious and useful. It gave birth, on the one hand, to the eccentric theory of
“criminal man,” by the Italian criminologist Cesare Lombroso (1835–
1909), who stated that a man's face betrayed his propension to crime, and
on the other, to the reliable field of anthropometry, the measurement of the
human body and face, which became used by police forces worldwide.

PAUL BROCA:  THE ERA OF LESIONS
 Brain of Mr. Leborgne, known as “Tan,” preserved in the Dupuytren Museum and scanned
with MRI by Nina Dronkers in the neuroradiology department of the Salpêtrière Hospital in
Paris.
 The analysis of brain lesions would finally shed some crucial light on the
origins of language. As early as 1769, in  D’Alembert's Dream , Denis
Diderot (1713–1784) imagined the effects of various damages to Isaac
Newton's brain:


<!-- Page 29 -->

For example, if I remove from Newton the two auditory threads, he has no more sense of sound; the
olfactory threads, no more sense of smell; the optic threads, no more sense of colour; the taste
threads, no more sense of taste—if I cut out or mix up the others, then farewell to the organic
structure  of  the  brain,  farewell  memory,  judgement,  desires,  aversions,  passions,  willing,
consciousness of the self. (Denis Diderot,  D’Alembert's Dream  [written in 1769])
 Was is a dream, or could it be reality? A French physician, Paul Broca
(1824–1880), made a decisive observation. For some years, Broca had been
aware of the observations of a Parisian colleague, the physician Jean-
Baptiste Bouillaud, according to which a cerebral attack could lead to
speech arrest—yet these ideas smacked of phrenology, by now a discredited
pseudo-science. However, in 1861, Broca himself examined a certain M.
Leborgne, who had been hospitalized at Bicêtre for the sudden loss, twenty-
one years earlier, of all articulated language. He was nicknamed “Tan,”
because this was the only syllable he could utter—besides, curiously, a
single swear, “Sacré nom de Dieu” (Holy name of God), which obviously
relied on a completely different brain circuit!
A few weeks later, Leborgne died, and Broca rushed to autopsy him. He
discovered that Leborgne's brain had suffered a significant lesion in the
inferior frontal gyrus, which could explain the loss of speech. Broca
claimed that this region was the center of articulate language—and it would
become known as “Broca's area.”
Preserved in formalin, Leborgne's brain would speak again in 2007,
when it was scanned by Nina Dronkers and other colleagues at the Hôpital
de la Salpêtrière in Paris.
2
In addition to the lesion identified by Broca, an
MRI scan revealed further damage, particularly to a subcortical nucleus, the
putamen,  and  to  a  large  fiber  bundle  called  the  arcuate  fasciculus.
Interestingly, we now know that all of these structures, together with several
additional regions of the temporal lobe, are jointly involved in language.
Since Broca's time, other observations, notably those of the German
neurologist Carl Wernicke (1848–1905), have confirmed that the faculty of
language emerges from a large-scale circuit, not just a single brain area.
Neurologists could readily see how distinct brain lesions, almost always
lateralized to the left hemisphere, could induce different types of aphasia.
Some language disorders affect the knowledge of grammar, others the
meaning of single words. Sometimes verbs are more impaired than nouns,
and others the converse. Started at the end of the 19th century, the


<!-- Page 30 -->

classification of aphasias remains a controversial topic of research to this
day.

NEURONS:  THE BUTTERFLIES OF THE
SOUL
 Drawings of neurons and circuits by Santiago Ramón y Cajal. Left: Schematic drawing of
motor and sensory pathways. Right: Purkinje cell from the human cerebellum.
 Under the lead of the Spanish neuroanatomist Santiago Ramón
y Cajal (1852–1934), brain anatomy would take a giant step
forward.
 Ramón y Cajal was born to a doctor who took him, at the age of sixteen,
to the cemetery to dig up bones and study them. His own medical degree in
hand, he quickly became fascinated by the microscopic study of the brain.


<!-- Page 32 -->

Indeed, a new method has just appeared: silver staining, also called “Golgi
staining” or the black reaction. Invented by the Italian Camillo Golgi, then
refined by Cajal himself, it involved dipping brains in a bath of silver salts
until one could see, in a deep black color, the complete arborescence of
individual nerve cells.
It was with this tool, which he liberally applied to various brain regions,
that Ramón y Cajal made his central discovery: the cellular organization of
the brain. At the time, the field was dominated by Golgi's reticular theory,
according to which the nervous tissue formed a single “syncytium,” a
continuous net without any cellular boundaries. “This is not what I see,”
Cajal retorted: the brain, like any organ, is made up of distinct cells. True,
these neurons almost touch each other at the synapses, the points of
communication from one neuron to another. However, they are quite
disjoint,  separated  by  a  gap.  Cajal's  “neuronal  theory”  became  the
foundation of contemporary neuroscience.
By studying the organization of nerve tissue under the microscope,
Ramón y Cajal also began to understand how neurons work. He adorned his
drawings with arrows that indicated the alleged direction of the nerve
impulse. Nervous messages, Cajal inferred, must be emitted from the cell
body. They travel along the axon, the long appendage that leaves each
neuron, until they reach the synapses, at which point they jump over the gap
and get a chance to influence other neurons. As we shall see in a few pages,
this jump across cells involves the release of a neurotransmitter, a molecule
that serves as a messenger and bridges across the synaptic cleft.
Ramón y Cajal understood that through their arborescence, called the
“dendritic  tree,”  neurons  could  collect  many  of  these  messages  and
synthesize from other cells. He also foresaw that this transmission was
plastic: neurons could grow, their branches could multiply, new synapses
could be formed or retracted. Such growth followed by pruning is now
thought to be the main basis of learning.
Camilo Golgi and Santiago Ramón y Cajal were jointly awarded the
Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1906 “in recognition of their
work on the structure of the nervous system.” But Cajal has a special place
in the hearts of neuroscientists: he was a superb anatomist but also an artist,
and his drawings continue to inspire generations of students and showcase
the beauty of neurons, which he affectionately nicknamed the “butterflies of
the soul.”

ONE BRAIN,  43 AREAS:  KORBINIAN
BRODMANN
 Subdivision of the human cortex into Brodmann areas, from K. Brodmann (1909),
 Vergleichende Lokalisationslehre der Grosshirnrinde  (Leipzig: Johann Ambrosius Barth).
 The  German  neurologist  Korbinian  Brodmann  (1868–1918)  was
responsible for the first complete map of the human cortex.
A passionate physician, disrespectful of the artificial boundaries that
separate anatomy from neurology and psychiatry, Brodmann first practiced
medicine  in  Lausanne,  Munich,  and Alexanderbad. There  he  met  the
psychiatrist Aloysius Alzheimer, before joining the anatomy laboratory of
Cécile and Oskar Vogt in Berlin. It was there that he dedicated himself to
the great scientific problem of the moment: could the human cortex be
subdivided into distinct regions, as the phrenologists had imagined, and as
Broca's discovery intimated? Did it include areas specific to the human
species, be it for language or for socializing? And if Charles Darwin was


<!-- Page 34 -->

right, did each of these regions possess an evolutionary precursor in other
animals?
To answer these questions, Brodmann gathered dozens of brains of all
species: deer, goat, kangaroo,  Cercopithecus  (an Old World monkey),
marmoset, macaque, orangutan—and of course  Homo sapiens . Those rare
anatomical pieces, sometimes unique, he would carefully slice into thin
sections, then color them with the dye invented by his colleague Frank
Nissl. We now know that this dye binds to the RNA and DNA of neurons,
which it colors a deep blue. Lit in this unreal color, the starry night of the
cortex appears in all its complexity. It ceases to be this continuous and
grayish ribbon, the “gray matter,” and becomes a universe of neurons of all
sizes and shapes.
 In the human brain, Brodmann distinguished six successive layers of
neurons. From one region to another, these layers varied considerably in
thickness, density, and size of the nerve cells. On this basis, Brodmann was
able to distinguish no fewer than forty-three distinct regions, to which he
assigned numbers.
3
Most of them were present in all mammals, but some
seemed species-specific—this is why Brodmann numbers go up to 52 in
humans, but skip certain numbers (12–16 and 48–51): according to him,
these areas, especially the olfactory ones, exist in other primates, but not in
us.
Brodmann's anatomical methods were mute about the function of all
these areas. It was only very gradually that scientists began to discover their


<!-- Page 35 -->

specific roles, such as the reception of visual, auditory, or tactile inputs, or
the control of motor gestures. In higher-level areas of the prefrontal,
parietal, and temporal lobes, the links between structure and function
remain uncertain to this day.

A CENTURY LATER:  BRODMANN  CRITICIZED
 Image from the BigBrain project, a collaboration between the Jülich Research Center
(Germany) and the Montreal Neurological Institute, partly funded by the European
Community's Human Brain Project. Data available on  ftp://bigbrain.loris.ca.
 Brodmann's successors did not hesitate to criticize his map of the cortex.
Indeed, it revealed only a limited aspect of the brain's architecture: its
“cytoarchitectony,” the anatomical distribution of nerve cells in layers of
varying thickness and size. However, as can be seen in this recent image
from the BigBrain project (2020),
4
the changes in thickness of the six layers
of the cortex, from one region to another, are often so gradual as to be
almost  imperceptible.  This  is  why  the  boundaries  that  Brodmann
confidently  drew  were,  in  fact,  somewhat  subjective.  Moreover,  their


<!-- Page 37 -->

insufficiently  fine  granularity  did  not  do  full  justice  to  the  genuine
complexity of the cortex.
By adding information on brain connections, which vary greatly from
one  area  to  another,  Cécile  and  Oskar  Vogt,  Brodmann's  colleagues,
managed to distinguish up to 200 cortical regions, a number undoubtedly
closer to the anatomical and functional reality.
But the main criticism was that Brodmann's map was blind to the
variability between brains. It is not fully clear how many human brains
Brodmann  used  for  his  publication—probably  very  few,  because  his
anatomical methods took enormous time. Even in 1988, the famous atlas of
Talairach and Tournoux,
5
which would long serve as a reference for the
emerging science of brain imaging, was based on the serial sections of only
a single brain, that of an elderly woman.
 The problem is that, as we now know, Brodmann areas are highly
variable from one person to the next. Their borders can shift by about one
centimeter, and their area can vary by a factor of two or more. Furthermore,
such variations are not without functional consequences. As the surface of
the primary visual area varies by a factor of three, it also affects the way we
perceive the world. The larger our visual area, V1, the better our vision, and
the less we fall into visual traps such as the famous Ebbinghaus illusion,
which leads us to misperceive the size of a circle.
6
 It was therefore essential that researchers set out to replicate and extend
Brodmann's work. This would become the vocation of two contemporary
German neuroanatomists, Karl Zilles and Katrin Amunts.

A MORE VARIABLE  BRAIN THAN IT SEEMS
 Left: Images from the Jülich Brain Atlas of the Jülich Research Center (Germany). Right:
Image created using the European E-Brains Atlas, financed by a European consortium
within the framework of the European Human Brain Project. The colors delineate different
brain areas and their average contours.
 As the new director of Cécile and Oskar Vogt's laboratory in Düsseldorf, the
physician  and  neuroanatomist  Karl  Zilles  (1944–2020)  set  himself  a
colossal  goal:  he  would  replicate  Brodmann's  work,  but  with  much
improved  objectivity. To  this  aim,  he  developed  quantitative  methods
capable of identifying the boundaries between regions without calling on
the observer's subjective eye. He also aimed to label the entire cortex, even
within the depth of its folds, rather than confine himself to the mere surface,
as Brodmann did—and of course, this required finding ways to report the
data in all its three-dimensional precision.


<!-- Page 39 -->

Zilles also intended to characterize the variations from person to person.
This ambition required brains, lots of brains, and so Zilles set up a fast brain
donation  system.  In  several  German  hospitals,  terminally  ill  patients,
perfectly informed and aware of the importance of scientific research,
accepted,  in  agreement  with  their  families,  to  donate  their  brains
immediately after death. As soon as death was confirmed, their brain was
immediately perfused and scanned using MRI while it was still intact and in
its normal position within the cranial cavity. In the following hours, it was
removed and fixed in thin sections that would then take years of patient
work to process at the cellular level.
The result of this colossal endeavor is an unmatched database of human
brain anatomy,
7
which maps, within a shared anatomical space, a variety of
data on the neuronal architecture of ten human brains. This map largely
confirms  the  boundaries  discovered  a  century  earlier  by  Korbinian
Brodmann. On average, all these regions, like a colored stained-glass
window, line the cortex in the expected places. However, the new map
refines their contours, visualizes them in three dimensions, and, above all,
provides an estimate of their variation from one person to the next. And this
variation is far from negligible. The core of each region is present in all
brains, but its contours vary. The borders push each other away, like in a
vast game of  Diplomacy . Each  Homo sapiens  is at once similar and unique:
from a universal deck, we each draw a slightly different set of cards that
partly betray our character, our talents, and our passions.

BIGBRAIN:  A UNIQUE BRAIN

(Germany) and the Montreal Neurological Institute, partly funded by the European Human

 Karl Zilles passed away in 2020 at the age of seventy-six. However, his
work is far from over, and it continues within the hands of his disciple
Katrin Amunts, in the German city of Jülich. Indeed, many areas remain
uncharted: they have not yet been mapped with the necessary precision. To


<!-- Page 41 -->

sidestep the vast amounts of tedious work needed, Zilles and Amunts
launched another revolutionary program, resting squarely on advances in
computer science: the BigBrain project, a genuine Big Bang for human
neuroanatomy.
The ambition of the BigBrain project is extraordinary: to collect the
position of  all  the neurons of the human brain, in order to understand its
three-dimensional  architecture,  and  to  integrate  it  into  computer
simulations, in particular those of the European Human Brain Project.
To give an idea of the scale of this endeavor, let us recall that our brain
comprises 86 billion neurons, and as many glial cells, which surround and
support them. In mammals, most of neuroscience research focuses on the
mouse, whose circuits are far from being mapped yet—and yet the volume
of its brain is 7,500 times smaller than ours!
 Katrin Amunts and her colleagues have not yet managed to achieve their
dream. However, in 2013, they published a pioneering paper in the journal
 Science .
8
They reported on the digitization of an entire human brain at the
extraordinary  spatial  resolution  of  20  ×  20  ×  20  microns,  which  is
practically  the  cellular  scale—indeed,  the  cell  bodies  of  the  largest
pyramidal neurons in our cortex are a few tens of microns in size.


<!-- Page 42 -->

Everything is out of the ordinary in this project: the dissection of a fresh
brain into thin, 20-micron slides, their digitization for a thousand hours,
their  realignment  using  high-performance  computers  to  correct  for
inevitable deformations. The final result is a mine of information freely
available to neuroanatomists worldwide, and which can be merged with
other  genetic  and  connectivity  data.  We  now  possess  multimodal
computerized atlases of the human brain that go beyond the dreams of
Vesalius, Broca, Ramón y Cajal, or Brodmann.

SEEING EVERY NEURON  ONE BY ONE
 Cortical neurons of the mouse brain, colored with Brainbow and imaged on Emmanuel
Beaurepaire's two-photon microscope at École Polytechnique in Palaiseau, France.
 In some animals, the dream of visualizing every single neuron of the brain,
one by one, in every detail, is about to come true. Take a good look at the
figure: each neuron has a different color, and yet this is a raw picture,
whose shades owe nothing to Photoshop. The mouse brain that was imaged
genuinely possesses multicolored neurons, whose brilliant hues can be
visualized  with  a  fluorescence  microscope. Their  distinct  colors  have
quickly become an essential tool for neuroscientists: they allow us to trace
the arborescence of each neuron in three dimensions, without confusing
them with others.


<!-- Page 46 -->

This revolution, born at Harvard University in 2007, is the outcome of
several major advances in genetic engineering and photonics. It all began
with the identification of a fluorescent protein found in certain jellyfish,
which lights them up in a brilliant green color when they are illuminated
with ultraviolet light. The second step was the transfer of this protein, and
several of its red and blue variants, to mice. Genetic engineering made it
possible for virtually any cell, anywhere in the body, to express these
proteins. Suddenly, any cell could emit fluorescent light, possibly under the
control of other electrical or molecular signals. Scientists even created an
entire fluorescent green mouse!
In the Brainbow method, each mouse neuron expresses up to three or
four fluorescent proteins, at random and to a variable degree. The mixture
of  these  proteins  creates  more  than  a  hundred  distinct  hues.  Under
ultraviolet light, the brain lights up like a rainbow—hence the name
“Brainbow.” Suddenly, Ramón y Cajal's butterflies of the soul flutter in a
flurry of color.
At École Polytechnique, Emmanuel Beaurepaire and his team achieved
an additional feat: they managed to scan all of these neurons in three
dimensions, with their original colors, at the amazing spatial scale of less
than a micron. Their serial images cover the entire mouse brain, for a total
of  75  million  neurons,  thus  characterizing  rodent  anatomy  with
unprecedented precision.

AT LEAST  10,000 SYNAPSES  PER NEURON
 Electron microscopy reconstruction of a 250 ×140 × 90 micron volume of mouse cortex.
Data from a consortium of the Allen Institute for Brain Science (Seattle), Princeton
University, and Baylor College of Medicine in Houston, freely available at  https://microns-

 What defines the function of a neuron is not its shape or its location, but its
connections. Each cortical neuron receives about 10,000 synapses, which
are the entry points of messages from other neurons. By recombining those
myriads of inputs, excitatory or inhibitory, within the complex tree structure
of its dendrites, each neuron acquires the peculiar response profile that
defines it as a visual, auditory, tactile, motor, or cognitive cell.
To fully understand how the brain works, it will therefore be essential to
map out its full connectivity matrix: which neuron is on talking terms with
which, and how intensely they chat. The problem is that there are about 86


<!-- Page 48 -->

billion neurons in the cortex (about 1011 neurons) and that their synapses
therefore number in the millions of billions (1015). Moreover, the branches
of all these neurons are intermingled into an incredibly tight gordian knot at
the nanometer scale (one millionth of a millimeter). The tangle is so
complex  that,  when  one  sees  a  synapse  under  the  microscope,  it  is
extremely difficult to trace it back to the cells it connects.
Enter the Allen Institute in Seattle. Funded to the tune of $2 billion by
philanthropist and Microsoft mogul Paul Allen, it is dedicated to providing
neuroscientists worldwide with a complete description of the human brain,
including its “connectome”—a detailed map of the different types of
neurons and their wiring diagram. To reach this goal, its engineers have
designed  fully  automated  electron  microscopes  capable  of  acquiring
nanometric images of an entire block of cortex.
The small volume of cortex that you see here contains exactly 364
excitatory neurons. Using powerful algorithms, scientists in the MICrONS
consortium (Machine Intelligence from Cortical Networks) have managed
to reconstruct all of their dendrites and axons, and virtually all of their
synapses.
 In the future, MICrONS scientists hope to extend this picture to the
whole brain, first in mice, then in humans. Furthermore, they will also
measure the activity of the same neurons in response to visual, auditory, or
tactile stimuli. We will then be able to determine whether brain structure
predicts function: do we understand enough about the anatomy of single


<!-- Page 49 -->

neurons to predict their responses from their connectivity? The jury is still
out on this ambitious project.

RECEPTORS AND NEUROTRANSMITTERS:
 THE BUILDING BLOCKS OF THE SYNAPSE
 Three-dimensional reconstruction of the molecular structure of the acetylcholine receptor.
The electron microscopy photograph shows how several of these molecules line the cell
membrane, in torpedo fish as well as in our synapses.
 The ultimate scale of analysis of the nervous system is that of the molecule.
Neurons do not communicate via electrical messages. Rather, they rely on
chemical messengers called “neurotransmitters,” such as acetylcholine,
serotonin, dopamine, or glutamate.
These molecules are stored in vesicles located at each synapse, in the
upstream neuron, within the pre-synaptic terminal of the axon. When an
electrical message reaches the vesicles, they fuse with the cell membrane


<!-- Page 51 -->

and let their contents spill out into the space between the two neurons, the
synaptic cleft.
The neurotransmitters quickly diffuse in the space between the two cells,
but  how  do  they  impact  the  second,  “post-synaptic”  neuron?  The
transmission mechanism is simple: the neurotransmitter acts as a key that
must attach to the corresponding lock, a receptor molecule.
The French researcher Jean-Pierre Changeux, at the Pasteur Institute in
Paris, was the first to isolate, in 1970, such a molecule: the acetylcholine
receptor. He used the fact that close variants of this receptor are found in the
brain, in muscles, and in the electric organ of the torpedo fish.
4
By binding
it to the toxin of a snake venom, Jean-Pierre Changeux succeeded in
extracting, purifying, and characterizing the acetylcholine receptor protein,
whose three-dimensional atomic structure is depicted here.
This three-dimensional structure exemplifies how the synapse works.
The receptor is made of five chains of atoms, five subunits that assemble to
form a pentagon. They enclose a central channel that crosses the membrane
of the post-synaptic neuron. In the presence of the neurotransmitter, the
channel opens up and lets calcium ions pass through, thus generating a
small electrical current. In this manner, the electrical message from the
presynaptic  neuron  is  transformed  first  into  a  chemical  signal—the
liberation of the neurotransmitter—and then back into an electrical message
at the post-synaptic level. Et voilà! This is how a neural message crosses the
synapse.

THE MOLECULAR SIGNATURE  OF
CORTICAL AREAS
 Sections of a human brain made by Karl Zilles and his group at the Jülich Research Center
in Germany. In the left slice, radioactive binding was used to determine the concentration in
muscarinic acetylcholine receptors M2. The right slice shows the boundaries of the various
visual areas, which are marked by changes in the thickness of the cortical layers.
 More than a dozen neurotransmitter molecules coexist in our brain. Most of
them open more than one lock: they can bind to several different receptors.
Furthermore, each receptor is different. Some allow only potassium ions to
pass, others chlorine, calcium, or sodium. And this choice of ions changes


<!-- Page 53 -->

everything:  the  effect  on  the  post-synaptic  cell  can  be  fast  or  slow,
excitatory or inhibitory, direct or indirect.
The complexity increases one step further if we consider that the very
same neuron can carry more than one type of receptor molecule, in very
different concentrations. Since these properties can also vary from one layer
of the cortex to another, the result is an enormous diversity of combinations,
and  thus  of  brain  communication  channels,  each  of  which  probably
implements a distinct function. The acetylcholine circuit, for example, is
involved in attention, while the dopamine circuit plays a major role in
reward and decision making.
Each region of the cortex is characterized by a distinct molecular
signature, depending on the number and type of receptors that occupy its
different layers.
5
This image illustrates how radical those variations can be.
Area V1, the first point of entry of visual information into the cortex, has a
high concentration of acetylcholine receptors of a particular type: the M2
muscarinic receptor. As soon as we move on to the next areas, V2 and V3,
this density decreases sharply, and it drops by a factor of 8 or 10 in
downstream areas.
What is the reason for these variations? This is not yet well understood,
but all primary sensory areas, whether visual, auditory, or tactile, share a
high density of M2 receptors—perhaps because those receptors allow
attentional signals, carried by acetylcholine, to selectively turn sensory
inputs on or off.
6
In this way, we avoid being distracted by an ambient
conversation when focusing on a crossword puzzle. In the final analysis,
cognition is a molecular affair.

THE TWINS  GAME
 Three-dimensional reconstruction of the left hemispheres of eight pairs of identical twins,
matched by color. Data acquired by Philippe Pinel on the 3-tesla magnet at the NeuroSpin
brain imaging center, Saclay, France.
 Take a close look at the sixteen brains shown here. Eight pairs of identical
twins were scanned in our high-field MRI scanner at NeuroSpin. We
genotyped them, so we are sure that each of them was the result of the
division of a single egg, and therefore share exactly the same DNA
sequence. And yet, can you find the twin pairs?
You may have to rely on the colors, because it is essentially impossible
to identify the twins on the basis of brain anatomy alone. Why? Because the
genetic heritability of brain organization is modest. To be sure, there is a
greater resemblance between identical twins than between two random


<!-- Page 55 -->

people.
7
However, the vagaries of development dominate over the rigidity
of a shared genome. The cortex, much like our fingerprints, never folds
twice  in  the  same  way,  even  in  perfect  twins. And  this  anatomical
variability is consistent with the fact that identical twins are not identical
persons—it is even possible for only one of them to suffer from autism or
schizophrenia.
Genetic inheritance thus fails to explain the fine-grained organization of
the human brain. Random deviations occur early during development, in
utero, through chance events of cell multiplication as well as fluctuations in
the chemical environment encountered by the dividing cells—and they
shape our cortical folds for the rest of our lives.
To gain an intuition of how your cortex is folded, imagine fitting inside a
human skull two large handkerchiefs that roughly match the cortical surface
of the two hemispheres. It would be impossible to do so without crumpling
them: the cortex must fold. Exactly where it does is partly due to chance,
and partly due to preexisting fracture lines that are genetically determined.
In  all  humans,  the  long-  and  medium-range  cortical  connections  that
characterize the  Homo sapiens  brain pull on the tissue and force it to fold in
systematic ways.
The crinkled surface of the cortex thus reflects the random events of
pregnancy as well as the systematic organization of the underlying bundles
of connections.
8
In the end, the precise pattern of our cortical folds betrays,
indirectly, the incidents and accidents of our lives, such as prematurity or
malnutrition.

These images of ultra-high spatial (200 micrometers) and angular resolution were obtained
thanks to 255 days of acquisition on the small 11.7-tesla magnet of NeuroSpin. Ongoing

 What defines a cortical area is, above all, the inputs it receives and the
outputs it sends. The primary visual area V1, for instance, owes its name to
the fact that it is the main target of neural projections from the lateral
geniculate body of the thalamus, which itself receives direct inputs from the
retina. Remove these visual inputs and, in the congenitally blind person,
area V1 changes its function completely: it gets recycled into a language


<!-- Page 57 -->

area.
9
And if you redirect those visual inputs to the auditory cortex, it is the
latter region that gets repurposed: it begins to respond to images rather than
sounds.
10
 To understand how the brain works, it is therefore crucial to map its
connections; yet this is a difficult problem. Underneath the cortex, the so-
called white matter is a vast jumble of crisscrossing axons. In humans, these
axons can be several centimeters long, thus making them extremely difficult
to track across the successive slices of a classical dissection.
Since the 1990s, however, a revolutionary technique called diffusion
MRI allows us to trace brain connections from the outside, using the
noninvasive  method  of  magnetic  resonance  imaging.
11
Diffusion  MRI
evaluates, at any point in the brain, the displacement of water molecules in
any given direction during a brief lapse of time. The water that is trapped
inside a neuron gets blocked by the cellular membrane and therefore tends
to move along the main direction of the axon or dendrites. Its motion thus
betrays, indirectly, the direction of those cables—by measuring one, we can
estimate the other.
In this image, the color indicates the local direction of nerve fibers:
green for those that run from front to back, red from left to right, and blue
for  vertical  fibers. A computer  algorithm  then  puts  together  all  this
information and deduces the most likely path of the axons—a method called
diffusion-based tractography.
12
 Unfortunately, our brains contain billions of crisscrossing cables—as
many as there are pyramidal neurons in the cortex. Each of them sends its
axon in one or more directions, sometimes as far as a dozen centimeters
away. Despite considerable advances in brain imaging, determining their
precise paths remains a distant challenge.

THE HIGHWAYS  OF THE BRAIN
 Main bundles of connections of the human brain, reconstructed using diffusion-based
tractography by Michel Thiebaut de Schotten, currently at the University of Bordeaux.
 Roughly speaking, all human brains share the same blueprint. In each of us,
diffusion MRI reveals the presence of the same long- and medium-range
fiber bundles that connect our cortical areas to each other and to the outside
world.
13
 The optical radiations, for instance, are large cables that carry inputs
from the retina to the visual cortex at the back of the head, in the occipital
region. They are shown in red at the bottom left of the image. Note that they
do not connect with the eyes—rather, neurons in the retina first contact a
relay located in the middle of the brain, in the thalamus, and it is those
thalamic neurons, in turn, that project onto the cortex.


<!-- Page 59 -->

On the output side, if we are able to act on the external world, it is
because a bundle of connections projects from the motor cortex to the
spinal cord and, from there, allows us to control our muscles. This is the
pyramidal tract, also called the cortico-spinal tract, which appears on the
left of the middle row.
However, the cortex mostly talks to itself. Its computations are mainly
done internally, by passing information from one region to another. For
instance, two large bundles share visual information from occipital areas to
the rest of the brain (second image in the bottom row): the inferior
longitudinal fasciculus projects to the temporal cortex, and the inferior
fronto-occipital fasciculus to the prefrontal cortex. It is probably through
the latter path that we gain awareness of visual images: information must
reach a superior level of the cortical hierarchy, within the prefrontal cortex,
in order to be consciously perceived.
14
 Dozens of other bundles, of variable length, gather information from the
different parietal, temporal, and frontal areas. Among these, the arcuate
fasciculus (far right in the middle row) deserves special mention: it is
probably thanks to this massive cable that we are able to express our
thoughts in words. This tract is quite voluminous, particularly in the left
hemisphere, and its connections, which are more branched in our species
than in any other primate, gather information from a great variety of
temporal and parietal areas, and send it to Broca's area, which puts it into
words and sentences.
These  large  cortical  highways  are  characteristic  markers  of  our
humanity. They are present since birth in every one of us, and their
distribution, extent, and lateralization make us who we are.

CONNECTIONS

 Brain connections are like fingerprints: they look similar, but no two are
exactly the same. The analogy is very real: anyone can be identified among
more than a hundred other persons, from their brain connections alone.
15
 This  image  shows  the  “connectome,”  the  cortical  connections  of
nineteen different people. The overall plan is the same, but the details vary.


<!-- Page 61 -->

One bundle is longer, another is thinner. The variations are quantitative: we
all share the same bundles, but their dimensions are not identical. During
development, axons surround themselves with an insulating sheath of
myelin, whose thickness can be estimated by MRI—and the number of
layers of this insulation varies from one person to the next.
Do these variations have any cognitive impact? Yes, and this is where the
analogy with fingerprints ends. Our fingerprints are purely arbitrary, but our
brain connections are not: they shape our minds. Over the past fifteen years,
researchers have identified dozens of subtle links between brain anatomy
and  personality.  From  brain  connectivity  alone,  advanced  algorithms
manage to guess, better than by chance, our age, IQ, pain threshold, or
sensitivity  to  drug  addiction.  The  connectivity  of  the  hippocampus
influences our memory capacity. The fiber bundles that connect prefrontal
cortex modulate the threshold beyond which we become aware of external
information, and they are altered in pathologies such as schizophrenia.
16
 Prematurity may also induce alterations in cortical connectivity, the
details of which correlate with the presence of possible cognitive deficits.
And, more positively, diffusion MRI allows us to guess, among a group of
dyslexic children, which ones will manage to compensate for their handicap
by using an alternative bundle of connections passing through the right
hemisphere.
17
 However, brain connectivity is not fixed at birth. It is not a definitive
sentence that puts a sharp limit to our talents. On the contrary, connections
and their myelin sheaths change as a function of our training. When we
learn  to  read,  for  instance,  we  massively  strengthen  certain  cortical
connections, so much so that about 50 percent of the variance in reading
scores can be predicted by looking at the anatomy of the brain.
18
 Our cortical connections are like the hands of pianists or violinists:
musicians must learn to play with their hands’ initial shape, but they also
discover that, far from being inflexible, their fingers subtly adjust to the
instrument on which they practice.

ONE MAGNET  TO RULE THEM ALL
 Delivery of the Iseult magnet at NeuroSpin in 2017. © P. Dumas CEA NeuroSpin.
 Visualizing the anatomy and the connections of the brain requires powerful
MRI magnets. NeuroSpin's latest MRI scanner, called Iseult, is the magnet
of all superlatives. With its unprecedented magnetic field of 11.7 tesla, its 5-
meter length and diameter, this magnet weighs 132 tons. It is also a gigantic
vessel  filled  with  thousands  of  liters  of  liquid  helium  that  must  be
maintained at an exceptionally low temperature of 1.8 Kelvin, very close to
absolute zero. Why? Because it is only under these conditions that its wires
remain superconductors in spite of the enormous magnetic field that bathes
them. To give you an idea, 11.7 tesla is 250,000 times the magnitude of the


<!-- Page 63 -->

Earth's magnetic field, and four times larger than most commercial MRI
scanners.
Why is the machine so big? Because the human body is also rather large
—indeed, it would be rather difficult to scan the head without letting in the
shoulders and the rest of the body that goes with it. Add to this a receiver
coil and a large generator of magnetic field gradients—both of which are
essential  to  generate  readable  images—and  you  arrive  at  an  internal
diameter of 90 centimeters.
Maintaining a high magnetic field homogenous over such a vast space is
a genuine challenge: the field must remain fixed to within 0.00005 percent.
This is why the magnet is so long: it consists of a gigantic stack of niobium-
titanium pancakes, over four meters long. A colossal current of 1,483
amperes flows through them: the magnet stores 338 megajoules of energy,
the equivalent of 80 kilograms of TNT.
Why deploy such technology? An MRI machine's magnetic field serves
to align the atoms of the body, whose spins then enter in resonance with the
magnet. The relevant atoms are those that possess a magnetic moment:
hydrogen, omnipresent as water (H2O), but also sodium, phosphorus, and
lithium, which are scarcer but still detectable. A larger magnetic field means
a stronger MRI signal—which will be used, for example, by NeuroSpin
researchers to refine the anatomy of the brain, trace its connections, or
detect neuronal activity and its pathologies.
Although a 10.5-tesla magnet is already in operation at the University of
Minnesota, Iseult is currently the only whole-body human MRI scanner to
reach 11.7 tesla. Only one other MRI scanner, located at the National
Institutes of Health in Bethesda, Maryland, reaches the same field, but only
at the head level. Will a Tristan ever surpass the power of Iseult? In China,
South Korea, and Germany, 14-tesla human MRI scanners are still in the
planning stage.

ZOOMING IN ON THE  HIPPOCAMPUS
 Images of the human hippocampus acquired by Cyril Poupon and the BAOBAB/Gingko
team on the 3-, 7-, and 11.7-tesla magnets of NeuroSpin, within the framework of the
European Human Brain Project. The images on the left show the different regions of the
hippocampus and their internal connections, as extracted from diffusion images acquired at
11.7 tesla with an isotropic resolution of 300 microns.
 Why do we keep increasing the magnetic field of our MRI scanners, even
though it requires a massive investment in technology? Because this is
currently the only way to improve the spatial resolution of our images of the
living human brain.
As the strength of the magnet increases, a larger fraction of atoms align
their spins in the direction of the magnetic field, and their synchronized
dance enhances the magnetic resonance signal. The number of magnetized


<!-- Page 65 -->

atoms determines the strength of the MRI signal. It is crucial to amplify this
signal  until  it  dominates  over  the  noise  resulting  from  the  random
movements of the atoms under the effect of body temperature.
To avoid diluting this minuscule signal in excessive noise, we must
average it over time and space. In doing so, however, we lose resolution:
the spatial and temporal precision of the images decreases.
The fundamental unit of brain imaging is the  voxel . We are all familiar
with  pixels  (picture elements): our digital cameras contain arrays of pixels,
elementary  components  of  the  light  sensor  whose  number  and  size
determine the sharpness of our pictures. In three dimensions, a pixel
becomes a voxel—the smallest element of volume that defines the spatial
resolution of our three-dimensional images.
No MRI image is ever continuous, as a good old photograph on silver
film.  All  of  our  brain  images  are  digitized,  pixelated—or  rather
“voxelated”—and their resolution is rather mediocre. With a classical MRI
scanner at 3 tesla, the cubes with which we discretize the brain are on the
order of about 1–2 millimeters on a side. This may seem small enough, but
a  cubic  millimeter  of  cortex  still  contains  about  50,000  neurons.  By
increasing the strength of the MRI scanner, we manage to get down to a few
hundred microns, which is still far from perfect: there are about 200
neurons in a 150-micron voxel of human cortex.
The images you see in the figure illustrate the importance of increasing
spatial resolution. They showcase the hippocampus, a deep subcortical
structure essential to memory, whose multiple layers of neurons compress
information from the cortex in order to store it in compact form. Achieving
better images of the hippocampus is one of the major challenges of brain
imaging, because the alteration of its circuits is one of the first signs of
Alzheimer's disease.

 What is thought? The discoveries of cognitive neuroscience suggest that

CAPTURING  THE BRAIN IN ACTION
 Images obtained in 1988 by Steve Petersen, Peter Fox, Michael Posner, Mark Mintum, and
Marcus Raichle with the positron emission tomography (PET) scanner of Saint Louis
University, Missouri.
 In 1988, the journal  Nature  published the first image of the cognitive brain
in action. From the outside, without slicing the cranium open, scientists had
managed to get a first glimpse of the regions activated by a variety of
cognitive  tasks:  reading,  listening,  speaking,  and  thinking  about  the
meaning of words.
1
 All  the  images  in  this  book  so  far  only  traced  the  anatomical
organization of the brain. It is beautiful to visualize brain anatomy in all its
details using classical MRI, but the brain might as well be dead! What
Marcus Raichle, Michael Posner, and their collaborators invented was a


<!-- Page 69 -->

way to visualize which of these brain regions were actually doing the work
when the person performed a specific mental operation.
Their method was awkward. First, they had to synthesize, in a cyclotron,
a tiny dose of water, H2O, in which the usual atom of oxygen 16O was
replaced by an isotope, 15O, which is radioactive. They injected the syringe
of radioactive water in a volunteer whose head had been placed in a
positron emission tomography scanner, a device capable of detecting the
radioactivity and determining where it came from. Everything had to go
very smoothly, because the radioactivity decreases by half every two
minutes, and thus is almost completely gone after a quarter of an hour.
When we carried out such experiments at the Service Hospitalier Frédéric
Joliot in Orsay, we had to run through the corridors with a lead cart that
sheltered the precious syringe!
Within  seconds  following  the  injection,  the  radioactive  water
accumulates in the regions where cerebral blood flow is the highest. By a
remarkable regulatory mechanism, these also are the most active brain
regions. This is because, as soon as neurons fire, the nearest capillaries
dilate  in  order  to  supply  this  area  with  oxygen  and  glucose,  thus
compensating  for  the  energy  consumed.  Thus,  the  water  collects  in
activated areas, where it loses its radioactivity by emitting positrons. Those
antimatter particles annihilate when they meet a nearby electron, thus
emitting gamma rays that can be detected from outside the skull.
One more thing was needed: to activate the participant's brain in a
reproducible manner. Michael Posner devised a hierarchical experiment in
which subjects saw or heard words, and had to remain passive, say the
words aloud, or generate an appropriate verb (what verb would you select if
the cue word was, say,  violin ?).
For each additional processing stage, one more brain region became
active. Each operation could thus be assigned to a specific area: left ventral
occipitotemporal cortex for reading, left temporal lobe for listening to
words, motor and premotor cortex for speaking, and left inferior prefrontal
cortex, including Broca's area, for thinking about the word's meaning in
order to select an appropriate verb. This was the very first dissection of
language networks in the living brain.

THE COUNTLESS DIMENSIONS  OF
MEANING

University of California, Berkeley, freely available at https://gallantlab.org/brain-viewer/.
 Nowadays, we no longer have to inject radioactivity in order to see the
brain in action. A variant of magnetic resonance imaging, functional MRI
(fMRI), locates the active spots with unprecedented precision and without
the need for any injection. This brain imaging method relies on a molecule
that already circumnavigates the entire body: hemoglobin.
Hemoglobin is present in each of our red blood cells: it carries oxygen
from the lungs to the tissues that burn it. Fortunately for us scientists, it
does not have the same magnetic properties when it carries an oxygen
molecule  as  when  it  does  not  :  deoxygenated  hemoglobin  acts  as  a


<!-- Page 71 -->

minuscule magnet, and magnetic resonance images can be sensitized to its
concentration in the blood. In this manner, we can evaluate, indirectly,
which regions of the brain are activated. Whenever enough neurons are
triggered, fresh blood quickly flows in. It carries with it a lot of hemoglobin
that was just brought in from the lungs and which thus carries an oxygen
molecule. Its presence increases the MRI signal—and we can manage to
detect this minuscule blip anywhere in the brain.
What is most extraordinary is the speed of fMRI. With positron emission
tomography, we could acquire only about five images per hour. In contrast,
fMRI can take snapshots of a brain slice several times per second. Various
acceleration techniques allow us to measure the activity of the whole brain
every one or two seconds, at a spatial resolution of a few millimeters or
less.
Functional MRI therefore allows for much richer experiments than
positron emission tomography. With it, we can follow the activity of the
brain, second after second, for several hours. The image shows a volunteer
who listened to stories from  The Moth Radio Hour  for two hours.
2

two seconds, the MRI scanner recorded the activity evoked by recent
words, which were classified according to 985 dimensions of meaning,

THE BRAIN AS A  MAPMAKER
 Top: Retinotopy of area V1 visualized in 1982 by Roger Tootell and colleagues at the
University of California, Berkeley. Bottom: Orientation mapping visualized by optical
imaging. The first such maps were published in 1991 by Tobias Bonhoeffer (then at
Rockefeller University) and Amiram Grinwald (Weizmann Institute, Israel), and this one is
due to William Bosking, David Fitzpatrick, and their collaborators at Durham University in
North Carolina.
 “Cortex” is Latin for “bark”—our gray matter is literally the bark of the
brain, a thin layer of cells, a few millimeters thick, that covers both
hemispheres. Neurons use this two-dimensional plane to form maps: the
brain spreads out all of its knowledge on the surface of the cortex.


<!-- Page 75 -->

This mapping is nowhere as evident as in the primary visual area V1, in
the occipital lobe, which is the main point of entry for visual information in
the cortex. There, on a microscopic scale, each point of the retina projects to
a particular cortical location. Thus, the topography of the retina is laid over
the brain's bark—but the map is cut in half: the right part of the visual field
lands in the left hemisphere, and vice versa. This systematic projection is
called “retinotopy.”
A remarkable  experiment  allows  us  to  visualize,  at  a  glance,  the
retinotopic map of area V1.
5
First, train an animal not to move its eyes, and
then expose it to a circular checkerboard pattern of flashing lights. This
display strongly activates V1 neurons, and they consume glucose, which
can be labeled with a radioactive atom of carbon-14. As a result, a black
and white map of the retina gets printed onto the cortex. As you can see in
the image on the right, each circle and each bar of the original image gets
transferred onto the brain—but the map is distorted: the central region,
called the fovea, takes up more cortical space than the periphery. This is
why we keep moving our eyes when reading, for instance: by doing so, we
bring the center of our gaze, which has a much higher visual acuity, to the
part of the image that we want to analyze more finely.
Within the primary visual area V1, a second map is embedded into the
first. Each V1 neuron responds not only to a specific position but also to a
particular orientation of visual information on the retina. Thus, different
neurons fire to a vertical, oblique, or horizontal bar. When we encode this
preference by different colors, a magnificent tapestry emerges, as the cortex
spreads out on its surface, as best it can, the circular space of line
orientations.
6
 This is actually a general principle: adjacent cortical sites tend to code
for similar information. In V1, a mini-map of orientation fits into a macro-
map of location.

VISUAL ILLUSIONS:  THE FAKE NEWS OF
THE BRAIN
 Image designed by the Japanese specialist of visual illusions Akiyoshi Kitaoka, inspired by
the painting  Enigma  by the artist Isia Levant.
 Look at this spiral, and experience the vertigo of visual perception. This
image is static, and yet its black and yellow stripes seem to vibrate, while
dark ripples run through its blue arms and converge toward the center.
Where does this illusory motion come from?
To understand this effect, we must take a second look at the neural
mechanisms of visual perception. The maps that our brain builds do not
stop at the primary visual area. Downstream of area V1, a region of the


<!-- Page 77 -->

brain called MT, for “middle temporal visual area,” specializes in motion
perception. As soon as something moves, even if it is barely detectable, the
neurons in this area discharge and indicate where the movement occurred. It
is the firing of MT neurons that allows you to detect, out of the corner of
your  eye,  the  presence  of  a  mosquito.  Each  MT  neuron  acts  as  a
spatiotemporal filter that detects how light moved across the retina within a
short period of time, and reports where and in which direction.
But why then do we perceive movement in this static spiral? Because the
vibrant pattern erroneously excites area MT. Place a person in a brain
imaging machine, and show them either this picture or other control images
that do not evoke any subjective feeling of movement. Only this picture
activates area MT.
8
Moreover, if you disrupt this area, the illusion stops.
9
 Thus, there is a direct and causal relationship between the motion illusion
and the activity of motion area MT.
Why does this region get activated? Because the illusion deceives our
MT  neurons.  Its  odd  line  pattern  is  designed  to  amplify  the
micromovements of our eyes. Even when we believe that we gaze fixedly at
something, our eyes still vibrate at random, and these small movements
shift the image on our retina. Most of the time, these tremors are too weak
to excite our motion neurons. However, this picture excites them with
extraordinary efficiency. In fact, research proves that the more illusory
motion we perceive, the more our eyes actually move.
10
 Researchers have discovered hundreds of visual illusions. Far from
revealing flaws in our brain, they exemplify the efficiency of our cortical
calculations. Our brain constantly strives to reconstruct the most plausible
interpretation of the outside world—even when, unbeknownst to it, it is
confronted with “fake news,” a work of art especially designed to deceive
it.

THE TIMING  OF MENTAL OPERATIONS
 Photograph of a volunteer equipped with a geodesic net of 256 EEG electrodes and sitting
in the NeuroSpin magnetoencephalography (MEG) machine.
 Sitting in a magnetoencephalography (MEG) machine, the head covered
with  electroencephalography  (EEG)  sensors,  a  volunteer  is  ready  to
participate in an experiment at the NeuroSpin center. In a few seconds, the
volunteer's head will be inserted deeper into the MEG machine, and we will
be able to visualize, to the nearest millisecond, the speed with which
information is processed in their brain.
What's the purpose of this new imaging machine? Couldn't MRI do same
job? No, because MRI has a major flaw: it is very slow relative to the
timing of mental operations. Recall that functional MRI does not measure


<!-- Page 79 -->

neuronal activity directly, but only through the oxygenation of the blood
that irrigates neural tissue. However, blood vessels do not respond at once;
it takes them a few seconds to dilate. As a consequence, MRI is rather
insensitive to the time course of most mental operations. It can resolve time
delays only on the order of a few tenths of a second,
11
much slower than the
time it takes for our brain to recognize a word, spot a face, or perform a
brief calculation.
Fortunately, EEG and MEG fill this gap, because these methods are
directly sensitive to the activity of the underlying neurons. Whenever a
neuron discharges, it is because its dendritic tree has collected, from its
incoming synapses, enough electric current to exceed its firing threshold.
Each dendritic current is minuscule, but if enough neurons activate in sync,
their sum becomes detectable at the surface of the head. The activation of
neuronal groups induces a small electrical potential, on the order of a few
millionths of a volt, which can be amplified and recorded by electrodes
placed on the scalp. A record of this voltage, which constantly changes, is
called the  electroencephalogram , or EEG.
Every  electrical  current  also  generates  a  magnetic  field. Thus,  the
activated  neurons  behave  like  small  magnets  and  emit  time-varying
magnetic waves. Those are really tiny, a billion times smaller than the
Earth's magnetic field. However, the magnetoencephalography machine is
designed to measure them. Its white helmet houses 306 sensors of ultralow
magnetic  fields,  cooled  in  liquid  helium  to  −269°C. The  machine  is
protected from the electromagnetic noise of the environment by a shielded
chamber made of mu-metal, an alloy of nickel and iron that stops external
magnetic fields. It is under these conditions that we manage reconstruct the
activity of the living human brain, millisecond by millisecond, without ever
having to open the cranium or inject any drug.

THROUGH THE CORTEX

the NeuroSpin MEG center.
 An image flashes on your retina—but how long does it take your brain to
recognize it? To find out, a simple experiment consists in recording,
millisecond  by  millisecond,  the  electro-  and  magnetoencephalography
signals evoked by the image as it progresses through the hierarchy of
cortical areas. We can then use decoding algorithms to determine when
these signals contain explicit information about the image content.
In this experiment, a person was presented with four categories of
images: houses, tools, faces, and human bodies.
12
Why those choices?


<!-- Page 81 -->

Because we know from functional MRI that these four categories are
processed by distinct regions of the visual cortex, so they should generate
spatially distinct brain responses. We then use an artificial intelligence
algorithm to search through all the MEG and EEG sensors for signals that
distinguish which image type was presented.
13

after the image arrives on the retina, we begin to detect, in the cortex,
signals that allow us to decode its identity. This visual decoding continues
for almost half a second. This is because each image induces a flow of
activity, a traveling wave that propagates through the cortex, from primary
area V1 to the depths of temporal cortex and, for images that trigger a
conscious experience, the prefrontal cortex. Each of these regions contains
a distinct neuronal code that we can decipher.
In the brain, processing time is relative: it depends on the region of the
cortex from which we record. An image takes less than 80 milliseconds to
reach primary visual cortex, but about 200 milliseconds to contact anterior
temporal  cortex,  and  up  to  300  milliseconds  for  prefrontal  cortex.
Processing may even last longer if the image is blurred or ambiguous, in
which case these regions exchange bidirectional signals until the ambiguity
is resolved.
What if we presented several images in close succession? That's what
my colleagues and I did in the experiment illustrated here.
14
We bombarded
the person with pictures at a frantic rate of nine per second. The results
showed that the cortex manages to keep up. At any moment, we can decode
which image was just seen, but again, the answer depends on where we
look: one area says, “I saw a face,” while another, slower region is still
processing the previous image and answers, “It's a house.” How the brain
manages to make sense of such crisscrossing signals and avoids mixing

RECORDING FROM  THOUSANDS  OF
 ELECTRODES
 NeuroPixels chip with its sensor tip. The Allen Institute for Brain Sciences in Seattle places

 To decode brain representations, we need to track the activity of large
populations of neurons over time. This cannot be done from the outside—
we have to penetrate the brain with long electrodes and bring them close to
the firing neurons. As soon as the electrode is close enough to the cell body
of a neuron, it can record each of its discharges, the “action potentials” that


<!-- Page 83 -->

it sends to other neurons. This method, which is more than fifty years old, is
called  single-cell electrophysiology .
For a long time, electrophysiology was a pure craft. In their laboratories,
researchers would build their own electrodes by stretching thin tungsten
wires. Then they would insert them into the brain, one at a time, through a
small hole in the skull. Finally, they would finely tune their depth with a
rotating  wheel.  Each  electrode  was  connected  to  an  amplifier  and  a
loudspeaker. The presence of a neuron was noticed by the clicks of its
action potentials, as in a Geiger counter. The electrophysiologist would then
freeze  the  setup  and  try  to  keep  this  neuron  for  the  duration  of  an
experiment, typically an hour or two. Recording three or four neurons per
day was quite a feat!
Today, the invention of parallel recording devices has revolutionized this
field of research. An international consortium including the Janelia Campus
of the Howard Hughes Medical Institute, the Allen Institute for Brain
Sciences in Seattle, and University College London set out to design the
electrodes of the future. The result was a miniaturized electronic device
capable of recording several hundred or even thousand neurons at once: the
NeuroPixels chip.
15
 Each NeuroPixels chip is a small printed circuit board that ends in a thin
tip, the electrode itself, which comprises 960 recording sites: those are the
little squares you can see on the left of the image. At any given time, 384 of

huge saving in space, noise level, and power consumption. Several chips

parallel. The electrodes can be left in place for days, even weeks or months,

THE GPS  OF THE BRAIN
 Reconstruction of a grid cell and its response as the rat moves through a square room.
Each blue dot represents an action potential. Recordings from May-Britt and Edvard
Moser's laboratory at the Kavli Institute for Systems Neuroscience in Trondheim, Norway.
 The crystal hexagon shown here encases a special type of cortical neuron: a
grid cell, part of a circuit that has been nicknamed “the brain's GPS.”
The neuron adorns the premises of the Kavli Institute for Systems
Neuroscience, where this type of cell was first discovered in the early

the Nobel Prize in 2014, only nine years after their first publication on this
topic.
16


<!-- Page 85 -->

It had been known for years that the brain creates internal maps of its
environment. This is probably true of all animal species. Even desert ants
never get lost when foraging, and always manage to return straight to their
nest once they have discovered food. Much like sailors keep a logbook of
their course through the oceans, their tiny brains seem to record, at any
moment, their best estimate of the place they have reached on the map. But
how do ants, mice, and rats do it? What neural circuit serves as their GPS?
The Mosers discovered how the neurons in a region of the rat brain
called the “medial entorhinal cortex” create a map of space. Whenever the
animal moves, these neurons systematically change their discharges. Each
neuron seems to apply a triangular grid to the room, and it fires only when
the animal visits one of the vertices of this grid—hence the name “grid
cells.”
The neuron you see in the figure was recorded for several minutes while
the rat searched its square litter box for hidden bits of chocolate. The thin
white line traces the animal's random wanderings, while the blue dots show
the moments when the neuron emitted an action potential. As you can see,
this neuron fires only when the animal's head is in certain places. These
preferred locations, which are called the neuron's “receptive field,” lie at the
vertices of a grid of equilateral triangles and regular hexagons.
Each neuron in the medial entorhinal cortex maps space with a slightly
different triangle, which varies in size and in position. Thus, a few thousand
grid cells suffice to cover the entire plane, without leaving any gaps.
Collectively, they form a map, but a rather peculiar one: instead of the
familiar  longitude  and  latitude  lines,  which  form  rectangles,  the  rat's
planisphere uses triangular coordinates.
The rat's GPS works day and night, even in the dark. It does so by
relying on information received from other neurons that encode body and
head movements, and which can ultimately be traced back to the labyrinths
of the inner ear. Grid cells also interconnect with the hippocampus, where
we find a whole zoo of place-related neurons that recognize locations such
as “the center of the room,” “the north wall” or “15° west of the cage door.”
The same GPS circuitry exists in the human brain, where it plays a crucial
role in our sense of direction.

PLAY AND REPLAY  WITH PLACE CELLS
 Place cells in the rat hippocampus recorded by Kamran Diba in the laboratory of György
Buzsáki at Rutgers University in New Jersey.
 Let a rat run in a corridor and, at the same time, using fine electrodes,
record a few dozen neurons in its hippocampus. You will see that the rat's
path translates into a neuronal trajectory. Many neurons in the hippocampus
behave  as  “place  cells”:  they  each  prefer  a  particular  place  in  the
environment, and discharge only if the rat is there. The sequence of
activated neurons thus constitutes a neural trace of the animal's path.


<!-- Page 87 -->

To create this image, the discharges of thirteen hippocampal neurons
were colored and ordered according to the location of their favorite place.
18
 Some neurons prefer the beginning of the corridor, others the middle or the
end. Their action potentials, symbolized by vertical bars, are spread out in
time. It took the animal a few seconds to cross the corridor, and at each
moment, its hippocampus faithfully encoded where it was. Within four
seconds, the neural discharges moved from the neurons colored in blue to
those in red. The journey was accompanied by a powerful burst of brain
waves in the theta range (a frequency of about 6–10 hertz); this oscillation
in the local electrical field appears at the very top of the image.
Now, look carefully at what happens at the beginning and at the end of
the trip. The rat no longer moves—it is not even inside the corridor—and
yet the neurons still activate as if it were there, but they do so at a much
higher speed: the burst of neuronal discharges lasts less than a tenth of a
second. What is happening? The hippocampus is replaying, purely from
memory, a sequence of activity that corresponds to a plausible journey in
real space. This is what researchers call hippocampal  replay —and it is one
of the most plausible mechanisms for episodic memory, which allows us to
remember the events of our lives.
Take an even closer look. Before the rat sets off, its neurons fire a series
of discharges in the correct order, from left to right. This is a  pre-play
 episode: the animal anticipates what it is going to do, and its discharges
predict where it will go.
19
After its journey is over, however, the very same
neurons discharge, but in reverse order. This is a moment of  replay ,
probably associated with retrospective memory, where the animal revisits
what it just did.
Pre-play and replay also occur during sleep. Throughout the night, under
the guidance of the hippocampus, our brain plays and replays, hundreds of
times, the most important events of the previous days, thus allowing us to
learn from them.

THE FLY’S  NEURONAL COMPASS
 Microscopic view of the neuronal compass of the  Drosophila  fly. Each neuron has been
made to fluoresce in a different color. Image created in Tanya Wolff's laboratory at the
Janelia Research Campus of the Howard Hughes Medical Institute, Loudon County,
Virginia.
 This funny little guy, entirely made up of neurons, lies in the middle of the
“central complex,” a tiny cluster of neurons that forms part of the fruit fly's


<!-- Page 89 -->

brain. The doughnut-shaped ring is called the ellipsoid body. Its circular
structure corresponds closely to its function: it is a compass. Thanks to this
clever neuronal circuit, the fly always knows where it is heading.
To produce this image, Tanya Wolff and her team achieved an amazing
genetic feat.
20
Using a technique similar to the Brainbow I mentioned
earlier, they colored each neuron with a different fluorescent hue. Their
different shades revealed that the torus is subdivided into 16 sectors, each
allocated to a different neuron—a neuronal compass rose.
Using another technique, the researchers managed to measure the real-
time calcium concentration within each of those sixteen neurons and, thus,
to visualize their activity. The fly was motionless, but it was awake and
kicking, and surrounded by fly-size screens arranged as a virtual reality
theater. Each time the fly tried to twirl with its wings, a force sensor
detected the effort and updated the images accordingly, thus convincing the
animal that it was indeed flying.
The result? Whenever the fly faced a given direction, a bump of calcium,
and thus of neuronal activity, emerged in a fixed sector of the torus. Each
colored neuron thus coded for a different direction of motion. As the fly
turned, the activity moved in sync, just like the needle of a compass, in
order to keep informing the fly about its new direction. The mechanism is
elegant: the neurons around the ring sense the sliding of the visual images
over the eyes, and their connections allow the compass to be updated.
Mammals, too, possess head-direction cells that encode the animal's
current bearing. These neurons feed their information to the place cells and
grid cells that map space. Like satellites, airplanes, or drones, all animals
must  possess  a  neural  navigation  platform  whose  built-in  compass
constantly keeps track of their motion.

ACTING  WITH NEURAL VECTORS
 Neural vectors recorded in the motor cortex as a macaque monkey moves its hand toward a
visual goal. Insets show how a particular neuron (right) and the entire population (left)
change activity depending on the direction of movement. Research by Apostolos
Georgopoulos, then at the Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine, Baltimore,
Maryland.
 A monkey reaches for a peanut. How does its brain calculate in which
direction to send the hand? When we record the neurons in its motor cortex,


<!-- Page 91 -->

we see that, a few tens of milliseconds before the movement, some cells
start to fire, while others remain silent. It is the combination of active and
inactive neurons that codes for the direction of movement.
21
 To understand this, let's look at the activity of a given neuron, at the
bottom right of the image, as the animal moves its hand in different
directions from the same starting point. We can see that this neuron has a
marked preference for movements toward the left. It discharges intensely
before any movement to the left, whereas it stops firing before a movement
to the right. Thus, this neuron has a favorite  movement vector , directed to
the left.
Our motor cortex contains thousands of such neurons, whose favorite
vectors cover all directions in space. In order to predict the direction of the
upcoming movement, as the American researcher Apostolos Georgopoulos
discovered, all you have to do is average all these vectors, each weighted by
the discharge rate of the corresponding neuron. The average vector indicates
what the animal will do—it points in the same direction as the arm.
In other words, each neuron “votes” for its favorite direction, and the
average of all those votes wins. And if the monkey changes its mind and
turns its hand in another direction, we can literally see the vector spin: first
it points to the initial direction, then it rotates in sync with the animal's
intention to move.
22
 Vector coding is a fundamental principle that applies to many, if not all,
regions of the cortex. Each neuron makes its small contribution to the
overall  brain  code.  Each  spike  is  like  a  vote  for  a  certain  mental
representation. When we pool these ballots over a large population of cells,
we can decode the animal's current thought.

DECIDING  BY ACCUMULATING NEURAL
VOTES
 Neural discharges recorded in the parietal cortex of the macaque monkey during a simple
decision-making task: do the dots move more to the left or to the right? Work by Mike
Shadlen's group, then at the University of Washington in Seattle.
 Look at the macaque monkey shown here. It has been trained to answer a
simple question: do the dots on screen move toward the right or toward the
left? The choice is far from obvious, because only some of the dots move in
a consistent direction, while the others jitter at random.
The monkey responds by turning its eyes in the corresponding direction.
By varying the proportion of coherent movements, the difficulty of the
decision can be controlled. The more difficult it is, the longer the response


<!-- Page 93 -->

time—sometimes the monkey takes a few hundreds of milliseconds before
responding.
What happens in the brain during this long deliberation? In the prefrontal
and parietal cortex, neurons accumulate the sensory data that tip the scale
one way or the other. The neuron in blue adds up the votes in favor of the
right, while the neuron in red keeps track of the votes for the left. As in any
election, the polls fluctuate over time. Gradually, however, they begin to
take a definite direction, and when the total reaches a threshold, the
decision is made: it will be a move to the right.
It was Mike Shadlen's team, then at the University of Washington in
Seattle, that discovered this fundamental mechanism by which our brain
makes decisions.
23
Each available choice is associated with a distinct set of
neurons that gathers evidence in its favor. These neuronal pools compete
with  each  other,  and  the  decision  is  made  as  soon  as  one  of  them
accumulates enough data to exceed a predetermined threshold.
The human brain also uses the same kind of algorithm.
24
Indeed, this
algorithm possesses a remarkable feature: under certain conditions, it can be
shown to be mathematically optimal.
25
There is simply no better way to
make a decision as quickly as possible on the basis of noisy data. This is
why the same algorithm is used in the industry in order to decide, from a
few samples, whether a batch meets the criteria for quality control, and it is
the same algorithm that Alan Turing used during World War II to decipher
the German Enigma code.
In short, our brain acts like a statistician: to make the best decision, it
monitors its inputs, patiently accumulates statistics, and waits until it has
enough data to conclude with some degree of certainty.

MAKING AN ERROR  AND DETECTING IT
 Voltage map at the surface of the head 72 milliseconds after a person clicked the wrong
button in a simple number comparison task, where the person merely has to decide if a
number is larger or smaller than five. Measurements obtained by the author at the
University of Oregon, Eugene, using one of the very first 128-electrode nets.
 Oops! I just clicked on the wrong button, and my email went out by
mistake. How many times have we done something and, in the same instant,
realized our mistake and frantically tried to stop it?
This image, which dates back almost thirty years, was one of the first to
capture this “Oh sh*t” effect—the precise moment when our brain realizes
its mistake.
26
It is the anterior cingulate cortex, a region located in the
midline of the forehead, at the heart of the prefrontal cortex, that detects our
errors. Its neurons code for our goals and intentions, and they discharge as


<!-- Page 95 -->

soon as our actions deviate from their target. The anterior cingulate then
tries to stop the ongoing action—but all too often, such conscious control
arrives too late and can only witness the disaster.
The simultaneous discharge of many neurons, which all shout “Error!,”
is easy to detect on the surface of the scalp. An intense negative electrical
wave invades the middle of the forehead. It is as if Homer Simpson was
banging his head with his famous “D’oh!” Later, the error signal gets
transmitted to many other regions of the cortex, and a positive wave appears
on the top of the head. Only this second wave is associated with the
awareness of the error: at this point, the whole brain is informed that
something went wrong and tries to draw the consequences.
But how can the same brain make a mistake and simultaneously detect
it? It is important to understand that, at any given moment, our brain
contains many circuits that are activated in parallel and, at times, pull in
different directions.
27
Some are conscious, others are beyond our control,
but they all try to accumulate, as best they can, the noisy data that reach
them. Thus, the neural statistician in the brain's basal ganglia may wrongly
conclude that we should click a button on the right, while the prefrontal
cortex comes to the opposite conclusion a tenth of a millisecond later. By
comparing the two signals, the cingulate cortex realizes that a mistake has
been made.
In brief, it is the random fluctuations of neuronal discharges that explain
our occasional slips—and it is the multiplicity and redundancy of our brain
circuits that allows us to detect them.

FACES  IN THE HEAD
 Responses of a macaque monkey neuron specialized for faces and located in a patch of
cortex called “ML,” in the middle lateral area of the inferotemporal cortex. Recording made
in the laboratory of Margaret Livingstone at Harvard University.
 How do we recognize a face? In all primates—to which our species
obviously belongs—social signals are so essential to survival that the brain
dedicates specific circuits to them. In functional MRI, these circuits show
up as voxels that light up whenever we see a face. They respond to portraits,
cartoons, or even caricatured faces, but not to all sorts of other images of
plants, tools, vehicles, houses, numbers, or words.
If we lower an electrode into these patches of cortex and record from
their neurons, what do we see? Almost every one of them discharges a few


<!-- Page 97 -->

tens of milliseconds after a face appears within its “receptive field,” the
place on the retina to which it responds.
In  the  experiment  illustrated  here,  Margaret  Livingstone  and  her
colleagues at Harvard presented all sorts of pictures at various positions
relative to a monkey's center of gaze, and for each position, they evaluated
the neuron's firing rate.
28
The colors thus represent the probability that a
neuron emits an action potential as a function of the position of the image.
As you can see, the presence of a face, any face, in the neuron's receptive
field massively increases its firing probability: these neurons are genuine
face detectors, as efficient as your digital camera. Even more, they manage
to “see” invisible faces, for instance, hiding behind a rug: the presence of
the body and limbs is all it takes for the neural network to infer the presence
of a face. Even an abstract stone pile suffices to evoke the presence of a
body and head.
The Californian researcher Doris Tsao has deciphered the neural code of
faces.
29
Most neurons do not respond to a single face, but to a whole array
of faces that all share the same features. Some neurons are sensitive to the
distance between the ears, others to chin size or even to higher-level
variables such as age, gender, or whether the face has a smile. About fifty of
these dimensions are enough to encode the entire space of variation of
faces. The correspondence between neurons and faces is so precise that,
from their firing train, one can draw an accurate sketch of the face that the
animal had seen. For the brain, each face is just a point in a fifty-
dimensional neuronal hyperspace.

 Making sense is, quite literally, the role of the cortex. We are bombarded

ARTIFICIAL NEURONS SHED LIGHT  ON
REAL ONES
 Top: Architecture of CORnet, an artificial neural network that roughly mimics the ventral
visual cortex of the macaque monkey. Bottom: Detailed visualization of the operation of a
convolutional neural network, freely available on the Distill website:
 https://distill.pub/2020/grand-tour/.
 Day after day, artificial intelligence comes closer to the human intellect.
Artificial neural networks mimic the architecture of our brains and, in some
cases, even surpass its performance. Organized as a series of layers, each
made up of millions of units similar to neurons, they learn to modify their
connections in order to perform a given function—in this case, image
recognition.


<!-- Page 103 -->

This  artificial  neural  network  was  trained  to  recognize  numbers,
regardless of their shape, size, or position. For decades now, post offices
throughout the world have been using such software to identify addresses
and postal codes.
35
The system relies on a hierarchy of processing stages
similar to those performed by our successive visual areas. To each portion
of the image, each neuron at the lowest level applies a simple filter that
searches, for example, for the corner that characterizes the number 7. At the
next stage, other neurons collect the votes of a small group of neighboring
units and keep a trace of their maximum value: this is the best evidence that
the image contains a 7. The process repeats at many levels—and in parallel,
many other neurons look for the typical characteristics of the other nine
numbers. All these messages compete, and may the best one win! The
recognized number is the one that gets the most votes in its favor.
Our brain too relies on a hierarchy of successive steps, where neurons
both collaborate and compete for the best interpretation of the input image.
Neuroscientists have even developed a measure called “Brain Score,”
which assesses how closely an artificial neural network captures actual
brain signals.
36
The CORnet network, shown here, exhibits responses quite
similar to those of the primate brain.
37
However, the jury isn't out yet. In a
healthy  emulation  between  artificial  intelligence  and  neuroscience,
researchers keep discovering new engineering techniques that bring the
computer closer to the brain. The latest of those tricks is the addition of
recurrent connections: the real brain does not solely operate from the
bottom up, but comprises a majority of connections in the opposite, top-
down direction. This useful addition allows artificial networks to converge,
slowly but surely, toward a better interpretation of their inputs.

CONCEPT  CELLS
 Human neuron sensitive to Luke Skywalker, or maybe to all Star Wars Jedi knights,
recorded by Rodrigo Quian Quiroga in Itzhak Fried's laboratory at the University of
California, Los Angeles.
 Do you know Luke Skywalker? If you have ever seen an episode of the  Star
Wars  saga, you must be familiar with the Jedi knight of Tatooine. And if so,
then your brain probably contains neurons tuned to him, like the one shown
here.
42
 Each of our brains contains millions of cells that are specialized for the
people we know—not just our close relatives, famous politicians, or movie
stars, but anyone you've heard about through all kinds of media. And these
neurons have a unique feature: in the human species, they respond to
people's names, written or spoken, just as well as to their faces.
43


<!-- Page 107 -->

The Argentine  researcher  Rodrigo  Quian  Quiroga  discovered  these
neurons by collaborating with the neurosurgeon Itzhak Fried. Deep into the
temporal cortex of epileptic patients, they inserted thin electrodes, each of
which could record one or a few neurons at random. Occasionally, the
researchers could see the neurons emit a spike or two. But how could they
figure out what sort of stimuli made these cells fire? They came up with the
idea of bombarding the patient with dozens of images selected to be likely
to interest the patient. Since the test was held at the University of California
in Los Angeles, it featured many Hollywood stars. Still, most of the time,
the neurons remained silent. However, once in 200 trials, a neuron would
suddenly discharge to one of the images.
Immediately,  the  researchers  rushed  to  the  Internet,  where  they
downloaded tens of images of the same person. The result was spectacular:
any picture of the person, regardless of age, pose, or clothing, and even the
mere mention of the person's name, in speech or in writing, could trigger
the neuron. Single cells in the human temporal cortex could be sharply
tuned to abstract concepts and respond whenever we retrieved our memory
of  them.  They  were  tuned  to  Luke  Skywalker,  Albert  Einstein,  the
Simpsons, the actress Jennifer Aniston, or to places like the World Trade
Center or the Tower of Pisa.
Take a closer look: this neuron does not respond solely to Luke, but also
to Yoda, his wise Jedi master. Is it because the mention of one immediately
triggers the thought of the other? Or because this neuron encodes a broader
concept—Jedi knight, perhaps? What is certain is that each of our memories
is encoded by a small group of neurons in the temporal cortex. Alas, the
neural hubs that assemble names, people, and places are the first to go when
Alzheimer's disease invades the brain.

in words. And conversely, whenever someone speaks to us, their words
evoke in us an awareness of their meaning.
Which areas are responsible for spoken language? Phrenology postulated
the existence of a language area, but wrongly located it below the left eye.
In the 1960s, the linguist Noam Chomsky speculated that all humans
possessed a  language acquisition device , a system specialized in language
acquisition—but he had, at the time, no direct way to empirically verify his
hypothesis.
Today, thanks to functional MRI, the network of brain areas involved in
language processing can be mapped in a few minutes.
50
Lay a volunteer
down in the MRI machine and have them listen, alternately, to sentences in
their native language and in an unknown language. The difference between
these two conditions reveals a string of cortical regions specialized in
spoken language processing. They are shown here in warm colors: red for
the most active, yellow for those one notch below.
These areas are distributed throughout the temporal lobe, from its most
anterior tip to its junction with the parietal lobe. Some of them are hardly
visible, because they are buried in the depths of cortical folds. Others, at the
front of the brain, shine like nuggets in a gold mine. This sector is part of
Broca's area, the region of inferior frontal cortex discovered by Paul Broca
and  whose  lesion  causes  deficits  of  sentence  formation,  parsing,  and
articulation. Its activation would be even more intense if the person was
asked to speak rather than listen—but even in silence, it lights up whenever
we select words to compose our thoughts.

 Recognizing a face, making a decision, reading, calculating, speaking—the
surface of our two hemispheres is covered with regions specialized for all
of our daily life activities. But what happens when we relax and let our
mind wander without thinking about anything in particular? Besides, is it
really possible to think of nothing? Does the brain ever stop working?


<!-- Page 113 -->

The answer is one of the striking discoveries of the last thirty years.
51
 Not only does the brain never take a break, but its spontaneous activity at
rest is responsible for most of its energy consumption. Its energy bill is
dramatically  unbalanced:  responses  to  external  demands,  the  famous
stimulus-response chains dear to Pavlov and the behaviorists, make up only
2 percent of the consumption of the cortex. All the rest corresponds to the
maintenance of our inner world—minding the billions of neurons and
synapses that store our memories and secret thoughts.
The brain's circuitry is equally unbalanced in favor of inner signals.
Simply count the synapses that bring information from the outside, bottom-
up, and those that send information in the opposite direction, top-down:
even in the primary visual cortex, inputs from the retina account for less
than 10 percent of the synapses. Thus, even in sensory areas, the dominant
signals arise from within. They probably represent anticipations and mental
images that we can elicit at will, from memory alone.
52
 If the brain's inner activity was long neglected, it is in part because of its
unpredictable character. For many years, most researchers mistook it for
noise, and they analyzed only the average activity evoked by external
stimuli. However, such averaging suppresses all the signal from within.
Spontaneous neuronal activity lives only in the moment: in a conscious
brain, it constantly fluctuates as circuits ceaselessly form and break.
53

regions that coactivate whenever we relax and stop doing anything, and is
therefore called the “default-mode network.” It is involved in inner speech
and private reflections on oneself and others. Its activity signals an episode
of introspection and a call to autobiographical memory. Everything that we
experience and that we judge important, our brain spontaneously relives and
replays, sometimes dozens of times, within our inner world.

what fiddlestrings and harps, drums and tambours

 The symphony of the brain is never finished. Throughout our lifetime, at
any age, our brain keeps changing under the influence of education and


<!-- Page 115 -->

encounters. The plasticity of the human brain is unparalleled in the animal
world: our species spends more than twenty years of youth refining its
internal  models  of  the  outside  world,  and  it  keeps  fine-tuning  them

without  mentioning  its  immense  capacity  to  learn,  which  artificial
intelligence is still far from imitating.
Learning begins even before birth, as synapses are made and unmade at
a rate of several million per second. Since the very first months, the baby's
brain, while constrained by evolution, quickly adapts to its environment. It
relies on its evolutionary heritage to project tentative hypotheses about the
outside world, and corrects them based on the success or failure of its
predictions.
Humanity invented schooling to take advantage of this remarkable
plasticity. Every cultural invention that we acquire, such as reading, writing,
or arithmetic, repurposes our ancient brain circuits and puts them to novel
uses. The capacity to read emerges from a pyramid of areas evolved for
object and face recognition. An old predisposition for numbers and space
serves as the foundation for the edifice of mathematics.
Learning is an integral part of being human. Let's discover some recent
images of a brain in perpetual motion.

This happy baby is wearing a high-tech brain-imaging instrument: a net

When the child is stimulated with a series of syllables— ba ,  ba ,  ba —

monotony of the stimulus and is able to predict what comes next. If we now
introduce a different syllable, such as  ga , the baby's brain is shocked by this

weak, but a second peak, 400 milliseconds after the onset of the sound,
jumps back to its initial level. This “dishabituation” response, lateralized to
the left hemisphere, proves that babies can detect the difference, however
minimal, between the syllables  ba  and  ga .

AN  EARLY  WIRING

 Eight weeks ago, a sperm met an egg, and the magic of epigenesis began:
the spontaneous formation of a human being. At two months of pregnancy,

it is already equipped with a well-developed nervous system. Its nerves,
radiating from the spinal cord, already reach their main targets and cover
the whole surface of the body.
11
 The initial wiring of our nerves is mostly under genetic control. Our
hands, for instance, are always innervated by three distinct nerves: the

 In the fetus, the first cortical folds appear at around six and a half months of
gestation. These folds are the trick that our primate brain uses to boost its
surface area, and therefore the number of neurons, without increasing the
diameter of the skull beyond reason—after all, that skull will soon have to
pass  through  the  narrow  tunnel  of  the  pelvis  during  delivery.  This
fundamental constraint played a key role in human evolution: by forcing us


<!-- Page 126 -->

to be born with a small, immature brain, it magnified our chances of
learning from interactions with our environment.
Around twenty-eight weeks of pregnancy, therefore, the human cortex
begins to fold.
12
Its first wrinkles are common to all primates: at around
eight months of pregnancy, a preterm baby's brain looks rather similar to a
chimpanzee's. Quickly, though, secondary and tertiary folds appear. They
emerge in between the main wrinkles and are both more specific to humans
and more variable from one individual to the other. At birth, each baby's
brain already possesses its own individuality—its folds are just as unique as
its fingerprints, and it is impossible to confuse them with those of other
apes.
By the middle of the third trimester of pregnancy, a well-informed
anatomist can already tell whether they are dealing with a  Homo sapiens  or
a chimpanzee embryo. Indeed, our cortex exhibits a singular characteristic:
the asymmetry of the two hemispheres.
13
In 96 percent of us, the left
hemisphere houses the processing of language, and this leftward bias can be
detected as early as a month and a half before birth. At this age, a deep
groove  called  the  superior  temporal  sulcus  is  shallower  in  the  left
hemisphere than in the right, probably because its deepening is blocked by
the arcuate bundle, a huge bundle of fibers that runs through the left
hemisphere and interconnects language areas. All human beings show this
asymmetry, which is totally absent in other primates. To be born a human is
to be born different.

THE GRADUAL  MATURATION  OF THE
CORTEX
 Top: Progressive folding of the cortex. Bottom: maturation of the gray matter in different
regions of the human cortex. MRI images acquired on NeuroSpin's 3-tesla MRI scanner by
Jessica Dubois and Jessica Lebenberg of Ghislaine Dehaene-Lambertz's team.
 Wrinkling is just one step in the lengthy development of the human brain.
This macroscopic change masks myriad other microscopic, cellular, and
molecular events that punctuate brain maturation around birth and beyond.
During the third trimester of pregnancy, billions of neurons migrate and
gradually populate the cortical ribbon. Two streams of neurons crisscross
and complement each other. The pyramidal neurons, which are excitatory,


<!-- Page 128 -->

arise from the depth of the brain, the periventricular germinal zone where
they are born from the repeated division of progenitor cells. Clinging to the
vines of glial cells, they climb toward the cortex until they overtake the
neurons already in place—the layers of the cortex are built from the bottom
up. At the same time, a river of smaller cells flows along the developing
cortical surface and supplies the cortical tissue with inhibitory neurons.
From the combination of these two flows, increasingly complex circuits are
born, which will take years to fully mature.
An important step in this cortical maturation is called “myelination.”
With time, axons wrap themselves in an insulating sheath, myelin, which
speeds up the transmission of nervous discharges. However, not all the
regions of cortex mature at the same speed: some are clearly ahead of
others. Toward the beginning of the twentieth century, the great German
neuroanatomist Paul Flechsig noticed it in postmortem brains, and today we
can visualize it in vivo thanks to MRI.
This false color image shows the degree of maturity of the different
regions of cortex in babies between zero and six months. At this age, the
primary areas, associated with vision, hearing, touch, and motricity, are
already myelinated. Their early maturation allows the child to begin to
make sense of their sensory inputs. Secondary cortical areas take months to
attain  their  maturity,  and  the  most  anterior  regions  of  temporal  and
prefrontal cortex, which appear in light and dark blue, lag behind by several
years.
Interestingly, the regions that are most unique to our species are also
those that mature the latest. Flechsig called them “associative” because they
interconnect to form a dense network capable of interweaving the data from
all sensory modalities. It takes years for them to sort out the incoming
information. For the cortex, ripening is a lifelong process.

AN EXUBERANCE  OF CONNECTIONS

Overproduction and progressive elimination of synapses and dendritic arborizations in

 Below the surface of the cortex, one of the most important events in brain
development is taking place: its wiring.
At birth, as can be seen in the upper images, the brain's major bundles of
connections are already in place. Their growth occurs mostly under genetic
guidance. However, their terminations are not fully established. Axons have
to search for their targets, sometimes for years, and their wiring depends on
experience. Synapse after synapse, learning shapes our nerve endings.


<!-- Page 130 -->

The child's cortex is an exuberant jungle of connections. At birth, most
cortical neurons are still very thin, and their arborizations form only a few
scattered and bare bushes. Very quickly, however, buds appear everywhere.
The first six months of life are the springtime of the developing brain:
dendritic  and  axonal  branches  multiply  rapidly  until  they  form  an
inextricable thicket, even denser than that of the adult.
This is the paradox: in children between the ages of one and two,
synapses are far more numerous than in adults. During development, our
cortex produces up to twice as many synapses than what it will eventually
need. This synaptic overproduction allows us to learn by selection. A
microcircuit blossoms randomly, grows in size, and is put to the test. If it is
useful, it will be preserved, even amplified, because other synapses will be
created to support the pioneering ones. If, on the other hand, the circuit is
not effective, it will be pruned in favor of other, more useful connections.
Such pruning does not occur at the same speed all over the cortex.
Sensory areas are the earliest. In the visual cortex, the overproduction of
synapses reaches its peak at around two years of age, and most circuits are
stabilized after a few years. The sensitive period is brief, which is why
attention must be paid to amblyopia or strabismus: if the squint is not
swiftly fixed, ideally before the age of three, visual circuits will forever
remain unable to evaluate the three-dimensional depth of images.
In the prefrontal cortex, on the other hand, the peak is attained much
later, and synaptic elimination continues until the age of twenty or twenty-
five. Our educational system has adjusted to our biology: we train our
children until their plasticity becomes less fluid. In adults, individual
synapses may remain stable for months or years.

SYNAPSES: THE MUSHROOMS  OF
LEARNING
 Three-dimensional reconstruction of the synapses that connect the axon of one neuron
(blue) with the dendrites of another neuron (green). The dendritic spines that receive these
synapses have been colored yellow and orange. Reconstruction based on electron
microscopy and performed by Bobby Kasthuri at Argonne National Laboratory, near
Chicago.
 When we speak of brain plasticity, the term must be taken in a very literal
sense: the brain is a pliable organ.
Thanks to the invention of new microscopes, it has become possible to
visualize not only the anatomy of synapses, but also their movement in real
time. And as strange as it may seem, neurons constantly move, especially


<!-- Page 132 -->

during development and education: their axonal tips grow and shrink as
they grope around for new contacts, while their dendritic trees bear the birth
and death of new synapses, with a turnover of millions of synapses per
second in the child's brain.
The minuscule mushrooms that cover the branches of this neuron are
called “dendritic spines.” Their spiny shapes are characteristic of pyramidal
neurons. Each spine hosts one or sometimes several synapses coming in
from other neurons. These organelles are ceaselessly moving: their size and
even their very existence can change within a few tens of minutes as a
function of learning.
Numerous experiments over the past twenty years have shown that
learning is primarily based on the strengthening or the elimination of
synapses, which constitute the memory traces of our experiences. Neuronal
activity  selectively  modulates  the  strength  and  stability  of  synapses.
Roughly speaking, a synapse whose two neurons (pre-synaptic and post-
synaptic) discharge simultaneously increases in strength: “neurons that fire
together wire together.” In this case, the size of the synapse grows to
accommodate more receptor molecules. Its strength is directly reflected in
the size of its spine's neck, which increases accordingly. Conversely, if a
synapse is no longer useful, if it goes against the flow, then its effectiveness
decreases, and it may ultimately vanish.
Artificial neural networks help us understand how millions of synaptic
changes allow us to learn to recognize a face or an object. In the real brain,
however, synapses are not the only correlate of learning. Axons, and
especially their myelination, may also change in order to accelerate the
processing of information. In the cerebellum, learning may also be based on
adjustments purely internal to the cell, without any synaptic modification.
15
 We are still far from understanding the vast nanometer-scale flexibility of
the developing brain.

THE JAILER  OF OUR NEURONS

Sigal, Xiaowei Zhuang, and Takao Hensch at Harvard University in Cambridge,
Massachusetts.
 The fluorescent grid that closely girdles those adult nerve cells is the jailer
of our neurons.
With age, certain neurons, particularly the large inhibitory neurons that
exert a tight control over the nearby cortex, become surrounded by a
“perineuronal net,” a rigid lattice that holds them still.
16
This aggregate of
proteins and sugar chains, which shares some ingredients with cartilage,
prevents nerve cells from moving around. Trapped within this chainmail,
the neuron can no longer form new connections, or even modify those


<!-- Page 135 -->

already in place. Locked in a sugary jail, adult nerve cells lose their
plasticity.
Perineuronal nets seem to play a key role in the closure of brain
plasticity. Their growth marks the end of the sensitive period, a phase of
exuberant plasticity during which a brain area rapidly adapts to the outside
world. During infancy, the brain possesses a vast learning potential and can,
for example, acquire any language. Everyone knows that this faculty
disappears—as adults, we have great difficulty learning a new language,
and we will always zpeak it with an aczent and with grammaticals error.
17
 Scientists used to speak of a “critical period” for learning, but today we
prefer the term “sensitive period,” because neuronal plasticity gets reduced
but never fully closes down. However, the result is essentially the same:
during the sensitive period, the child's brain learns at record speed; once
that golden age is over, it favors stability over plasticity.
Understanding how synaptic plasticity opens and closes is a major goal
for clinical research. Indeed, if we could return adult neurons to the
flexibility of their childhood, we might be able to erase traumatic memories
or alleviate the aftereffects of a stroke. This is not just a distant dream: in
animals, several pharmacological agents that dissolve perineural networks
seem to reopen plasticity and allow the nervous system to forget some
memories and replace them with new ones.
Psychedelics such as LSD or psylocibin (“magic mushrooms”) figure
among those promising molecules. Although long decried, they are now
proving to be of growing interest in psychiatry. MDMA, for example, better
known as ecstasy, can rejuvenate adult mice: it reopens a sensitive period
for learning how to live in society, which closes with puberty.
18
In humans,
the same molecule, in combination with psychotherapy, seems to alleviate
the post-traumatic stress disorder that affects veterans of the Iraq war.
19
 Will scientists soon learn to safely break the jail that imprisons our adult
neurons, and free up their synaptic plasticity?

competence, unexpected in evolution: recognizing scribbles on paper and,
in a fraction of a second, giving them a meaning.

Learning to read is gaining access to language through vision.

to read only as adults—but to a lesser extent. Beyond the sensitive period of
childhood, it is very difficult, perhaps even impossible, for an adult to reach
a high reading speed. This is why our schools take advantage of brain
plasticity by starting very early.

NEURONAL  RECYCLING
 Impact of literacy on visual areas. Each colored circle represents a group of people ranging
from those who are illiterate, with a reading score close to zero, to those who are highly
literate, who can read a hundred words or more per minute. The better one reads, the more
the visual word form area responds to letters and stops responding to other images. When
the visual areas of the left hemisphere are invaded by letters, faces shift to the right

 Reading is a recent invention. The alphabet is no more than 4,000 years old,
a trifle compared with the time scale of biological evolution. Darwinian
selection had no time to adapt our brain circuits to the written letter. And
yet,  in  the  brain  of  all  good  readers,  a  dedicated  region  of  the  left
hemisphere is in charge of identifying written words. In our scientific
papers, Laurent Cohen and I termed it the “visual word form area”—but


<!-- Page 141 -->

informally, I dub it the “brain's letter box,” because it concentrates all our
knowledge of letters and their relations.
How is it that our brain dedicates such a specific circuit to reading? The
solution is simple: reading acquisition takes advantage of the plasticity of a
preexisting  brain  circuit,  in  this  case  the  areas  for  object  and  face
recognition. By exploiting their margin of plasticity, schooling redirects part
of them toward the shapes of letters. This is what I call  neuronal recycling .
Whenever we learn a new skill, such as reading or algebra, we reconvert an
old circuit that evolved for a completely different role, and we repurpose it
to fit its new function.
Let us look at what happens in the brains of people who are illiterate.
23
If
we had not had the chance to go to school and learn to read, would we
possess a silent brain site? Would the brain's letter box remain inactive,
because it would be deprived of inputs? Not at all. As you can see here, in a
person who is illiterate, the site of the visual word form area responds
vigorously to faces, objects, and checkerboards. And the better a person can
read, the more this response to pictures decreases, in favor of strings of
familiar letters.
The better we read, the more letters invade a visual area that would
otherwise specialize in faces and objects. But then, what happens to the
latter categories of knowledge? Do we lose them as we learn to read? No,
but we move them around. While responses to letters increase in the left
hemisphere, responses to faces shift to the right hemisphere. The better we
read, the more the visual areas are lateralized: to the left for written words,
to the right for faces. This finding supports the idea of neuronal recycling.
Given  that  we  possess  a  nearly  fixed  number  of  neurons,  reading
acquisition induces a competition within the cortex and reshuffles our
cortical maps.

BRAIN PLASTICITY AND  REORGANIZATION
 Reorganization of the visual word form area in an eleven-year-old child who underwent
extensive surgical removal of the left occipitotemporal region (lesion shown in red in the top
image). Images acquired by Laurent Cohen at the Salpêtrière Hospital in Paris.
 Since reading involves a dedicated circuit, always at the same location in all
of us, what would happen if this circuit were damaged? Would we lose the
ability to read?
In adults, the answer is an unfortunate yes. Even a small stroke, if it
affects the brain's letter box area, can render any of us unable to read.
26
This
syndrome is called pure alexia, and it was described as early as 1892 by the
French physician Joseph-Jules Déjerine. “Alexia” means loss of reading,
but why “pure”? Because the deficit can be extraordinarily selective. The


<!-- Page 145 -->

patient is not blind; he can still recognize faces, objects, places, sometimes
even numbers or musical scores. However, he is unable to read a word: for
him, the pages of any book look as if they are full of indecipherable
characters. He does not suffer from a general language disorder, however,
since he can still speak. He may even be able to write—but a few seconds
later, he is unable to reread his own writing! Many such patients never
recover fluent reading.
Remarkably, the same lesion, in childhood, does not have the same
impact at all. The child's brain is so plastic that it is much more resistant to
damage, even massive damage.
Take the child whose brain is shown here. This little girl underwent
major brain surgery at the age of four because she suffered from Sturge-
Weber syndrome, a congenital disease accompanied by epileptic seizures.
To control them, the surgeon removed her entire left occipital lobe and part
of the posterior temporal region, including the usual site of the brain's letter
box. After surgery, this child went to school, where she easily learned to
read. When Laurent Cohen scanned her at age eleven, her letter box was
perfectly in place, exactly where it should be—but in the intact right
hemisphere! The rest of her language areas, which had been spared by the
surgery, were still at their normal position in the left hemisphere, but
reading itself now involved a dedicated right-hemisphere site.
In summary, the vast plasticity of the child's brain allows it to reorganize
itself. It contains a privileged circuit for reading, but if this circuit is
damaged, a second, redundant one, exactly symmetrical to the first, can
replace it.

THE BRAIN AS A  STATISTICIAN
 Patches of cortex specialized for reading in a person who is bilingual in English and French.
The colored bars show the activation evoked by fourteen different types of letter strings that
vary in their proximity to the statistics of English and French. Research by Minye Zhan,
Laurent Cohen, and the author using NeuroSpin's 7-tesla MRI scanner.
 How do human neurons get tuned to reading? We don't know yet, but
thanks to the increasing power of its magnetic field, we can use MRI to
zoom in on the portions of the cortex that activate during reading, and try to
understand what excites them.
The images you see here were acquired at a scale of 1.2 millimeters
using NeuroSpin's 7-tesla MRI machine. Unlike most previous images, they
represent not the average of a few dozen people, but the brain of a single


<!-- Page 147 -->

volunteer. Unblurred, they show the reading circuit with unprecedented
resolution.
First surprise: what previously seemed to be a vast region engaged in
reading has exploded into a constellation of scattered patches. It is as if, by
deploying a new telescope, we discover that what we thought was a star is
actually a galaxy.
The new images suggest that reading mobilizes tiny specialized areas.
This is exactly what has been observed for face recognition: a small number
of cortical patches contain up to 98 percent of neurons entirely dedicated to
faces.
27
Similar learning rules probably govern the acquisition of reading
and face recognition.
Second surprise: these regions accumulate statistics on written words,
regardless of language. The person you see here is bilingual in English and
French. However, their brain does not contain any region dedicated to only
one of these languages. Instead, the two writing systems overlap. What
matters to the brain is not which language is written, but whether a
sequence of letters respects the statistics of the languages that the person
can read.
Here, we manipulated those statistics continually by presenting stimuli
ranging  from  completely  implausible  strings,  made  of  rare  letters,  to
reasonable ones that might be real words—and some actually were. In the
visual areas of occipital cortex, all of those stimuli evoke the same activity:
only the lines they contain matter. However, higher up into the cortex, we
begin to see a specialization in favor of the known languages. In the
temporal cortex, and even more so in Broca's area, a sequence of letters
must look like a plausible word in order to excite the neuronal circuits for
reading.
Thus, with experience, human neurons probably become tuned to the
statistics of writing. I speculate that the cortex of all good readers houses
neurons tuned to frequent letters, common letter pairs, word roots, and
perhaps even entire words—a prediction that could ultimately be tested
once we manage to record from single cells in humans.
28
 Reading is just one example; in all domains, our brain acts as a
statistician  that  accumulates  observations  and  uses  them  to  refine  its
internal models of the world.

NUMBER  NEURONS
 Discovery of number neurons in the macaque monkey. The regions in red, in the prefrontal
and parietal cortex, contain a large proportion of neurons tuned to a given number of
objects, here between one and five. Research carried out by Andreas Nieder and his
colleagues, first at MIT, then at the University of Tübingen in Germany.
 Learning to read is clearly the prerogative of the human species, but what
about mathematics? Is basic math accessible to other animals? To find out,
Andreas Nieder and Earl Miller examined whether macaque monkeys
understand the concept of number.
29
They trained them on a same/different
judgment: on each trial, the animal saw two sets of objects, one after the
other, and had to decide whether they shared the same number.


<!-- Page 149 -->

First finding: after some training, the monkeys performed this task
remarkably well. They succeeded with a great variety of configurations, and
could clearly focus solely on number. Their only problem was to answer
“same” when the two numbers were close, like 4 and 5. Thus, monkeys
cannot count, but can only estimate an approximate quantity of items.
Second finding: the macaque brain contains neurons tuned to number.
Each of these cells responds to a certain number of objects. Some fire only
when  the  animal  sees  one  object;  others  prefer  two;  still  others  are
stimulated by seeing three, four, or five objects. Each neuron possesses a
favorite number, but their tuning is not perfectly sharp: neurons that fire the
most to four objects, for example, also respond to three or five objects.
Again, monkeys’ number sense is approximate.
A similar number sense is present in humans.
30
We too are capable of
estimating a number without counting, and our parietal cortex contains a
number map with different sectors tuned to different quantities of objects.
31
 Single neurons similar to those of monkeys have even been found in the
human hippocampus.
32
 None of this, by the way, is unique to primates. Many species, without
any special training, pay attention to the numbers around them. Number
neurons virtually identical to those of monkeys have even been observed in
crows.
33
Basic arithmetic is so useful for survival, whether to find as much
food as possible or to check how many conspecifics are in a coalition, that
many distinct animal lineages seem to have evolved the same sort of brain
circuit for number sense; alternatively, number may be such a fundamental
concept that the brain has paid attention to it for hundreds of millions of
years.
Is there nothing special, then, about us humans? Of course there is: we
are  the  only  animal  that  manages  to  overcome  the  fuzziness  of  this
approximate number sense. Using symbols, we develop a precise and
rigorous  arithmetic  that  serves  as  the  foundation  for  the  edifice  of
mathematics. The acquisition of a language of mathematics considerably
amplifies the initial potential of our brain.

THE BRAIN OF  MATHEMATICIANS

professional mathematicians. Work by Marie Amalric and the author at the NeuroSpin
center, Saclay, France.
 What happens in the brains of those who learn mathematics? Do great
mathematicians develop specific brain circuits? Which areas of the cortex
allow them to think about infinite-dimensional vector spaces, imaginary
numbers, or group theory?
To answer those questions, Marie Amalric and I conducted the first
systematic brain-imaging experiments of professional mathematicians.
34
 Lying in NeuroSpin's 3-tesla MRI scanner, they would hear sentences such
as  “The  Euclidean  orthogonal  group  has  exactly  two  connected
components” and had to decide whether they were true or not (this one is


<!-- Page 151 -->

true, but to nonmathematicians, it is far from obvious). Other sentences, of
equal difficulty, assessed nonmathematical knowledge, for example, “The
end of the Council of Trent coincides with the fall of the Western Roman
Empire” (this is false).
The results showed that the brain uses completely different areas to store
mathematical and nonmathematical knowledge. When a mathematician
thinks about math—whether it is analysis, algebra, geometry, or topology—
the parietal and inferior temporal regions of both hemispheres light up. A
completely different set of regions, notably, the temporal pole and the
angular  gyrus,  encode  other  types  of  semantic  knowledge.  The
mathematician's  brain  therefore  houses  a  specialized  circuit  for
mathematical knowledge.
In  our  experiment,  mathematicians  activated  these  regions  when
listening to mathematical sentences, but this was not the case for the
humanities  and  social  sciences  teachers  who  listened  to  the  same
statements. Does this mean that mathematicians possess a radically different
cortical organization than the average person? Not at all: the regions for
mathematics are present in each of us. All it takes to activate them are much
simpler sentences such as “How much is two times three?” or “Any number
multiplied by zero is zero.” In fact, these areas have been around for a long
time in evolution: they are the same ones that light up when a macaque
monkey evaluates an approximate quantity of objects.
In a nutshell, learning mathematics recycles a circuit that has existed for
millions of years, and which is already interested in numbers and space.
However, only the human species, with its remarkable knack for language
and symbols, manages to build, on top of this ancient foundation, a pyramid
of concepts of increasing abstraction: whole numbers, decimals, fractions,
operations, functions, infinite spaces, and other concepts.

 Why is it that some people, like Lautréamont, adore mathematics, while
others hate it? Education plays a major role: all human brains, if they go to

the right school, can learn to master questions that, in ancient times, stood
up to the greatest mathematicians.
Genetics also comes into play. Mathematical skills are partly heritable:
identical twins tend to share the same talents, even if they are raised under
different conditions. Conversely, some genetic diseases, particularly when
they affect the parietal lobe, are associated with disorders of mathematical
operations, or dyscalculia.
35
However, no gene specifically associated with
mathematics has yet been identified with certainty.
In the study illustrated here, the researchers examined the impact of ten
candidate genes on the brains of children aged three to six years.
36

across  individuals  correlated  with  developmental  impairments  in  the

different groups, the alleles of the ROBO1 gene modulated the thickness of
the cortex in the right intraparietal region. This thickness, in turn, predicted
about 10–20 percent of the variance in school grades in mathematics
between the ages of seven and nine.
This result makes sense: not only is the intraparietal sulcus a core region
that activates as soon as we think about numbers and math, but it lights up
most clearly in the right hemisphere of children and newborns. From birth,
in fact, all children possess a number sense, the ability to assess, without
counting, the number of a set of objects—and this innate ability seems to be
associated with the right parietal region.
37
 In brief, we all share a knack for math, but it is modulated by genetic
variants, including those of the ROBO1 gene. Dozens of other genes are
also likely to be involved. If the genetics of cognitive functions is still in its
infancy, it is undoubtedly because the effects of genes and education are
irremediably intertwined. We should not overestimate the power of genes:
their effects are real, but they are dwarfed by the power of education.

WHEN WRITING RECYCLES  AN ANCIENT
BRAIN CIRCUIT

 When we learn to read, whether it is words or equations, we recycle older
brain circuits that we reorient to a new cultural use. Indeed, we are born
with an essentially fixed pool of neurons, but their function can be partially
redirected.


<!-- Page 157 -->

The flexibility of our brain circuits, however, is not unlimited. This is
why each of our cultural inventions, such as writing or mathematical
notation, must fit within the constraints of our cortex. Centuries of cultural
evolution gradually select the forms that are the easiest to learn, those that
require the least cortical change because they match our preexisting skills.
Take a look at this random array of black and white lines. Within it, a
round shape stands out. Why? Because its segments are roughly aligned.
This pop-out effect arises from the primary visual area V1. There, the
neurons are specialized in the detection of image contours. To fill this role,
each neuron responds to bars of a given orientation, and those that prefer
aligned bars tend to interconnect and support each other.
40
The larger the

shape that they trace pops out, exactly as if its contour lines were brighter
than the others. A moment later, the neurons that code for the interior of the
figure  are  also  amplified,  and  the  entire  image  stands  out  from  the
background.
Egyptian scribes seem to have discovered this effect 4,500 years ago:
they invented the “cartouche,” the ovoid shape that frames a pharaoh's
name. Thanks to it, the name of the ruler stands out as if it were written in
bold type. On the same principle, around the fifteenth century, Florentine
and  Venetian  typesetters  invented  brackets  and  parentheses,  and
mathematicians quickly adopted them to facilitate the grouping of terms in
an equation. In my lab, we have shown that, within a mathematical
equation, the area enclosed in parentheses jumps to the eye: it causes an
overactivation at the corresponding location of the visual cortex, and thus
attracts our gaze in less than one fifth of a second.
41
 Think about it the next time a colleague circles your mistakes with a
pencil—they are just exploiting the architecture of your visual cortex to get
your attention!

 PRIMATES?

 What is this macaque monkey thinking? Do his thoughts differ from ours?
Is he able to calculate, imagine, predict, communicate, sympathize, or
regret?
The deep look of this adult monkey can be deceiving. True, he is skillful
with his hands, recognizes faces, lives in society, collaborates with others,
maps space, and understands the concept of number. Yet he does not think


<!-- Page 162 -->

like we do. Numerous ethological experiments suggest that there is a
genuine gap between us and other primates. We are singular in many ways.
The first singularity that comes to mind is language. All species, except
ours, communicate only by means of a limited repertoire of vocal calls. We
are the only ones to combine these calls into phrases and sentences.
1
All our
languages are based on a double articulation: on the one hand, a few dozen
phonemes combine to form tens of thousands of words; on the other hand,

human group without language: even in communities of people who are
deaf,  the  sophisticated  structure  of  sign  language  emerges  within  a
generation.
2
None of this exists in our chimpanzee cousins, even when they
are raised in a human family.

understand each other. We know that others do not necessarily share the
same ideas as we do. We are aware that they may ignore information, forget
it,  lie,  or  pretend. Although  it  remains  debated,  it  seems  that  those
competences are absent in other primates: they lack the ability to represent
the thoughts of others. In particular, they do not distinguish well between
what they know and what others know.
3
As a result, they do not teach.
Education is the prerogative of the human species and gives us an immense
evolutionary advantage: we are  Homo docens , we educate each other.
4
 Our species is also the only one to have invented mathematics and

objects, but they do not understand that 9 and 10 are equally distinct

5
They are also
unable to understand geometry, and fail to grasp that a square is more

6

four species of primates, from the tiny gray mouse monkey, a mouse-sized
lemur, to the much larger  Homo sapiens . Together, they cover a significant


<!-- Page 164 -->

fraction of all Old World and New World monkeys, whose evolutionary tree
spans more than 70 million years.
What does this picture reveal? Obviously, brain size increases a lot in
great apes, here labeled by the technical term  Hominoidea . This is the group
to which we belong, and within it, we clearly hold the record for biggest
brain of all. A rule also emerges: the bigger the brain, the more it folds, thus
allowing to fit even more gray matter within the skull.
Now, take a close look at the left and right hemispheres. You may see
that, in humans, they became increasingly asymmetrical. In the course of
hominization, the two sides of the brain lost their equipotentiality and began
specializing for different functions. Distributing the work between the two
hemispheres is yet another way to save space. In this domain too, our
species is unique: we seem to be the only ones to possess circuitry almost
fully lateralized to a single hemisphere—typically, language on the left and
spatial attention on the right.
Why this race toward a bigger brain? Larger brains house more neurons
and more synapses, which are the computing units of the nervous system.
Simulations of artificial neural networks indicate that the deeper a network,
the better it learns: more layers and units allow for greater abstraction. And
indeed,  across  species,  brain  size  correlates  with  cognitive  abilities,
including the ability to adapt to new living conditions and to a complex
social environment.
However, brain size does not suffice to explain the human singularity.
Indeed, the bodies and the brains of all primates are linked by scaling laws,
a field called “allometry”—and once these laws are taken into account, the
proportions of our brain no longer seem exceptional: the size of our
neocortex is as expected for a primate of our body size. Whence, then, do
our exceptional faculties arise? Brain size matters, but does not explain
everything: we must look elsewhere, into how we use it.

 At birth, the human brain is not much larger than that of other primates.

Greek  neo , “new,” and  teinein , “to extend”—that is, the extension of the

mammals and primates, our species has remained childlike. Our skulls,
brains, neurons, and synapses retain many juvenile characteristics.
8

 There is little doubt that humans are characterized by their big brains—but

allometry. In the end, is the human brain just a big primate brain? The

 Compared with other primates, the expansion of the human neocortex is
spectacular, particularly that of the prefrontal cortex, located behind the
forehead. This is a long-term evolutionary trend: relative to other brain
areas, the prefrontal cortex has undergone several episodes of expansion in
the great apes.
17
The first occurred within the hominoid branch, which
includes gibbons, gorillas, orangutans, and humans. Scientists estimate that,
in their common ancestor, who lived some 20 million years ago, the
prefrontal cortex had already increased three times more than the primary


<!-- Page 172 -->

visual cortex. Another expansion of the same order occurred within the
hominid branch—and yet another huge factor of six again separates our
prefrontal cortex from that of our closest cousins, the chimpanzees. All in
all, humans ended up with a huge blistering of the prefrontal surface, which
hosts so many of our higher cognitive faculties.
A prominent human prefrontal function is the control of language
production. Broca's famous area is located at the foot of the third frontal
convolution of the left hemisphere. Does this region even exist in other
primates? It does, but in a miniature version. Broca's area covers the areas
that the great German neuroanatomist Korbinian Brodmann numbered as 44
and 45, and which are shown here in red and yellow. Their cellular
architecture is not unique to our species: postmortem anatomy reveals the
existence of a similar area in all the primates illustrated here. They lack
speech, of course, but they already use this area to control their hands and
mouths.
There are, however, several key differences between our Broca's area
and its precursors in other primates.
18
Not only did this area become
massively enlarged in the human species, but it also became asymmetric. In
96 percent of right-handed people, language is primarily controlled by the
left hemisphere, and Broca's area is significantly larger in the left than in
the right hemisphere. Not so in chimpanzees: the size of this region may
vary, but not systematically in favor of the left hemisphere.
Closer examination also shows that in this region, neurons are much
more widely spaced in humans than in other apes, thus leaving more room
for dendritic trees, and therefore for connections from distant cortical
regions.

Our species may simply be better connected!

 The silhouettes shown in the figure are not brains, but cerebral endocasts:
casts of the inside of the skull, obtained from fossils of early humans and
their  ancestors.  Those  are  the  only  tenuous  clues  that  allow
paleoanthropologists to ask this crucial question: when did the human
species acquire its large brain? When and how did it acquire its modern
features, such as the expansion of the frontal lobe and language circuits?

The brain, unfortunately, does not fossilize: it is a soft organ that quickly
decays after death. The cranial cavity, however, bears a few permanent
traces of brain shape. During its growth, the brain, or rather the meninges,

marks of its main grooves and of the large arteries that irrigate them. A cast
of the inside of the skull, either in plaster or on a computer, can restore the
approximate shape of the brain and make it possible to estimate the size of
its various sectors.

at the University of Zurich had a surprise: the brains of our ancestors
remain similar to those of the great apes for much longer than previously
thought.
24
Not only did the australopithecines discovered in Ethiopia,
including the famous Lucy who lived more than 3 million years ago, have
small ape-like brains,
25
but so did the earliest members of the genus
 Homo .
26
Our species walked out of Africa with a small brain that, according
to these studies, had not yet acquired a large Broca's area. It was not until

species that lived in Indonesia as recently as 50,000 years ago,
27
and of
 Homo naledi , a small human that dates back 300,000 years and of which
fifteen members were recently discovered in a South African cave,
28
we
know that brain size is not a direct reflection of brain architecture. These
humans had very small brains, yet their neurocognitive characteristics were
very close to those of modern humans. It is therefore less in the size of the
brain than in its development, circuits, and cellular and molecular features
that we must now look for the ultimate roots of human singularity.

THE INVASION OF  ORGANOIDS
 Brain organoids from the laboratories of Alysson Muotri at the University of California, San
Diego (top), and Madeline Lancaster at the University of Cambridge, UK (bottom).
 Contrary to appearances, what you see here is not a brain slice, but a
cerebral organoid: an artificial tissue that grew in vitro, in a glass container
filled with a nutrient gel. As they divided in a dish, the cells self-organized
to form a living, brain-like blob. These objects, at the frontier of biology
and bioengineering, raise complex ethical issues, but also offer incredible
opportunities for neuroscience and medicine.
In 2013, Madeline Lancaster published the first recipe for organoids. Her
work showed how, starting from a single pluripotent stem cell, one could
grow a tissue that mimicked the cellular organization of the developing
human brain.
29
The dividing cells spontaneously differentiated to form


<!-- Page 178 -->

interconnected  brain  regions,  including  a  cortex  with  most  of  the
appropriate subtypes of neurons.
Since then, these strange biological objects, at the frontier of life, have
invaded  laboratories  and  revolutionized  human  neurobiology.  Indeed,
organoids allow us to recapitulate most of human brain development in a
test tube. Thus, the formation of the human brain can be studied in minute
detail, which would be totally inconceivable in utero. Moreover, organoids
can be grown from a single stem cell of virtually any child or adult, whether
healthy or sick—thus paving the way to an understanding of the disorders
that lead to microcephaly, autism, or schizophrenia.
More amazingly still, Alysson Muotri, at the University of California,
San Diego, has shown that cerebral organoids exhibit a rudimentary form of
brain activity. Even though they lie in a dish, disconnected from the
external world, they are constantly traversed by electrical waves that self-
organize into regular patterns reminiscent of those seen in the brains of
premature babies.
30
 Muotri also uses organoids to test new drugs or to probe the impact of
Neanderthal genes.
31
Indeed, organoids offer a unique opportunity to shed
light on the singularity of the human species. Why do we have such large
brains? Madeline Lancaster and her colleagues compared the speed of brain
development in organoids derived from human, gorilla, and chimpanzee
cells.
32
They identified a key stage during which the “progenitor” cells
(which later grow into neurons) multiply exponentially fast. In humans, this
stage lasts two days longer than in other species—a difference that explains
why our brain comprises so many more neurons. A gene called ZEB2 seems
to play the conductor's role: when its activation is artificially delayed in a
nonhuman cerebral organoid, even a gorilla mini-brain starts to resemble
the human brain, at least in size.
What remains unclear is whether its circuits also gain new faculties: do
human organoids perform calculations that surpass those of other mini-
brains? And if so, could we harness their power in artificial intelligence or
medicine? Cerebral organoids are one of the hottest and most controversial
areas of contemporary neuroscience.

cortex, recorded at the Free University of Amsterdam in the Netherlands and modeled by

 For a few years now, several research groups have been engaged in an
exciting research program. They extract living neurons from the human
brain,  visualize  their  three-dimensional  morphology,  record  their
discharges, and model all this information on a computer in order to better
understand what sophisticated calculations these cells are capable of.
But  how  do  you  get  live  human  neurons  in  the  first  place?  In
neurosurgery departments, it is not uncommon to harvest healthy brain
tissue. When neurosurgeons reach for a tumor or for the source of an


<!-- Page 180 -->

intractable epilepsy, they may not be able to access the target site, often
located deep inside the temporal lobe, without removing a few square
inches of healthy cortex. With the patient's consent, this valuable tissue can
then be used for research.
At the University of Amsterdam, only 500 meters separate the operating
room from the electrophysiology laboratory. Fifteen minutes are all it takes
for researchers to prepare human brain slices. Immersed in a bath of
artificial cerebrospinal fluid, these neuronal circuits can be kept alive for a
long time. Their three-dimensional architecture can then be measured, their
responses to the stimulation of other neurons can be recorded, and they can
be simulated on a computer.
The results are striking. We already knew that human neurons are larger
and, above all, more branched than those of other primates.
33
We now
understand that, beyond this change in size, human dendrites also perform
much more sophisticated operations than those of other primates.
34
Each
neuron comprises as many as 30,000 synapses, and the activation of only
135 of them suffices to generate an activation potential—thus allowing for a
much larger number of combinations than in other animals.
Most current artificial neural networks assume that a neuron merely
computes a weighted sum of the inputs it receives from other neurons.
However, we know today that this is a simplification, especially in the
 Homo sapiens  brain: human dendrites are nonlinear and perform logical
operations much more complex than simple addition. Thus, the number of
computational units in the human brain has been vastly underestimated: it is
not just the number of neurons (16 billion in the cortex alone), but the
number of their independent dendritic branches, which is at least twenty-
five times more.

FRONTIER

 Most neuroscientists regard consciousness as a property or
function of the brain, comparable to breathing or digestion, that
in principle should be explicable in terms of the activity of
neurons and synapses and their regulation by chemical signals.
 —Jean-Pierre Changeux ,  The Physiology of Truth  (2009)
 Is  explaining  consciousness  an  unreachable  dream,  a  mad  scientist's

neuroscientists regarded consciousness as a taboo subject, better left to the

speculations of retired scientists. Indeed, how could one study scientifically,

untouched—mentioning it was running the risk of compromising one's

In a few decades, the tides have turned. Research on the origins of

subliminal images to inattention, anesthesia, or coma, researchers have
discovered  many  ways  to  access  the  brain  mechanisms  of  subjective

THE  SUBLIMINAL  BRAIN
 Images of brain activity evoked by conscious and nonconscious processing of the same
words. Work carried out since 1998 by the author using the 3-tesla MRI scanner of the
Frédéric Joliot Hospital in Orsay.
 Since the time of Descartes, consciousness is shrouded in mystery. Is it an
ineffable essence inaccessible to scientific experimentation? Not at all.
Dozens of experimental paradigms now allow us to contrast conscious and
nonconscious  information  processing  in  the  brain.  Their  results  are
convergent  and  allow  us  to  dissect  the  cerebral  mechanisms  of
consciousness.
My lab focuses on the processing of subliminal images—pictures that
are flashed ever so briefly, and sandwiched between two others, such that


<!-- Page 184 -->

they cannot consciously perceived. The word  radio , for example, can be
made invisible by presenting it for less than one-twentieth of a second and
surrounding it with geometric shapes that mask it. Even with effort, it is
impossible to see it.
One might think that, since the word cannot be seen, it must mean that
the brain circuits of reading have become inoperative. However, this is not
the case. Not only does the invisible word continue to activate retinal cells
and the early visual cortex, but as you can see here, it excites the visual
word form area, the brain's letter box. Experiments demonstrate that all of
its letters are recognized, in proper order, regardless of the font in which
they are displayed.
1
 More sensitive methods show that the invisible word penetrates even
deeper, and reaches the areas responsible for word comprehension and
articulation. If the word refers to a number, such as  four  or  nine , it activates
the number sense area in parietal cortex. If it conveys a scary meaning, such
as  shark  or  rape , it reaches the amygdala, a subcortical nucleus involved in
the processing of emotions, particularly fear.
In this manner, experiment after experiment have shown that practically
all areas of the cortex can be contacted by a nonconscious stimulus.
2
Does
this imply that the conscious/nonconscious distinction is a mere convention,
a subjective epiphenomenon without any biological reality? Here again, the
answer is negative.
With a minimal displacement of the masks, the word they surround
becomes visible. This is an ideal design to contrast the brain activity evoked
by the same word when it is processed consciously versus nonconsciously.
Functional MRI reveals two signatures of awareness. First, perceptual
activity is massively amplified: in the visual form area, it is multiplied by
twelve. Second, conscious activity spreads to a broad network of additional
regions, notably in the parietal and prefrontal lobes. Whenever a stimulus
crosses the threshold for conscious perception, those areas abruptly switch
to a high and lasting state of activity that stabilizes the percept in working
memory.
3
This  sudden  flare  is  called  “conscious  ignition,”  and  it
characterizes any novel episode of awareness.

A GLOBAL  NEURONAL WORKSPACE
 Schematic representation of the global neural workspace model, as depicted in 2006 by
Jean-Pierre Changeux, Lionel Naccache, Jérôme Sackur, Claire Sergent, and the author.
 Hundreds of experiments show that unconscious processing is omnipresent
in the brain. Bombarded with stimuli, our brain processes them in parallel
and constantly performs thousands of calculations and adjustments of which
we are not aware. What, then, corresponds to the subjective impression of
becoming aware of a piece of information, of making a conscious effort, or
of taking a deliberate decision?
According to the theory that Jean-Pierre Changeux, Lionel Naccache,
and I have developed, a global neuronal workspace underlies all the


<!-- Page 186 -->

operations of which we are aware.
4
The idea is simple: our brain is made up
of a myriad of encapsulated processors, distributed in the cortex and various
subcortical nuclei, all of which process information nonconsciously: what
one processor does, most of the others ignore.
At the top of the cortical hierarchy, however, these processors become
tightly interconnected and exchange information through a vast network of
long-distance connections. What we call “awareness” is nothing else than
the entry of information into this distinct neural system, the global neuronal
workspace. This is a set of neurons that are broadly distributed throughout
the cortex, but are particularly dense in prefrontal and parietal regions, and
whose long axons diffuse information globally. The activation of a large-
enough subset of these neurons constitutes the neural code for a conscious
thought.
5
 Why should such a state evoke a conscious experience? Because the
information it encodes can spread to any other processor. According to the
theory, this is what we call “being conscious”: information is stable, “in
mind,” online, no longer confined to a few specialized circuits, but broadly
disseminated and therefore capable of influencing any of our mental or
physical  acts.  Whenever  an  idea  is  conscious,  our  global  neuronal
workspace allows us to talk about it, to store and recall it, to act on it—in
short, to use it in all our actions and decisions.
Why is our consciousness limited? Why is there so much unconscious
processing in the brain? Because the global workspace acts as a bottleneck:
whenever it engages in the representation of a conscious thought, it cannot
simultaneously represent a second one. The latter must remain on hold, at
the  edge  of  the  workspace—we  call  it  “preconscious,”  following  a
terminology proposed by Sigmund Freud in 1900. Also, some stimuli are so
brief or so weak that they lead to only a modest wave of activity. When this
ripple dies out at the doorstep of the workspace, without succeeding in
propagating further, its content remains “subliminal,” literally below the
 limen  (Latin for threshold) needed for full-blown ignition.
This is only a theoretical model, but it is supported by numerous
experimental  observations  in  both  humans  and  animals.
6
A  large
international  collaboration  aims  to  test  it  empirically  against  other
alternative hypotheses.
7

GLOBAL COMMUNICATION:  THE SPARK OF
CONSCIOUSNESS

groups of human participants. Work carried out by Jean-Rémi King and Jacobo Sitt of
Lionel Naccache's team at the Salpêtrière Hospital in Paris.
 The halos that inflame those heads bring to mind the “aura” dear to
paranormal buffs—the envelope of energy that would emanate from a
conscious soul. The colors, however, represent something at once more
prosaic and more fundamental: the outcome of a calculation that evaluates
the amount of information exchanged between distant brain areas, and
which turns out to be an excellent marker of consciousness.

According  to  global  neural  workspace  theory,  the  raison  d’être  of
consciousness, in evolution, is that it grants the organism a useful function:
global  information  sharing.  Being  conscious  is  possessing  a  global
workspace, a set of neurons so tightly interconnected that they can pick up
any local information and broadcast it to the entire brain. A conscious brain
is a brain that shares information—so the theory goes. But how can one
measure it?
To produce these images, global brain communication was estimated
from electrical measurement of spontaneous brain activity, that is, by
electroencephalography (EEG). For each pair of electrodes, we computed a
mathematical quantity called “weighted mutual symbolic information,”
which assesses how much information the underlying regions pass to each
other.
8
This quantity was turned into a color, from blue to green and then
red.
In the normal brain, as well as in patients who recovered consciousness,
information exchanges are massive, as indicated by warm colors. When a
brain injury causes us to lose consciousness, however, the exchanges shrink
dramatically, and they become minimal whenever the brain goes into a
vegetative state, also called unresponsive wakefulness. In this pathological
state, the sleep-wake cycle is preserved, contrary to coma, but the patient
ceases to react to stimulation, fails to respond to queries, does not act
intentionally, and thus seems to have lost all consciousness.
On  average,  global  information  sharing  is  an  excellent  marker  of
consciousness. But the situation is more complex: among patients with
unresponsive wakefulness, about 20 percent may still be conscious. Even
though they can no longer move, their brain still responds to the doctor's
requests.
9
The measurement of spontaneous brain activity sheds light on
their enigmatic plight: conscious patients are those whose cortex continues
to constantly shift from one state of global activity to another, without
freezing into a fixed state imposed by anatomy alone.
10
The stream of
consciousness corresponds to a ceaseless flux of information sharing across
the cortex and thalamus.

THE SIGNATURES  OF CONSCIOUSNESS
 Ninety-two potential markers of consciousness derived from high-resolution digital EEG.
Data collected and analyzed by Jacobo Sitt, Jean-Rémi King, and Denis Engemann of
Lionel Naccache's team at the Salpêtrière Hospital in Paris.
 “Is this person still conscious?” This question often arises in the clinic. The


<!-- Page 190 -->

anesthesiologist who puts a patient to sleep needs to ensure that the patient
does not regain consciousness in the midst of surgery—a horrible condition
that does occur in a small fraction of cases. The neurologist, on the other
hand, may have a hard time knowing whether, following a brain injury, the
patient has lost consciousness or is merely locked in, unable to move and
therefore to express that they are still conscious. In the first months of
hospitalization, nearly 40 percent of patients who are locked in escape the
vigilance  of  the  clinical  team,  who  believe  that  they  are  utterly
unconscious.
11
 How could those horror stories be avoided? Nowadays, brain imaging,
coupled with artificial intelligence, comes to the help of researchers and
clinicians. This image represents the topography, on the surface of the head,
of ninety-two distinct measures derived from the EEG, and which are all
potential indicators of consciousness. From a clinical database, an algorithm
learned which optimal combination of these indicators can predict not only
the patient's current state of consciousness, but also the probability of a
recovery in the coming weeks or months.
12
Any clinician can now send an
EEG recording to a computer server and receive back a tentative diagnosis
within minutes.
While EEG is one of the easiest brain measures to implement, the search
for brain signatures of consciousness has progressed so much that we now
possess many more clinically useful markers. Using positron emission
tomography, one can assess glucose metabolism in major areas of the global
neuronal workspace.
13
Another method consists in stimulating the brain
with a magnetic pulse and examining whether it evokes a complex response
that diffuses widely throughout the cortex.
14
One may also replace this
pulse with a simple series of tones such as  beep beep beep beep , and
measure the preservation of prefrontal cortex responses to an unexpected
change in the melody.
15
Finally, one may show the patient a movie and,
using functional MRI, check whether their parietal and frontal regions still
activate during moments of suspense.
16
All of these markers provide
excellent signatures of residual consciousness.
Once  shrouded  in  mystery,  the  mechanisms  of  consciousness  have
become a hot research topic with hundreds of publications every year. The
resolution of the problem is underway, with crucial implications for patients
with disorders of consciousness.

DECODING  MENTAL IMAGES
 Use of retinotopic maps to reconstruct an image from the activity it evokes in the brain.
Research performed in 2005–2006 by Bertrand Thirion's team and the author's using the 3-
tesla MRI scanner of the Frédéric Joliot Hospital in Orsay.
 The ultimate proof that we understand the brain code is to decode it. By
measuring brain activity, could we know what a person sees or hears, what
they say or think? The answer is yes—in principle at least: neuroscience
tells us that each of our perceptions, actions, and thoughts finds its origin in
the firing of a group of neurons in our brain. If we could read out the
neurons, we could guess the thought.
In practice, thanks to advances in brain imaging, we are just starting to
obtain images of sufficient quality to decode simple mental images. One of


<!-- Page 192 -->

the very first decoding experiments that I conducted with Bertrand Thirion
exploited the fact that the visual areas of the cortex contain a map of what
we see.
17
In these regions, each point of the cortex codes for a precise
sector of the retina. This property is called “retinotopy,” and it means that,
by examining which cortical sites are active, we can reconstruct a rough
approximation of what the person is seeing.
The map, however, is variable—so how can we establish the precise
correspondence between the pixels of the image and the points of the cortex
in a given human brain? On a geographical map, longitude and latitude
provide precise coordinates: when we scan the map from left to right, for
example, we also scan the globe from west to east. In order to get oriented
on the cortical maps of our participants, we used a similar trick: we scanned
the retina with expanding rings and rotating wedges, and examined how the
activated regions traversed the cortex. By crossing this eccentricity and
angle information, we obtained the precise retinal coordinates of each point
in the visual cortex.
We could then take any new series of MRI images and, for each active
point on the cortex, add a touch of virtual paint to the corresponding part of
the  image.  And  it  worked:  we  ended  up  with  a  movie  showing  a
reconstruction of what the subject had seen in the MRI machine, second per
second, whether it was the expanding rings or the rotating wedges. From
the person's brain activity, we could even decode their mental images;
whether they imagined an  X , an  L , an  O , or a  T , with eyes closed, we still
managed to project an image of their inner thoughts.


<!-- Page 193 -->

GUESSING  WHAT A PERSON IS SEEING OR
DOING
 Comparison between the images actually seen by a person (top) and those reconstructed
by an artificial intelligence algorithm from the activity of their brain measured using fMRI
(middle). Below: Letter-by-letter decoding of the word NEURON. Research carried out by
Yukiyasu Kamitani and his team using the 3-tesla MRI scanner of Kyoto University in Japan.
 Decoding methods have made a lot of progress in the past fifteen years.
Today, thanks to artificial intelligence, combined with increasingly accurate
measurements  of  brain  activity  with  functional  MRI,  we  manage  to
reconstruct the letters, the movies, or even the dreams that invade the
brain's visual areas.
18
 The idea is simple: it consists in using all of the active brain areas, and
not just retinotopic maps, in order to constrain the image reconstruction


<!-- Page 194 -->

process. Indeed, visual maps are quite limited: they allow us to know only if
such and such pixel of the image is on or off, but at the millimetric scale of
MRI,  this  mapping  is  very  coarse,  and  allows  us  to  trace  only  the
approximate contours of the image, as if we were seeing it through a thick
fog.
Probing the activity of additional cortical areas adds a lot of detail. Some
brain areas are sensitive to color, for instance, and therefore provide an
additional constraint: the image probably contains red. Others identify the
scene: is it an object, a face, an animal, or a body part? By finely matching
each voxel of the brain with the units of an artificial neural network, it is
possible to go much further and constrain the image in terms of its form, its
content, and even its meaning.
The result appears on the left: for each state of brain activity measured
by MRI, the software manages to reconstruct a fairly accurate view of the
image that caused it. Even the letters of the word  NEURON  become
recognizable.
Let's go one step further: by waking up a person who fell asleep in the
MRI machine, we can use the activity of their brain to guess, at least in part,
what they were dreaming of. Were they thinking of a person or an animal?
Was  the  dream  taking  place  indoors  or  outdoors?  This  subjective
information is no longer inaccessible: it can now be objectified by brain
imaging.
Why is this research important? The hope is that it may lead to the
development of new brain-machine interfaces that will benefit patients who
have become unable to communicate with the outside world. In that respect,
MRI offers little more than a proof of principle that such decoding is
feasible. The future lies in the insertion of electrodes directly into the brain,
for  instance  using  NeuroPixel  electrodes  to  record  the  fine-grained
dynamics of cortical activity in every detail. Based on the activity of a few
hundred  neurons,  pioneering  experiments  show  that  it  is  possible  to
reconstruct a person's actions and intentions—including what they say or
what they want to write—more precisely and much faster than with MRI.
19
 A person who has been paralyzed can thus regain the ability to speak or
even to act through a robotic arm.

AND  THE BLIND SHALL SEE
 Electrical stimulation of the visual cortex inducing an illusory perception of the letter O.
Image created by Xing Chen with Pieter Roelfsema and their collaborators at the
Netherlands Institute of Neuroscience, Amsterdam.
 What if we stimulated the cortex? The theory is simple: since each image
we see, each sound we hear, each thought we have corresponds to the
activation of a specific population of cells in our cortex, it should be
possible to evoke them by stimulating the appropriate neurons. Thus, a
blind person could recover sight: it should suffice to “paint,” on their visual
cortex, an electric image that corresponds to the one normally transmitted
by the retina.


<!-- Page 196 -->

Science fiction? No—this is the feat that Xing Chen, Pieter Roelfsema,
and their colleagues achieved in 2020.
20
In two macaque monkeys, they
implanted sixteen electronic chips, each equipped with sixty-four electrodes
capable of establishing contact with one or more pyramidal neurons. They
targeted the visual areas V1 and V4, located at the back of the skull, in the
occipital region. In those areas, indeed, the cortex maps the visual field,
such that each point on the retina corresponds to a precise sector of the
cortex.
The researchers first proved that, each time they stimulated a given
electrode with a small electric current, the monkey perceived a small point
of light at a specific location (even though it was in complete darkness).
Here, those 1,024 points are represented by green dots on the screen.
As a second step, by stimulating a set of electrodes, it became possible to
paint a letter on the surface of the cortex. The electrodes surrounded by a
red circle, for example, traced the shape of the letter  O —and when they
were activated, the monkey chose the shape  O  among the four letters that
appeared on screen. Each time a character was painted on his cortex, the
monkey moved his eyes toward the corresponding letter, thus confirming
that he had seen it. The letter did not exist, and no photon had ever been
emitted. What the monkey had seen was a pure illusion printed on his
cortex by clever researchers.
In the near future, flexible electrodes, printed on an ultra-thin plastic
film, will make it possible to stimulate tens of thousands of neurons. It will
then be possible to cover the entire visual field and to depict a detailed
scene and—why not?—even in color.
In his  Meditations on First Philosophy , published in 1641, Descartes
already foresaw this possibility: “I shall suppose . . . that an evil genius, no
less cunning and deceitful than powerful, has employed all his industry to
deceive me. I will think that the heavens, the air, the earth, colors, figures,
sounds and all the external things that we see, are nothing but illusions and
deceptions of which this genius has availed himself in order to lay traps for
my credulity.”
Descartes’ doubts were justified: Do I ever perceive the outside world, or
just the inner activity of my brain? Neurotechnologies for brain stimulation
erase the border between illusion and reality, but they also open up vast
therapeutic perspectives for patients whose injured or deprived brain could
benefit from a helping hand from computers.

DETECTING  ALZHEIMER’S  DISEASE
 Anatomical MRI scan (left), positron emission tomography image (right), and their fusion
(middle) in a patient with Alzheimer's disease. Colors reflect the concentration of the
fluorine-18 labeled AV1451 tracer, which is sensitive to the accumulation of tau protein.
Work by Marie Sarrazin, Michel Bottlaender, and their colleagues using the PET-MRI
scanner at CEA's Frédéric Joliot Hospital in Orsay.
 Aging is not inevitable—but with age, the probability of developing a
neurodegenerative disease increases. Over the age of eighty-five, at least
one third of people develop some form of dementia. Alzheimer's disease is a
terrible curse against which we currently have no effective vaccine or
treatment.
The main difficulty is that the disease starts early and remains silent for a
long time. Accumulations of senile plaques, composed of aggregates of
beta-amyloid peptide, precede the onset of the first symptoms by at least ten
or twenty years. The first neurofibrillary clusters, dense in a protein called


<!-- Page 198 -->

tau, also emerge very early on. It is only much later that anatomical MRI
detects brain atrophy, first in the hippocampus and entorhinal cortex, then
expanding into the temporal lobe and precuneus, and finally the rest of the
cortex. This evolution explains why patients typically lose their sense of
orientation in space and time first, together with their memory for proper
names,  then  their  semantic  memory,  before  sinking  into  full-blown
dementia.
The tau protein is one of the early markers of the disease. Thanks to
radioactive tracers, including a molecule called flortaucipir, also known as
AV1451, its concentration can now be measured at any point in the brain
without having to wait for autopsy.
21
The technique is simple: this tracer,
marked with a radioactive atom of fluorine-18, is injected in the patient, and
a positron emission tomography (PET) camera is used to visualize where
the radioactivity lands. Today, some brain-imaging machines combine PET
with MRI, which facilitates the fusion of the images and makes it possible
to precisely delineate the most affected regions of the brain. Furthermore,
such a clinical exam can be repeated regularly in order to follow the
evolution of the disease and its response to therapies.
In Alzheimer's, as in other related diseases called “tauopathies,” it is the
progression of the tau protein that most closely tracks the evolution of the
symptoms. This protein is therefore the main target of current clinical trials.
Immunotherapies that target tau are just starting to show some preclinical
results in animal models.
22
Hope is on the rise as brain imaging and animal
models shed increasing light on the cellular and molecular mechanisms of
this dreadful illness.

GLIA:  THE OTHER BRAIN CELLS
 Confocal image of microglia (in green) and the cell nuclei of neighboring neurons (in blue).
Research by Sonia Garel, professor at Collège de France, and her team at the Institute of
Biology of École Normale Supérieure, Paris.
 The arborescent cells that you see here, which were rendered fluorescent by
genetic manipulations, are not neurons—these are glial cells, and more
precisely microglia.
We often forget that the brain is made not only of neurons; at least 50
percent of its cells are glial cells. They are involved in all sorts of support
functions, for instance, the recycling of neurotransmitters released at the
synapse or the coupling of such synaptic activity with cerebral blood flow.
Indeed, the glial cells called astrocytes are responsible for the signal that we


<!-- Page 200 -->

detect with functional MRI: whenever they sense an increase in synaptic
activities, it takes them only one or two seconds to dilate the neighboring
capillaries in order to increase the local blood flow, thus bringing extra
oxygen and glucose.
Microglia have a different function. Those glial cells represent between 5
and 25 percent of the cells in the central nervous system, yet their role is
only starting to be understood. They are responsible for the brain's immune
defenses, a role normally assumed by white blood cells in the rest of the
body. However, unlike white blood cells, microglia do not come from the
bloodstream.  They  colonize  the  brain  long  before  birth,  and  renew
themselves throughout life.
Minute and agile, microglial cells activate as soon as they are needed.
For a long time, they were thought to only intervene in order to repair the
damaged brain, in case of injury or infection. However, we now know that
they play a much more fundamental role in the construction of the brain, in
its plasticity, and in neurodevelopmental or degenerative diseases such as
Alzheimer's.
23
 In fact, microglia proliferate and activate around the amyloid plaques
that  characterize Alzheimer's  disease—and  the  majority  of  genes  that
increase  the  risk  of  developing Alzheimer's  are  expressed,  sometimes
selectively, in microglial cells. However, we still fail to understand whether
their reaction plays a positive or negative role in the evolution of the
disease.  On  the  one  hand,  it  seems  to  be  protective,  because  any
interference with their work, and in particular their response to amyloid-
beta peptide, increases the risk of Alzheimer's. By accumulating around the
plaques, they may form an insulating barrier that limits their impact on
nearby axons and synapses.
On the other hand, activated microglia are known to be able to damage
neurons. Microglial cells can literally devour some synapses and may
increase  the  deleterious  effects  of  the  tau  protein  as  well  as  the
inflammation of brain tissue. Achieving an improved control over those
greedy brain cleaners and a better understanding of their role is essential for
contemporary clinical neuroscience.

STIMULATING  THE BRAIN TO RELIEVE
DISEASES
 Implantation of depth electrodes in the vicinity of the deep brain nuclei and neighboring fiber
bundles. Images created by Andreas Horn at the Charité Hospital in Berlin. The very high
resolution (100-micron) anatomical MRI scan in the background was acquired ex vivo on
the 7-tesla MRI magnet at Massachusetts General Hospital in Boston by Brian Edlow and
colleagues. It is available at  https://histopath.nmr.mgh.harvard.edu/100micronMRI.
 In the 1980s–1990s, the French neurosurgeon Alim-Louis Benabid and
neurologist Pierre Pollak had a revolutionary idea: could the symptoms of
Parkinson's disease be relieved by stimulating certain groups of neurons?
The answer was a resounding yes. Thirty years later, more than 150,000
patients worldwide have already benefited from this spectacular procedure.
How does it work? Underneath the cortex of each hemisphere, an MRI scan


<!-- Page 202 -->

easily locates the basal ganglia—deep subcortical nuclei involved in the
control of voluntary movements. In Parkinson's, the progressive loss of
dopamine neurons prevents those circuits from working. Through a very
small hole in the skull, the neurosurgeon brings one or several long
electrodes deep into the brain, targeting a specific group of neurons called
the subthalamic nucleus. Once in place, these electrodes are attached to the
skull and connected to an electrical stimulator via wires running under the
skin.
Deep brain stimulation can now begin. By trial and error, the surgeon
discovers which electrode, current intensity, and frequency alleviate the
symptoms.  The  result  is  often  spectacular:  many  patients  who  were
petrified, unable to move, and who had stopped responding to dopamine
medication experience a sudden resurrection—when the current is on, they
can walk, write, speak, even dance as if they were back to normal health.
The procedure does not cure the disease, but only compensates for its
symptoms. Yet thanks to it, a young patient may resume a largely normal
life for many years. The mechanism of this amazing revival is not yet fully
understood. The recovery involves a distributed neuronal circuit, and the
bundles of connections that pass nearby, here visualized in red, play an
essential role. Using diffusion MRI, those fibers can now be traced with
high accuracy, thus improving the efficiency of the procedure.
Surgeons are now starting to find novel uses for deep brain stimulation.
In psychiatry, for instance, it may be used to alleviate the symptoms of
obsessive compulsive disorder. As this image illustrates, different circuits
must be targeted in order to relieve those distinct pathologies. In a few
years, when we grow old, will it be commonplace to turn into cyborgs with
electrified brains?

STIMULATING  WITHOUT IMPLANTING
 Device for simultaneous stimulation of the cortex using transcranial magnetic and
ultrasound pulses. The colors show the affected regions according to computer simulations.
Research conducted by Anton Fomenko and Kai-Hsiang Stanley Chen in the laboratory of
Robert Chen at the Krembil Research Institute, Toronto.
 Luckily, it is also possible to stimulate the brain without piercing it with
long electrodes. Such stimulation is crucial in order to test, in normal
humans, the existence of a causal relationship between brain activity and
cognition. Indeed, brain imaging can only detect a correlation between the
execution of a cognitive task and the activation of such and such brain
circuit. But, as everyone knows, correlation is not causation. An event may
be systematically associated with another while playing no role in causing it


<!-- Page 204 -->

—otherwise, we would conclude that it is the noise of the engine that makes
the car move!
To test the causal role of a group of neurons, one must interfere with its
activity, either by stimulating it or by blocking it, and evaluate how this
intervention impacts behavior. In animals or in certain patients, for good
clinical reasons, we may be allowed to implant stimulation electrodes. In
mice and a few other species, a new method called “optogenetics” can also
be  used:  the  injection  of  a  virus  causes  neurons  to  express
channelrhodopsin, a light-sensitive molecule capable of opening an ion
channel in the cell membrane. Once this molecule is present in the neuron's
membrane, a laser suffices to turn it on or off at will.
24
 In typical humans, however, these invasive techniques are obviously
excluded for ethical reasons. However, as illustrated here, a range of
noninvasive brain stimulation tools is also available. Transcranial magnetic
stimulation consists in placing a figure-of-eight loop on the head. A brief
but intense electrical current is discharged into it. It generates a strong
magnetic field that penetrates the brain and stimulates the underlying
neurons. Another device relies on ultrasound: a probe sends ultrasound
pulses whose energy is precisely calculated to stimulate the neurons by
subtly squeezing them at a distance.
All these techniques confirm that it is possible to alter our perceptions,
decisions, and actions by interfering with the corresponding brain circuits.
Place the probe over visual cortex and, even in the dark, the stimulated
person sees points of light. Excite the motor cortex, and the hand starts to
move on its own. We may even slow down reading, calculation, attention,
or awareness by selectively interfering with the corresponding cortical
areas, after identifying them with functional MRI.
In  neurology  and  psychiatry,  these  techniques  offer  revolutionary
opportunities to treat depression, for example, without having to resort to
stronger interventions such as electroconvulsive therapy or year-long drug
taking.

DEMOCRATIZING  MRI
 Portable ultra-low-field MRI scanner installed at the bedside of a patient in intensive care.
The slices show images obtained in two patients. Their quality can be compared with the
reference clinical images obtained in traditional MRI or CT scanners and shown in the
bottom row. Research performed at Yale University in New Haven, Connecticut.
 Imagine a tiny, inexpensive MRI machine that could be simply rolled to a
patient's bedside and plugged into a standard power outlet, a machine that
every doctor could afford and that would allow for an immediate diagnosis
of strokes, perhaps even in the ambulance.


<!-- Page 206 -->

Democratizing MRI—rendering it accessible to any patient, without
them  having  to  wait  for  weeks—is  a  vital  goal  that  could  radically
transform the field of clinical neuroscience. However, that goal is hard to
reach. Traditional MRI scanners require expensive superconducting wires
cooled in liquid helium, as well as shielding by tons of metal. They cannot
be moved around, and are largely incompatible with traditional medical
equipment: in such a giant magnet, ferrous metals are irresistibly attracted,
and most electronic circuits stop working properly.
The MRI scanner you see here, which was recently approved by the
FDA, differs radically from those monsters: it weighs only 630 kilograms,
is mounted on wheels, and plugs into a normal electrical socket. Its
invention rests upon a revolution in the measurement of magnetic fields.
Several devices are now available to measure ultra-low magnetic fields with
excellent accuracy. They are based on recent advances in the physics of
optical pumping or giant magnetoresistance, two discoveries that earned
their inventors, French physicists Alfred Kastler and Albert Fert, the Nobel
Prize in 1966 and 2007.
With these sensors, an MRI scanner no longer requires a giant magnetic
field. A few loops of wire suffice, which means that the machine can easily
be turned on and off. The machine that you see here operates at 0.064 tesla,
a field almost fifty times lower than a standard 3-tesla MRI machine.
The only drawback is that the images are noisier. However, as you can
see in these images of two patients with COVID-related pathologies, the
signal-to-noise ratio is good enough to detect strokes, their nature, and
location,  with  clinically  relevant  accuracy.  And  unlike  any  other
conventional MRI scanner, the machine can be left at the patient's bedside,
thus allowing multiple scans per day to evaluate the disease's evolution. A
revolution in brain imaging is underway.

EPILOGUE:  MY BRAIN AND I
 Sagittal section of mouse hippocampus acquired by Laurent Moreaux at the Californian
Institute of Technology.
 What am I then? A thing that thinks.
 —René Descartes ,  Second Meditation  (1641)
 Can my consciousness be reduced to the activity of my brain? Is there no
immaterial  soul? After  showing  you  a  hundred  pictures  of  scientific
evidence, my answer to both questions, obviously, is a resounding yes. We
are neuronal machines, and what we call our “mind” is just the electrical
firing of incredibly sophisticated circuits of nerve cells in our brain.
Some of my friends find this vision upsetting. The most religious of
them, enthused by the prospect of a future life, of an infinitely powerful
God, and of a man made in His image, loathe the progress of neuroscience.
It terrifies a few of them, who believe that it can lead only to depression,
nihilism, and selfishness.
I understand those fears—yet after pondering these issues for years, I
have reached the conclusion that it is perfectly possible to be a lucid, happy,


<!-- Page 208 -->

and sociable neuronal human being.
First, neuroscience research is just the latest avatar of this ancient
injunction: “Know thyself.” Seeking to understand the universe, including
oneself, is a fundamental human quest. All religions and all philosophical
systems are in search of meaning. Unless we have no respect for human
reason, we cannot turn our backs on the answers that it yields about our
own nature. Our reason dictates that we face reality and accept the truth.
Besides, why should the world become gloomy once it is enlightened by
science? Did the rainbow become darker once Newton explained its colors?
Brain imaging never ceases to highlight the unparalleled beauty of our
neural circuits. In awe of this masterpiece of evolution, I marvel at the
mathematician's demonstration of a theorem, the astronomer's grasp of
distant galaxies, or the violinist's hands playing a frenzied pizzicato whose
neuronal symphony I can barely imagine in my own mind. Not only do I
rejoice to see my granddaughters acquire new talents, but my happiness
doubles when I remember that every minute of dialogue affects their neural
circuits, that their dendritic trees grow by several billion synapses every
day, and that their mental representations flourish like a magnificent coral.
Their tiny brains are unmatched learning machines, and in a few years, they
too may speak the language of atoms and stars. May our science help all
children  blossom  in  whatever  they  are  passionate  about—this  is  the
meaning of my commitment as the first president of the French Scientific
Council for Education.
Acknowledging  our  neuronal  nature  is  a  liberating  act.  Forget  the
inaccessible longing for perfection, which crushes us with guilt: we are
“just” the inheritors of hundreds of millions of years of evolution, followed
by several thousand years of culture. Accepting it is a lesson in humility,
tolerance, and humor. True, each of us is a small miracle of neurobiological
computation, but also an imperfect biological being, torn between various
vital impulses. A stroke, brain injury, or pharmacological imbalance, and we
sink into depression, aphasia, schizophrenia or dementia. May we find in us
the source of humility, because we do not decide on our own neuronal
balance; tolerance, because the burden that overpowers others may well
affect us tomorrow; and humor, because, considered with the eye of a
naturalist, human behavior is an inexhaustible source of amusement—think
about it during your child's next adolescent crisis.


<!-- Page 209 -->

Coming face to face with our brain requires lucidity and courage. We are
just an electrical motif on the tapestry of our neurons, and our mind will
disappear with our brain. Yet I rejoice at the thought that our future lies
solely in our hands, in our ability to understand and improve ourselves. This
is why I am not scared by the recent advances in neurotechnology. Like all
inventions, they could be misused, and they will have to kept in check. Yet I
trust that humanity will manage to orient them toward the greater good:
healing, improving, embellishing our neuronal selves, as so many other
human inventions, from writing to vaccines, have already helped us.
We are no longer surprised to see the surface of Mars through the eyes of
a robot. Tomorrow, in neural symbiosis with other machines, we will
perhaps feel the soil of the red planet using a brain-machine interface.
Being afraid of it is useless. Only one thing is certain: I am my brain.

ABOUT THE AUTHOR


<!-- Page 213 -->

Stanislas Dehaene has been conducting research in cognitive neuroscience
since the age of twenty. He entered the École Normale Supérieure in Paris
in  1984  through  the  mathematics  track,  and  immediately  joined  the
cognitive  science  and  psycholinguistics  laboratory  headed  by  Jacques
Mehler at the École des Hautes Études en Sciences Sociales in Paris. It was


<!-- Page 214 -->

under his direction that he conducted his first research on the mental
representation of numbers. This became the central topic of his PhD thesis,
which he defended in 1989.
At the same time, he attended the classes of Jean-Pierre Changeux at the
Collège de France. In 1985, Changeux proposed that he join his lab on a
part-time basis in order to develop neural models of cognitive functions,
particularly those associated with the prefrontal cortex.
1
In line with the
work of David Marr, Jay McClelland, and David Rumelhart, and with
Changeux's best-selling book  Neuronal Man ,
2
the aim was to model neural
circuits with mathematical equations, and to simulate them on a computer
in order to shed light on how they implement such functions as the
representation  of  concepts,  working  memory,  decision  making,  or
reasoning. This was the beginning of a long friendship and collaboration
that would result in a predictive model of the neural code for numbers
3
and
in the proposal of a neuronal ignition during access to consciousness: the
theory of the global neuronal workspace.
4
 Between 1992 and 1994, during a postdoctoral stay, Dehaene benefited
from a third mentor: Michael Posner, professor at the University of Oregon
and the world's specialist in attention and mental chronometry. A few years
earlier,  Posner  had  published,  with  Marcus  Raichle,  the  very  first
decomposition of cognitive functions using positron emission tomography.
5
 Fascinating by those revolutionary techniques, Dehaene and his wife and
colleague Ghislaine Dehaene-Lambertz returned to France determined to
develop brain imaging—which they did, particularly using geodesic nets for
electroencephalography (EEG) such as the one shown in this photograph. In
collaboration with Harvard professor Elizabeth Spelke, their work also
focused  on  studying  the  development  of  cognitive  functions  and
understanding  the  singularity  of  the  human  brain  for  language,
mathematics, and conscious thought.
Since 2005, Stanislas Dehaene has held the chair of Experimental
Cognitive Psychology at the Collège de France. In 2017, he became the
director of NeuroSpin, France's advanced brain imaging center, located in
Saclay, south of Paris. Since 2018, he has chaired the Scientific Council of
the French Ministry of Education, which attempts to bring scientific insight
to pedagogy.
