 EXECUTIVE FUNCTIONING FROM A
LIFESPAN PERSPECTIVE
 Although this chapter promotes a lifespan perspec-
tive on processes that are comprised in executive
functioning (EF), the majority of research reviewed
in the following sections relies on data from adults.
This adult bias is inevitable, given that EF does not
fully develop until adulthood and that, in children,
components of EF are not sufficiently differentiated,
either behaviorally or neuroanatomically (for a
review, see Fiske & Holmboe, 2019). Research that
does examine EF in children is primarily concerned
with characterizing  developmental  trajectories of
EF . This research has shown that rudimentary EF
begins to emerge in infancy (approximately around
5–6 months of age) and experiences a fairly rapid
development in early childhood (for reviews, see
Best & Miller, 2010; Fiske & Holmboe, 2019). EF
then continues to mature throughout adolescence
and into the mid- to late 20s, as evidenced by
improvements in both EF speed and EF accuracy
during this period (Delis et al., 2001). Developmen-
tal improvements in performance parallel progres-
sively greater processing efficiency (suggested by
progressively more localized brain activation during
EF task performance), as well as increases in synap-
togenesis, neuronal proliferation and pruning, and
myelination of relevant networks (for a review, see
Best & Miller, 2010).
In adulthood, following 1 to 2 decades of sta-
bility, a gradual decline in the speed of EF perfor-
mance begins to take place, starting in the mid-40s
to early 50s (Delis et al., 2001). This initial decline
may simply reflect general declines in speed of pro-
cessing as it is not associated with increases in error
rates. However, performance accuracy does begin to
decline after age 60, with a continuing progressive
decline thereafter (Delis et al., 2001). This decline
is associated with more pronounced age-related
cortical atrophy in prefrontal regions as compared
with primary and secondary cortical areas, as well
as declines in the efficiency of processing suggested
by a more distributed (i.e., less localized) brain acti-
vation during performance of EF tasks (Greenwood,
2007). In summary, EF is the last neurocognitive
domain to develop and among the first to begin
to decline with age, peaking in young to middle
adulthood.

were based on a range of methodologies, including
functional fractionation of the presumed neuroana-

so-called unity and diversity model of EF , which
proposes that EF comprises three correlated but dis-
sociable components: working memory, set shifting,

so-called function-led approach to test develop-

this function-led approach to test development has
had a strong following since its inception, it has not
been matched by commensurate advances in EF
conceptualization: In research and clinical practice
alike, the EF construct is still all too often defined
narrowly, either by neuroanatomy or by factor anal-
yses of traditional EF tasks.
In this chapter, I advocate for a more compre-
hensive, function-led approach to defining the EF
construct, one that is inspired by the function-led
approach to test development (Burgess et al., 1998).
This function-led EF construct deliberately encom-
passes capacities that are required for successful
daily functioning, based on the assumption that the
evolutionary purpose of EF is the ability to engage
in adaptive, purposeful, and goal-directed behavior
in daily life (Lezak et al., 2004; Stuss et al., 1997).

 WHAT THE FIELD KNOWS
 This section reviews what the field knows about
12 elemental processes that have been extensively
studied and that together make up the function-led
EF construct. For convenience and ease of clin-
ical translation, these processes are organized
into four EF subdomains that correspond to four
well-characterized clinical syndromes (see Volume 1,
Chapter 9, this handbook). Additionally, each of
the four subdomains is delineated by a cohesive
collection of cognitive or behavioral outputs and by
common neuroanatomic underpinnings. The four
subdomains are referred to herein as the (a) executive
cognitive functions, (b) initiation and maintenance of
action, (c) response selection, and (d) metatasking.
See Table 8.1 for an overview of these processes and
associated neuroanatomic regions.
 Executive Cognitive Functions
 Executive cognitive functions  (ECFs) refer to a set of
higher order neurocognitive processes that allow
one to generate “cognitive products” that precede
and are prerequisite to purposeful and goal-directed
behavior. The cognitive products at hand are deci-
sions about which goals to pursue (Del Missier et
al., 2012), plans about how to effectively achieve
the chosen goals, and solutions to problems when
a given plan is thwarted. Because ECFs are respon-
sible for adaptive goal selection and generation of
plans  before  any action is taken, they do not, in
and of themselves, guarantee that a plan will be
executed or that a goal will be achieved (Shallice &
Burgess, 1991).
Making a decision about which goal to pursue
requires a process that is sometimes referred to as
thoughtfully reflective decision making (TRDM),
comprising (a) generation of several alternative
choices or solutions; (b) collection of information
about each choice; (c) systematic deliberation over
alternatives, such as comparisons of pros and cons;
and (d) retrieval of memories of prior decisions and
comparisons of their outcomes with the current
options (Paternoster & Pogarsky, 2009; Paternoster

 Goal-directed search and retrieval  refers to a men-
tal search for, and identification of, relevant seman-
tic or episodic memories. Another well-recognized
term that refers to a related construct is generative
fluency. The role of this neurocognitive process in
planning, reasoning, and problem solving has been
well established (Brown et al., 2014; Schacter et al.,
2015) and has to do with the requirement that rel-
evant information be gathered before generation of
plans or solutions. Because goal-directed search and
retrieval relies in part on episodic memory, memory
impairments necessarily preclude effective employ-
ment of ECFs (Addis et al., 2009; Volume 1, Chap-
ter 10, this handbook). However, intact memory
and learning capacity in and of itself is not sufficient
for goal-directed search and retrieval because intact
memory only requires that one be able to recall
information on command. In contrast, goal-directed
retrieval requires self-cuing and fluent generation of
the targets of a given search.
Retrieval of information, whether semantic or
episodic, relies in large part on the integrity of
dorsolateral and ventrolateral convexities of the
prefrontal cortex (PFC; Kostopoulos & Petrides,
2008; Suzuki et al., 2009), in conjunction with the
posterior parietal cortex (Shimamura, 2011) and
medial temporal structures (Gaesser et al., 2013).
These neuroanatomic networks are known to play
a role in performance on tests of verbal fluency
(Costafreda et al., 2006; Verma & Howard, 2012),
which are among the staples of neuropsychological
assessment of EF (Rabin et al., 2005). Importantly,
goal-directed retrieval differs neuroanatomically
from simple retrieval on command, such that
goal-directed retrieval relies on coactivation of
memory retrieval networks with the default mode
network (Gerlach et al., 2011), which itself has
been implicated in the ability to recall and identify
episodic memories that are relevant for imagining
the future and, consequently, for prospective plan-
ning (K. C. R. Fox et al., 2015; Roberts et al., 2017).
The next elemental process of ECFs,  working
memory  (WM), refers to the ability to hold and
manipulate information in mind, and as such is key
for the ability to mentally organize (e.g., sequence,
compare) relevant pieces of information. The role of
WM in planning, reasoning, and problem solving is
well established, so much so that some theoretical
accounts consider WM to be a principal, or even the
only, neurocognitive substrate of the EF construct
(Baddeley & Della Sala, 1998; McCabe et al., 2010).
Depending on the exact conceptualization, other
terms that are synonymous (or nearly synonymous)
with WM are cognitive or mental control. Conse-
quently, the neuroanatomic network that subserves
WM is sometimes referred to as the cognitive con-
trol network (K. C. R. Fox et al., 2015).
A large body of research, including single neuron
recordings, functional imaging, and animal- and
human-lesion studies, has shown that the dorsolat-
eral PFC (DLPFC; particularly Brodmann’s areas 46,
9, and 6) plays a key role in WM (Edin et al., 2009;
Goldman-Rakic & Leung, 2002; Nácher et al.,
2006). However, effective processing within these
cortical areas intrinsically relies on intact cortical–
subcortical loops that involve the dorsolateral
head of the caudate nucleus, lateral dorsomedial
globus pallidus internal, and ventral anterior and
mediodorsal nuclei of the thalamus (i.e., the  direct
 cortico–subcortical pathway), as well as dorsal glo-
bus pallidus external, lateral subthalamic nucleus,
and globus pallidus/substantia nigra complex (the
 indirect  pathways; Lichter & Cummings, 2001).
Of note, the DLPFC appears to be specialized for
content along a rostrocaudal gradient, such that the
more abstract information is held in more rostral
DLPFC regions, whereas the more concrete infor-
mation is held in the more caudal DLPFC regions
(Choi et al., 2018; Nee & Brown, 2012).
Effective WM additionally requires intact con-
nectivity of the DLPFC with aspects of the parietal
lobe, most notably, superior parietal lobule and
intraparietal sulcus (Berryhill & Olson, 2008;
Crone et al., 2006; Edin et al., 2009; Rottschy et
al., 2012; Wendelken et al., 2008), the hippocam-
pus (implicated in the retrieval of information from
WM; Chein et al., 2011; Harms et al., 2013), and
the anterior and posterior cingulate cortex (impli-
cated in the maintenance of information in WM;
Chein et al., 2011; Harms et al., 2013). WM also
appears to rely on intact functioning of the cerebel-
lum, especially the ventral regions of the dentate
nucleus and associated networks, as evidenced by
both functional imaging and lesion studies (Justus

 Mental flexibility , the third aspect of ECFs, allows
fluid mental travel between ideas, concepts, levels
of analysis, perspectives, or perceptual sets. It is
central to the ability to generalize from the past
to the future or from the concrete to the abstract
(Moscovitch & Winocur, 2002; Shimamura, 2002).
An important aspect of mental flexibility is the
notion of abandoning one mental set in favor of
another. Thus, for example, when performing the
Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (Heaton et al., 1993),
an examinee must flexibly shift between  perceiving
 color, form, or number of items presented on cards.
Similarly, in many task-switching paradigms, fun-
damental mental and perceptual shifts are needed,
such as switching between concrete versus abstract,
color versus shape, or local versus global character-
istics of a given stimulus (Cools et al., 2004; Huff et
al., 2015; Stasenko et al., 2017). Of note, in cogni-
tive neuroscience and clinical neuropsychology, the
term “mental flexibility” (or “cognitive flexibility”) is
often used somewhat loosely, referring to a broader
set of processes, well beyond those described
earlier. For example, “mental flexibility” is often
used to describe the ability to adapt behaviorally or
the ability to flexibly shift from one daily activity
to another. However, these latter processes reflect
behavioral, not cognitive, aspects of EF and rely
on a separate set of neurocognitive processes and
separate neuroanatomic networks that are discussed
later in this chapter.
From a neurophysiologic standpoint, mental
flexibility is thought to reflect the ability of discrete
neuronal networks to become flexibly engaged or
disengaged, a neuronal process known as oscillatory
synchrony (Gregoriou et al., 2015). Of relevance
here is the fact that imagining a stimulus and actu-
ally perceiving a stimulus activate the same neuro-
nal network (Johnson & Johnson, 2014), and that
imagery typically plays a role during planning and
problem solving (Addis & Schacter, 2013; Madore
& Schacter, 2014). Neuroanatomically, the regions
that subserve mental flexibility largely overlap with
those that subserve WM, consistent with reported
correlations between WM and switching abilities
(Vandierendonck, 2012). Within the broader WM
network, mental flexibility appears to be particu-
larly related to the inferior frontal gyrus in the left
hemisphere (Hirshorn & Thompson-Schill, 2006),
pre-supplementary motor area (pre-SMA; Crone
et al., 2006; Mansfield et al., 2011), and medial
superior parietal lobule and intraparietal sulcus
(Tamber-Rosenau et al., 2011), although the tem-
poral lobes have also been implicated (Barrett et al.,
2003; Smith et al., 2004). Lastly, mental flexibility,
particularly as it relates to future-oriented thinking,
also depends on coactivation between default mode
network and WM networks (Roberts et al., 2017).
 Initiation and Maintenance
 Once goals are mentally selected and planned out,
the actual attainment of goals depends on  initiation
and maintenance  (I&M) of appropriate behavioral
output. I&M is the first step in transitioning from
the purely cognitive products of ECF to actual
behavioral  execution . I&M comprises (a) mobiliza-
tion of effort, (b) purposeful and internally moti-
vated initiation of action (which is in contrast to
reflexive, automatic, externally motivated actions),
and (c) continuous refreshing of one’s mental set
so as to maintain relevant behavioral output. It is
important to dissociate I&M from ECFs, such that
intact ECFs do not guarantee that relevant actions
will be initiated and followed through. Conversely,
deficits in ECFs do not preclude normal employ-
ment of I&M—in other words, regardless of whether
a plan is generated by one’s own ECFs or whether it
is provided by another person, I&M can proceed.
The first elemental process of I&M,  effort mobi-
lization , has also been referred to as “energization”
(Stuss, 2011), “psychomotor” or “incentive sensiti-
zation” (Robinson & Berridge, 2000), “behavioral
activation” (Salamone et al., 2007), or “wanting”
(Berridge & Kringelbach, 2008). It has been con-
ceptualized by a number of authors as an aspect of
EF (for a review, see Ducharme et al., 2018) and
refers to an inherent tendency to physically engage
with the environment for the purposes of either goal

 Initiation , the second elemental process of I&M,
refers to processing that immediately precedes
observable motor output but is also inextrica-
bly a part of that output. This process involves
an assembly of discrete components of motor or
verbal sequences. Neuronal activation that immedi-
ately precedes movement involves multiple motor
circuits within the cortex, the basal ganglia, and
the pyramidal tracts. For reviews, see Klaus et al.
(2019) and Volume 1, Chapter 8, this handbook.
Although initiation is sometimes conceptualized as
motor or action planning, it is nevertheless a cogni-
tive process that forms a bridge between processes
that are purely motor and those that reflect higher
order cognition. For example, action-planning
latencies (i.e., a brief pause before an action is
released) evidence strong associations (i.e., large
effects sizes) with performance on measures of EF
(Niermeyer et al., 2017; Suchy & Kraybill, 2007;
Suchy et al., 2010), as well as with the magnitude
of the so-called motor readiness potential, which
itself is also correlated with EF (Euler et al., 2015).
Impairments in initiation are characterized by
longer latencies preceding motor or verbal output,
as well as assembly of shorter sequences of action
components, which ultimately result in slower, less
smooth, and less efficient performance overall (Ver-
wey et al., 2011). Initiation relies on superomedial
aspects of the frontal lobes, including the anterior
cingulate gyrus, rostral medial PFC, supplementary
motor area (SMA), and pre-SMA (Hoffstaedter et al.,
2014; Massimo et al., 2015; Volle et al., 2012).
The last aspect of I&M is  maintenance . It refers
to an elemental process responsible for maintaining
intended behavioral output over time. Maintenance
can be conceptualized as the capacity for vigilant
attention. There is considerable evidence that the
ability to maintain attention relies on the ability
to continually refresh a certain configuration of
neuronal firing (or oscillatory synchrony), with
each refresh cycle lasting approximately 10 seconds
(for a review, see Langner & Eickhoff, 2013). The
neural network that controls the refresh cycles is
largely right-hemisphere dominant and includes
the dorsomedial, medial, and ventromedial PFCs;
anterior insula; intraparietal sulcus; temporoparietal
junction; cerebellar vermis; thalamus; putamen; and
midbrain (Langner & Eickhoff, 2013).
 Response Selection
 Response selection  (RS), the third subdomain of
EF , comes into play once plans are translated into
actions. RS refers to rapid and immediate behavioral
decisions that become necessary when contextual
changes signal that ongoing behavior is no lon-
ger adaptive or that new options are available. In
such situations, a rapid decision needs to be made
about whether to exploit the ongoing behavior or
explore new behavioral opportunities (Domenech
& Koechlin, 2015; Donoso et al., 2014). Contex-
tual changes and availability of options are ever
present, so all behavior must be accompanied by
sustained monitoring for potentially relevant cues
(e.g., a change from green to red on a traffic light).
As was the case with I&M, the products of RS are
inherently “behavioral” in that any processes that
precede a given action are inextricably linked to that
action and, if separated from the behavioral out-
comes, no longer hold any relevance. Therefore, the
rapid behavioral decisions should not be confused
with deliberative decision making. In fact, many
daily behavioral choices do not afford adequate time
for deliberation (Paternoster & Pogarsky, 2009),
requiring instead that a decision be made on the fly.
Ultimately, the goal of such rapid decision making is
to maximize rewards and minimize punishments in
the here and now.

Different theoretical models for such rapid
decision making have been proposed. Among the
most prominent is the somatic marker hypothesis,
which proposes that rapid decisions are based on
gut feelings that reflect physiological changes asso-
ciated with certain situations and stimuli (Bechara
et al., 2005). In brief, according to this model, the
brain stores memories of autonomic experiences
associated with prior outcomes of specific behav-
ioral choices. Thus, whenever the same behavior is
initiated, physiologic memories signal whether the
expected outcome is likely to be positive or nega-
tive. This model incorporates cognitive, emotional,
and physiologic processes and explains the rapid
decision-making process in terms that are highly
intuitive and match the subjective phenomenology
of the human experience; it therefore has gained
a considerable popularity and prominence in the
literature. In sharp contrast to the somatic marker
hypothesis is a computational model that is based
on the Dirichlet process mixture models, a type of
calculation used in Bayesian statistics (Donoso et al.,
2014). This model, while conceptually and mathe-
matically complex, allows for elegant comparison of
actual behavioral responses to responses modeled
mathematically and thus illustrates the feasibility
of making somewhat complex adaptive choices in
the absence of human consciousness. Briefly, this
model proposes two inferential streams within the
PFC: one that makes predictions about the certainty
of an ongoing behavior, and one that makes predic-
tions about the certainty of alternative behavioral
options. Despite the apparent differences between
the somatic marker hypothesis and the Dirichlet
process model, they share the notion that neuro-
cognitive processes that enable RS occur largely
on a preconscious level, involving (a) discrepancy,
error, or threat detection; (b) rapid and flexible
updating of previously learned contingencies; and
(c) inhibition of one response in favor of another
response (for a review, see also Castellanos-Ryan &
Séguin, 2016).
The first aspect of RS,  detection of discrepancies,
errors, or threats , refers to a continuous moni-
toring of one’s actions and external context for

The second aspect of RS,  contingency updating ,
refers to a rapid, preconscious updating of pre-
viously learned associations between behaviors
and outcomes, considering a broad array of cues
that signal potential threats or rewards and thus
require targeted allocation of attentional resources
(Cox et al., 2005; Grossberg et al., 2008). Contin-
gency updating allows one to change or reverse
a response to a given stimulus or, conversely, to
change which stimulus is used as a trigger for a
particular response. A classic example from clinical
neuropsychology is the switches that are built into
the administration of the Wisconsin Card Sorting
Test (Heaton et al., 1993). Similar to the capacity
of the error-detection system to implicitly code
the significance of errors (Maier et al., 2012), the
contingency-updating system has the capacity to
implicitly compute the probabilities of positive or
negative outcomes and to conduct a rudimentary
cost–benefit analysis (Rolls, 2004; Windmann et
al., 2006).
The primary substrate of contingency updating
is the orbitofrontal cortex, including ventrolateral
and ventromedial regions (Rolls, 2004). Within
these regions, the coding of more abstract (e.g.,
monetary) and more temporally distant rewards
is thought to occur in the more rostral regions,
whereas more concrete (e.g., food) and more
immediate rewards are thought to be localized
caudally (Kringelbach & Rolls, 2004). There is less
agreement with respect to coding of valence, with
some accounts suggesting that coding of rewards
and punishments takes place in the medial and
lateral orbitofrontal cortices, respectively (Graben-
horst et al., 2007; Ursu & Carter, 2005), and others

The last elemental process of RS,  inhibition , refers
to the stopping and prevention of motor actions.
Inhibition can occur in a top-down fashion in
response to a deliberative decision making, or it
can be triggered by bottom-up processes such as
discrepancy detection and contingency updating
and can take place without a conscious awareness
(Hughes et al., 2009). Because the neural processing
of inhibition of a response is faster than response
initiation (i.e., 200 vs. 250 ms, respectively), it
can be effected even  after  a given motor program
has been released and abort an incipient action
(Logan & Irwin, 2000). For a review, see Rossetti
and Pisella (2003) and Volume 1, Chapter 8, this
handbook. Response inhibition relies primarily on
the right inferior frontal gyrus (Aron et al., 2014;
Chamberlain & Sahakian, 2007; Chikazoe, 2010),
which coordinates the inhibitory action with other
brain regions involved in discrepancy detection (i.e.,
the right orbitofrontal gyrus, the anterior cingulate
gyrus, pre-SMA) as well as with those involved in
generation of plans for future actions (i.e., the WM
networks; Cohen et al., 2013; Roger et al., 2014).
 Metatasking
 Metatasking  (MT) is the final subdomain of EF . 4
It refers to one’s ability to effectively interleave
subgoals of one task with subgoals of other tasks,
allowing for a completion of multiple,  multistep
 tasks across somewhat extended periods of time.
MT is not possible without intact ECFs, I&M, and
RS; however, these subdomains, while necessary,
are not sufficient for MT to take place. This sec-
tion focuses on those elemental aspects of MT that
are distinct from those already covered under the
previous three EF subdomains. Specifically, MT
comprises three elemental neurocognitive pro-
cesses, including branching, prospective memory,
and metacognition (Logie et al., 2010; McAlister &
Schmitter-Edgecombe, 2013).

 Branching  refers to the ability to temporarily abort
performance of one task for the purpose of complet-
ing another task and then return to the original task
as appropriate. Thus, branching requires the ability
to hold in mind multiple tasks and goals and flexibly
switch among them, requiring initiation, stopping,
and reinitiation of activities, often in the absence of
any external cues. In clinical neuropsychology, the
Condition 4 of the Color–Word Interference Test
from the Delis–Kaplan Executive Function System
battery (Delis et al., 2001) assesses branching, requir-
ing that examinees name the color of ink (i.e., Stroop
interference effect), except if the word is placed in a
box, in which case they should simply read the word.
Research examining the neuroanatomy of branching
has consistently implicated the frontopolar cortex
(Koechlin et al., 1999; Mansouri et al., 2017).
 Prospective memory  refers to the capacity to
retrieve prior intentions in response to external cues
(event-based prospective memory) or passage of
a certain amount of time (time-based prospective
memory). Prospective memory requires not only
the ability to remember what one’s intentions are
(clearly requiring intact episodic memory) but also
the ability to retrieve such intentions when appro-
priate, which typically requires strategic monitoring
of the environment for relevant cues or strategic
monitoring of the passage of time. There is some
evidence that event-based and time-based pro-
spective memories are dissociable, such that tasks
of time-based prospective memory have consis-
tently been found to be more difficult than tasks
of event-based prospective memory (Gonneaud et
al., 2014). Additionally, although functional imag-
ing and lesions studies show that both time-based
and event-based prospective memories rely on the
integrity of the frontopolar cortex (Burgess et al.,
2011; Volle et al., 2011), the two types of prospec-
tive memory differ with respect to the involvement
of additional brain regions. Specifically, event-based
prospective memory is associated with additional
activation in relevant sensory areas, likely due to
monitoring the environment for cues, whereas
time-based prospective memory involves addi-
tional extensive activation of the WM networks, the
cuneus and precuneus, superior temporal gyrus,
and cerebellum (Gonneaud et al., 2014).
The third elemental process of MT is  meta-
cognition , sometimes also referred to as insight or
self-knowledge. Metacognition has been shown
to be related to the ability to metatask in daily life
(Fazeli et al., 2017) and to performance on EF tests
(Fazeli et al., 2017; Villalobos et al., 2021). It reflects
one’s capacity to retrospectively judge how well
one accomplished a given task and to prospectively
estimate how well one will be able to accomplish an
ongoing or future task (Fleming & Dolan, 2014).
With respect to achievement of MT goals, metacog-
nition allows us to judge (a) which subgoals have
been completed and which have yet to be done,
(b) whether subgoal completion is accurate or
matches a plan, and (c) whether our own cognitive
resources (e.g., episodic memory, WM) are sufficient
for successful execution of MT activities or whether
compensatory strategies such as reminders, written
checklists, and the like need to be employed. Various
frontal lobe structures have been implicated in meta-
cognition using functional (De Martino et al., 2013;
Paul et al., 2015; Valk et al., 2016) and structural
(Grossner et al., 2018) imaging research, with some
research pointing specifically to the frontopolar cor-
tex (Miele et al., 2011). Additionally, there appears
to be some distinction between retrospective versus
prospective metacognitive judgments, such that the
lateral frontopolar and dorsolateral cortices play a
role in retrospective judgments carried forward when
planning an execution of a new task, whereas medial
structures (including the default mode network;
Grossner et al., 2019) play a role in the retrospective
and immediate judgments (for a review, see Fleming
& Dolan, 2014). However, findings regarding addi-
tional structures implicated by functional imaging
research are quite varied, perhaps because of differ-
ences in the specific content of a given metacognitive
task (Baird et al., 2015; Valk et al., 2016).

 In this chapter, EF is defined as a comprehensive
construct responsible for selection, planning, and
execution of future-oriented behavior in daily life.
The 12 neurocognitive processes that fall under
this comprehensive umbrella can be organized into
four subdomains: executive cognitive functions,
initiation and maintenance, response selection, and

 QUESTIONS AND CONTROVERSIES
 As already alluded to in the introduction to this
chapter, arguably the most prominent model of
EF is the one proposed by Miyake and colleagues
(2000). The model proposes that EF comprises
three related entities: working memory, set shifting,
and inhibition. The model was developed factor
analytically and has been slightly revised over
the years to accommodate the emergence of new
factor-analytic findings (for a review, see Karr et al.,
2018). Aside from the well-described limitations of
factor analysis as a methodology for investigation of
neurocognitive constructs (Delis et al., 2003), the
Miyake model is limited in that it does not clearly
differentiate conceptually (and sometimes method-
ologically) between cognitive and behavioral pro-
cesses. For example,  cognitive inhibition  (i.e., keeping
unwanted percepts or thoughts from entering work-
ing memory) is confounded with  behavioral inhibi-
tion  (i.e., stopping a behavioral response). Similarly,
 mental flexibility  (i.e., the ability to change one’s
mental set) is confounded with  behavioral flexibility
 (i.e., the ability to update reinforcing contingencies
toward a behavioral change).
Such semantic overlap among functionally
and neuroanatomically distinct constructs (as
reviewed earlier in this chapter) has represented
an insidious but persistent barrier to progress in
investigations of EF, making it difficult for studies
or models to build upon each other. This barrier
is further augmented by the pervasive discon-
nect between conceptualizations of EF that are
strictly cognitive in nature and those that take
a broader behavioral perspective. The former
approach is typically adopted in cognitive psy-
chology and cognitive neuroscience and closely
links conceptualizations of EF to available cogni-
tive paradigms, typically conducted with healthy
participants. The latter approach incorporates
experience with patient populations and, in addi-
tion to test performance, considers functioning
in daily life. Although there is some permeability
between the two approaches, pervasive limitations
in semantics and terminology preclude fruitful
“cross-pollination.” Thus, it can be argued that
the most pressing issue in the study of EF today is
the need for an interdisciplinary consensus about
definitions and terminology pertaining to dis-
crete elemental processes. Additionally, consensus
about the scope of the EF construct is needed, as
it has been argued that a thorough understanding
of EF requires that both cognitive and behavioral
processes be included under the umbrella of this
complex construct (Ardila, 2008).
